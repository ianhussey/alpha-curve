Long-term chronic alcoholic individuals exhibit impairments in cognitive functioning that resemble the profile of cognitive deficits found in patients with damage to the right hemisphere (RH) of the brain that is unrelated to alcoholism (Ellis & Oscar-Berman, 1989; Goodglass & Kaplan, 1979; Milner, 1972). Some researchers have proposed an RH model of alcoholism-related cognitive decline that suggests that functions for which the RH of the brain is normally specialized are differentially affected by the harmful effects of long-term alcoholism relative to those for which the left hemisphere (LH) of the brain is normally specialized (for reviews, see Evert & Oscar-Berman, 1995; Oscar-Berman, 1992). A number of investigators who independently studied normal chronological aging have also noted that as people age, they become less reliant on functions of the RH and more reliant on functions of the LH (Elias, 1979; Ellis & Oscar-Berman, 1989; Goodglass & Kaplan, 1979; Hochanadel & Kaplan, 1984; Kaplan, 1980, 1988).

The similarity in findings for alcoholic and normal aging individuals led to the hypothesis that alcoholism may result in premature aging of the brain (Ellis & Oscar-Berman, 1989). In general, the premature aging model suggests that alcoholism is accompanied by the precocious onset of neuroanatomical and behavioral changes typically associated with advancing age because of either the cumulative effects of alcohol ingestion or the increased vulnerability of an aging brain to the toxic effects of alcoholism. According to one version of the premature aging model—the accelerated aging hypothesis—alcoholism may accelerate age-related cognitive decline regardless of the age at which problematic drinking begins (Eckardt, Ryback, & Pautler, 1980; Noonberg, Goldstein, & Page, 1985). According to another version—the increased vulnerability hypothesis—vulnerability to alcoholism-related brain damage is magnified in problematic drinkers over age 50, which is after the normal manifestations of aging generally begin (Jones & Parsons, 1971; Klisz & Parsons, 1977). Thus, older people who abuse alcohol may suffer proportionately greater age-related cognitive decline than their nonalcoholic peers, whereas younger alcoholic individuals are not expected to show cognitive impairments relative to younger control participants. Although the accelerated aging and the increased vulnerability versions of the premature aging model differ in the prediction of the age of onset for alcoholism-related cognitive decline, both versions predict that RH functions are more vulnerable in aging alcoholic individuals compared with younger and older nonalcoholic controls.

Attentional functioning is interesting to study with respect to alcoholism and aging because the RH, proposed to be affected by long-term chronic alcoholism and normal chronological aging, is also implicated as playing a specialized role in attentional processing (e.g., Gainotti, Messerli, & Tissot, 1972; Heilman, Valenstein, & Watson, 1985; Heilman & Van Den Abell, 1979, 1980; Mesulam, 1981, 1990; Mosidze, Mkheidze, & Makashvili, 1994; Weintraub & Mesulam, 1989; Whitehead, 1991). Furthermore, the study of abnormalities in attentional functioning in alcoholic individuals has largely been neglected (e.g., see Hunt & Nixon, 1993). Thus, methods used to study attentional processing provide one means with which to further evaluate the claims of the RH models of age- and alcoholism-related cognitive decline.

A visual selective attention task was used in the present study to assess RH specialization for attentional processing in young healthy individuals and to assess potential RH dysfunction in aging and alcoholic individuals. A cued-detection paradigm was used in which a peripherally located precue provided information about the most probable spatial location of a subsequent target letter (Posner, 1980). The target letter could appear in either the left visual field (LVF) or the right visual field (RVF), while a distractor letter was simultaneously presented in the opposing visual field. Valid, neutral, and invalid cues were used to assess whether any hemispheric differences in attentional processing were due to differences in the extent to which participants benefited from valid cues compared with neutral cues (benefits), or the extent to which they were disadvantaged by invalid cues compared with neutral cues (costs). The efficiency of detecting target stimuli following either LH or RH activation for each cue condition was assessed with the d′ measure of sensitivity (Green & Swets, 1966). Younger and older nonalcoholic and alcoholic participants were tested on this task.

The following operational definition of RH specialization for selective attentional processing was used to assess whether aging and/or alcoholism was associated with RH functional decline on the task used here. Because of the RH's ability to direct limited attentional resources over a wider spatial array than can the LH (Corbetta et al., 1993; Heilman & Van Den Abell, 1980; Roy et al., 1987), we expected RH specialization for selective attention to be manifested as less reliance on the cues following RH activation. In other words, if the RH is activated by the cue, and if the RH directs attention to a broader spatial array (i.e., not just the LVF), then the cue should not be as critical in helping to detect the target letter, and the target could be processed efficiently regardless of the nature of the cue. Thus, one would expect a reduced (or no) cost effect associated with invalid cues and perhaps a slightly reduced benefit effect associated with valid cues. However, following LH activation, we expected both cost and benefit effects because the LH primarily directs attention to the RVF. Thus, the magnitude of both cost and benefit effects were expected to be smaller following RH activation than following LH activation by the cue.

Given this operational definition, a primary goal of our study was to determine whether aging and/or alcoholism disrupted this RH specialization for selective attentional processing. We reasoned that a disruption in the RH's ability to attend to both sides of space might be manifested in one of two ways. The first possibility was that the disrupted RH might be more restricted in its ability to attend to the whole visual field and might attend primarily to the left side of space (in much the same way that the LH primarily mediates attention in the right side of space). One would then expect that the RH would no longer exhibit an enhanced ability to process the target regardless of the validity of the cue (i.e., costs would be evident on invalid cue trials following RH activation). Another possibility was that the RH would no longer be able to efficiently process information from either side of space; thus, performance overall would be impaired following RH activation relative to performance following LH activation.

Regardless of the exact nature of the disruption, we were interested in determining whether aging was associated with RH functional decline, whether alcoholism was associated with RH functional decline, and whether any alcoholism effects were independent of, or interacted with, aging effects. The present study was designed to address these issues.

Method

Participants

A total of 73 individuals participated in the study. Thirty-seven were nonalcoholic controls (22 women, 15 men) and 36 were abstinent alcoholic individuals (11 women, 25 men). The nonalcoholic control participants and the alcoholic participants were assigned to one of four groups on the basis of their alcohol history and age: younger nonalcoholic controls (n = 12; M age = 37 years, range = 26–47 years), younger alcoholic participants (n = 14; M age = 43 years, range = 31–49 years), older nonalcoholic controls (n = 25; M age = 63 years, range = 52–76 years), and older alcoholic participants (n = 22; M age = 61 years, range = 50–74 years). All participants were recruited from the outpatient populations of the Boston Department of Veterans Affairs Medical Center as well as from responses to advertisements in local newspapers. All participants were community dwelling residents, and all participants gave informed consent and were financially compensated for participating in the study.

As seen in Table 1, the groups were equivalent with respect to years of education and IQ scores from the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale—Revised (Wechsler, 1981) and the General Memory score from the Wechsler Memory Scale—Revised (Wechsler, 1987). The older alcoholic participants and controls were equivalent in age, but the mean age for the young controls was somewhat lower than the mean age for the young alcoholic participants.

Participant Characteristics

A series of questionnaires (e.g., handedness, alcohol and drug use) and interviews was administered to all participants to ensure that they met the inclusion criteria for the study. As part of this assessment, we administered to all participants subsections of a computerized version of the Diagnostic Interview Schedule (DIS; Robins, Helzer, Cottler, & Goldring, 1989), which provides lifetime psychiatric diagnoses according to Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (3rd ed., rev.; DSM–III–R; American Psychiatric Association, 1987) criteria. These subsections include Generalized Anxiety, Depression, Mania, Schizophrenia, Alcohol Abuse and Dependence, Drug Abuse and Dependence, and Organic Brain Syndrome. A vision test was also administered at the time of testing. The demographic and medical history information was obtained from participants' self-reports and from medical records when available.

The inclusion criteria for the study were as follows: (a) being right-handed (excluding forced right-handers and ambidextrous individuals); (b) having normal or corrected-to-normal vision and hearing; (c) having no history of neurological dysfunction (e.g., stroke, significant head trauma, loss of consciousness for longer than 15 min, or epilepsy); (d) having no history of learning disabilities, dyslexia, or attention deficit disorders; (e) having no major medical illness (e.g., significant diabetes, liver disease, and heart disease); (f) having no history of polydrug abuse, extensive drug use, or recent drug use; and (g) having no major psychiatric disorders, including not currently taking antidepressants or antianxiety medications and having no history of electroconvulsive therapy.

Abstinent alcoholic participants also met the following additional criteria: (h) a minimum of 5 years of drinking at least 21 drinks per week; (i) a sobriety period of at least 1 month so as not to confound the long-term residual effects of alcoholism with acute effects; and (j) a positive diagnosis of alcohol abuse and dependence according to DSM–III–R criteria, determined by administering the Alcohol Abuse and Dependence subsection of the DIS (Robins et al., 1989; none of the control participants had a positive diagnosis on this measure).

On the basis of the answers from the questionnaires and interviews, the alcoholic participants drank 21 or more drinks per week for an average of 18.44 years (range = 5–40 years) and were sober for an average of 7.66 years (range = 1 month–28.92 years). A Quantity-Frequency Index (QFI; Cahalan, Cisin, & Crossley, 1969) score was calculated for each participant, which took into consideration the amount, type, and frequency of use of alcoholic beverages either during the past 6 months (for the nonalcoholic participants) or during the 6 months preceding cessation of drinking (for the alcoholic participants). The average QFI for the alcoholic participants was 8.57 (SE = 0.88; range = 2.36–26.72) and 0.14 for the controls (SE = 0.03; range = 0–0.78), a difference that was statistically significant, t(71) = 9.71, p < .0001.

Most participants completed the entire experimental protocol, including the neuropsychological and cognitive screening, in two testing sessions, totaling approximately 6 hr.

Apparatus and Stimuli

A Macintosh IIci microcomputer (Apple Computer, Inc., Cupertino, CA) with a high-resolution 13-in. RGB color monitor (RGB Spectrum, Alameda, CA) was used to present stimuli and record responses. Stimuli were prepared in Aldus SuperPaint (1993) graphics program. Millisecond timing of stimulus presentations and responses were controlled by VScope (Rensink & Enns, 1992), a multiple-field tachistoscopic software program. The participants were given a detection task in which they manually responded to the presence or absence of a critical target letter (M) that could appear in one of two lateralized spatial locations: one located to the left and one located to the right of a central fixation point (see Figure 1). On some trials, the test display was preceded by an informative precue that indicated which location would most likely contain the target letter. On other trials, the test display was preceded by a neutral precue that provided no information regarding the most likely target location.

Figure 1. Examples of stimulus displays. Although not depicted here, there were also nontarget trials in which only one of the two squares was outlined. M = target letter; W = nontarget letter

Precue display

The different types of precues were superimposed on a fixation display. This display consisted of two empty black squares, one located to the left and one located to the right of the central fixation point. The fixation point was a black dot that was 0.6 cm in diameter and was visible at all times. Participants viewed this display from a distance of approximately 50 cm, where the side of each square subtended 1.38° visual angle. The distance between the center of the fixation point and the center of one of the empty squares subtended 3.44° visual angle. A random two thirds of the trials contained an informative precue, and the remaining one third contained a neutral precue. On trials in which an informative precue was presented, one of the two squares was outlined. This indicated that if the target letter was presented, then it would most likely appear in that square. This cue correctly indicated the subsequent target location on a random two thirds of the informative precue trials (valid cue condition) and incorrectly indicated its location on the remaining one third of the trials (invalid cue condition). Each of the two empty squares was outlined equally often. On trials in which a neutral precue was presented, both of the empty black squares were outlined. This cue provided no information regarding the probable target location.

Test display

The test display consisted of uppercase black letters that were briefly and simultaneously presented in each of the two squares. On target trials, the target letter M appeared in one of the squares, and the nontarget letter W appeared in the other square (the letter M was equally likely to appear in each of the two squares). On nontarget trials, the letter W appeared in both squares. A random two thirds of the trials were target trials, and the remaining one third were nontarget trials. Examples of the stimulus displays are presented in Figure 1.

A trial began with a tone and the presentation of the fixation display for 500 ms; this was immediately followed by the precue display for 75 ms. After an interstimulus interval of 90 ms (stimulus onset asynchrony [SOA] = 165 ms) the test display was presented. The exposure duration of the test display was predetermined for each participant during a preliminary threshold testing session to avoid both ceiling and floor effects. Each trial lasted approximately 6 s.

Procedure

The participants were told that their task was to decide whether or not a critical target letter M was presented in one of the two empty squares. They were told that sometimes they would be given a hint (i.e., when one of the squares was outlined) about where the target letter would most likely appear, and other times they would not be given a hint (i.e., when both squares were outlined). The participants were instructed to respond with the index finger of their right hand; a key press to one of two horizontally aligned keys labeled “yes” and “no” recorded responses.

The participants were required to center their gaze on the fixation point and not to move their eyes during the task. They were warned that if they moved their eyes to one side of the screen, they might miss what appeared on the other side of the screen because the displays were flashed very briefly. Given such a short SOA, participants did not have time to initiate an eye movement (which takes approximately 200–250 ms) to the precued location before the test display was presented (for further explanation, see Jones & Santi, 1970; Posner, Snyder, & Davidson, 1980).

Each participant was administered six blocks of 54 trials. Each block of trials took approximately 5.5 min, and the entire experiment took approximately 1.5 hr. Brief rest periods were given after each block, and a longer rest period was given halfway through the testing session.

Results and Discussion

For each participant, we used the proportion of hits (i.e., correctly indicating the presence of the target letter on a target present trial) and the proportion of false alarms (i.e., incorrectly indicating the presence of the target letter on a nontarget trial) to estimate d′ values separately for valid, neutral, and invalid cued trials for targets presented in the LVF and in the RVF (see d′ table from Green & Swets, 1966). The larger the d′ value, the better the detection sensitivity. The magnitude of the benefits was calculated by subtracting the mean d′ value for the neutral cue condition from the mean d′ value for the valid cue condition. The magnitude of the costs was calculated by subtracting the mean d′ value for the invalid cue condition from the mean d′ value for the neutral cue condition. The results are divided into separate sections, each of which presents analyses that evaluate the main questions we designed this study to address.

Evidence for RH Specialization for Selective Attentional Processing in Young Nonalcoholic Control Participants

An analysis was performed on the data for the young nonalcoholic controls in order to establish hemispheric specialization for selective attentional processing. The within-subjects factors were visual field of the target (LVF, RVF) and cue validity (valid, neutral, invalid). The analysis revealed a significant Visual Field × Cue Validity interaction, F(2, 22) = 3.89, p < .05. The mean d′ values for each cue condition for LVF and RVF targets are depicted in the top portion of Figure 2. Means comparisons indicated that following LH activation, benefits were significant, F(1, 22) = 19.90, p < .001, and although the costs were not significant, F(1, 22) = 2.45, the means were in the expected direction (i.e., a smaller d′ on invalid than on neutral cue trials). Following RH activation, benefits were marginally significant, F(1, 22) = 3.56, p < .10, and there was a reversed cost effect. In other words, when an invalid cue activated the RH (and the target appeared in the RVF), detection sensitivity was enhanced relative to the neutral cue condition.

Figure 2. d ′ values (and standard errors) for targets presented in each visual field as a function of cue validity for the younger and older nonalcoholic control participants. LVF = left visual field; RVF = right visual field

An analysis on the magnitude of benefit and cost effects also revealed a significant interaction with visual field, F(2, 22) = 8.85, p < .01. Means comparisons indicated that the magnitude of the benefits following RH activation (0.21) was significantly less than following LH activation (0.49), F(1, 22) = 5.57, p < .05. Also, because of the reversed cost effect following RH activation, there were costs associated with LH activation (0.17) but not with RH activation (−0.25), F(1, 22) = 12.60, p < .01.

On this selective attention task, the results for the young nonalcoholic participants revealed that the magnitude of the benefits and costs differed as a function of which hemisphere was activated by the cue. Following LH activation by the cue, the findings indicated that attention was primarily directed toward the right (cued) side of space; there was both an advantage for benefits from valid cues and a slight, but nonsignificant cost associated with invalid cues. Following RH activation, the results indicated that the RH's ability to direct attention to a larger portion of the spatial array resulted in no differential effect of cue validity; in fact, performance was enhanced on both valid and invalid cue trials. The results for the young nonalcoholic controls were, therefore, consistent with the operational definition of RH specialization for selective attention; the cue was less critical in helping to detect the target letter, and the magnitude of the benefit and cost effects were smaller following RH activation than following LH activation.

Evidence for the Effects of Aging on RH Performance in the Nonalcoholic Population

An analysis was performed on the data for both the younger and the older nonalcoholic controls in order to assess the effects of aging on hemispheric specialization for selective attentional processing. As before, the within-subjects factors were visual field of the target (LVF, RVF) and cue validity (valid, neutral, invalid), and the between-subjects factor was age (young, 49 years and younger; older, 50 years and older). The analysis revealed a significant Age × Visual Field × Cue Validity interaction, F(2, 70) = 3.12, p < .05. The mean d′ values for each cue condition for LVF and RVF targets for the older participants are depicted in the bottom portion of Figure 2.

As in the analysis for the younger participants, the Visual Field × Cue Validity interaction was significant for the older participants, F(2, 48) = 3.67, p < .05, but the pattern of results was different. Means comparisons for the older control participants indicated that following LH activation, neither benefits, F(1, 48) < 1, nor costs, F(1, 48) < 1, were significant. Following RH activation, however, benefits were significant, F(1, 48) = 5.47, p < .05, but costs were not, F(1, 48) < 1. Additional analyses on the difference scores indicated that the magnitude of the benefits was significantly greater following RH activation (0.27) than following LH activation (−0.09), F(1, 48) = 14.36, p < .001, whereas the magnitude of the costs did not differ significantly following either LH (0.10) or RH (0.06) activation, F(1, 48) < 1.

The main question of interest is whether the pattern of results for the older participants is indicative of RH functional decline. Recall that for the younger participants, an RH advantage for selective attentional processing was characterized by an ability to attend to both sides of space following RH activation, such that the validity of the cue was less critical in directing attention. According to this operational definition of RH specialization, the results for the older controls may be partially consistent with RH functional decline. For the older controls, a significant benefit following RH activation suggests that attention was primarily directed to the LVF and not to both sides of space. Also consistent with this explanation is the finding that detection sensitivity on invalid cue trials was lower than on neutral cue trials for both visual fields; in other words, unlike the younger controls, the older controls did not demonstrate enhanced processing following RH activation regardless of cue validity. However, a deficit in the ability of the RH to direct attention to both sides of space presumably should not interfere with the LH's ability to process information in the right side of space. Thus, the lack of a significant benefit following LH activation poses a problem for a purely RH dysfunction interpretation of the results. Taken together, the older nonalcoholic participants showed a decreased ability to detect targets following LH activation, and they also did not appear to exhibit a preserved ability to attend to a larger region of the visual field following RH activation.

Evidence for the Effects of Alcoholism on RH Performance

A third goal of this experiment was to assess whether alcoholic participants showed deficits on a selective attention task relative to nonalcoholic control participants and, if so, whether the pattern of results could be accounted for by the RH model of alcoholism-related cognitive decline.

The Age × Group × Visual Field × Cue Validity interaction was not significant, F(2, 138) = 1.33. However, the Group × Visual Field × Cue Validity interaction was significant, F(2, 142) = 3.72, p < .05, indicating that, when collapsed across age group, the pattern of results for the alcoholic participants differed from that of the nonalcoholic control participants. This interaction with group was due to the fact that the Visual Field × Cue Validity interaction was significant for the nonalcoholic controls, F(2, 72) = 3.19, p < .05, but not for the alcoholic participants, F(2, 70) = 1.03. The mean d′ values for each cue condition for LVF and RVF targets for the nonalcoholic and alcoholic participants (collapsed across age groups) are depicted in Figure 3.

Figure 3. d ′ values (and standard errors) for targets presented in each visual field as a function of cue validity for the nonalcoholic and alcoholic participants (collapsed across age). LVF = left visual field; RVF = right visual field

Means comparisons for the nonalcoholic control participants indicated that benefits were significant following RH activation, F(1, 72) = 7.93, p < .01, but were not significant following LH activation, F(1, 72) = 1.12. Costs were not significant following either LH activation, F(1, 72) = 1.86, or RH activation, F(1, 72) < 1. Additional analyses on the difference scores indicated two trends: First, the magnitude of the benefits was greater following RH activation (0.25) than following LH activation (0.09), F(1, 72) = 3.36, p < .10; and second, the magnitude of the costs were greater following LH activation (0.12) than following RH activation (−0.04), F(1, 72) = 3.57, p < .10.

It is not surprising that the pattern of results for the nonalcoholic control group (collapsed across age) is very similar to that of the older control group given that they made up two thirds of the participants in the group. This accounts for why the results indicated greater benefits following RH activation. However, the visual field asymmetry for cost effects found with the younger nonalcoholic controls was still present in the analysis on the whole group of nonalcoholic controls. Specifically, participants were affected by the invalidity of the cue following LH activation but not following RH activation. Thus, these results support the notion that the RH has an advantage in selective attentional processing in that it can attend to a larger portion of the visual field than can the LH.

Means comparisons for the alcoholic participants indicated that benefits were significant following LH activation, F(1, 70) = 10.63, p < .01, but not following RH activation, F(1, 70) = 2.61. Costs were not significant following either LH activation, F(1, 70) = 1.92, or RH activation, F(1, 70) = 2.56. Additional analyses on the difference scores indicated that the magnitude of the alcoholic participants' benefits did not differ significantly following RH activation (0.15) and LH activation (0.30), F(1, 70) = 2.80. The alcoholic participants also did not show significant evidence of a visual field asymmetry for costs, F(1, 70) < 1; the magnitude of the costs was 0.13 following LH activation and 0.15 following RH activation.

Thus, unlike the nonalcoholic controls, the alcoholic participants did not show evidence of an asymmetric cost effect; that is, invalid cues hindered performance following both LH and RH activation. Furthermore, the alcoholic participants did not benefit from valid cues following RH activation. Taken together, the findings for the alcoholic participants were consistent with the RH model of alcoholism-related cognitive decline, but not with the premature aging hypothesis (i.e., none of the Group × Age interactions with analyses of variance [ANOVAs] were statistically reliable). However, to further address the premature aging hypothesis, we used regression analyses to explore a possible interaction between the effects of aging and alcoholism.

Effects of Alcoholism Interact With Effects of Aging

To investigate the importance of each of the independent (predictor) variables (group, age, gender, years of sobriety, years of >21 drinks per week, and QFI score) on visual field effects, we first ran separate multiple regression analyses on data from the following: valid LVF, neutral LVF, invalid LVF, valid RVF, neutral RVF, and invalid RVF. In all six of the multiple regression analyses, age and the three drinking variables were treated as continuous variables. None of the six multiple regression analyses proved to be significant. Next, because we were interested in determining whether there was a possible interaction between the separate predictor variables of age and group, we ran six analyses of covariance (ANCOVAs) through regression analyses. We found that the inclusion of this interaction factor greatly reduced the probability of making a Type I error in all but one case (i.e., neutral RVF). In addition, as can be seen in Table 2, the models containing data from the LVF in all of the ANCOVAs showed significant effects of group (alcoholic participants performed more poorly than controls) as well as a significant interaction between group and age (p < .05). Of interest, the Group × Age interaction indicated that alcoholism's LVF effects decreased with age.

Significance of Predictor Variables in the Presence of Interactions in the Analyses of Covariance

We also performed principal-components analyses to (a) minimize the effects of multiple comparisons and (b) determine which dependent variables went together in clusters. Results of the principal-components analyses showed clusters among all of the LVF variables and among all of the RVF variables. In other words, across participants, high scores on valid, invalid, and neutral cue conditions occurred together for each visual field separately. To determine the effects of the predictor variables on each of the two visual fields, we ran ANCOVAs through multiple regressions (as described earlier). This time, the dependent variables were the sums of all three LVF scores and the sums of all the RVF scores, and the independent variables were the separate predictors (group, age, Group × Age, gender, and the three drinking variables) and interactions among them.

By using stepwise selection, we found that the model for the LVF was significant (p < .05), and the significant predictors were group (alcoholic participants were worse than controls, p < .05), age (older participants were worse than younger participants, p < .05), and Group × Age (the effects of alcoholism decreased with age, p < .05). In other words, the effects of alcoholism and aging were in the direction we expected; that is, performance declined, but the interaction between alcoholism and aging was positive such that the magnitude of alcoholism-related decline decreased with aging. The model for the RVF also was significant (p < .05). Here the significant predictors were group (alcoholic participants were worse than controls, p < .05), age (older participants were worse than younger participants, p < .05), and gender (women performed better than men, p < .01), and there was a significant Age × Gender interaction (aging harmed men less than women, p < .05).

Like the ANOVAs reported above, the results from these analyses do not appear to support the premature aging hypothesis. In fact, the analyses indicated that although the alcoholic participants performed worse overall than the controls, older alcoholic participants performed better than the younger alcoholic participants did. It is not clear what might account for this pattern of results. The fact that this interaction was significant only when the targets were presented in the LVF, however, is consistent with the hypothesis that RH functioning is uniquely affected in alcoholic individuals.

General Discussion

In the present study, we used a cued target detection task to establish RH specialization for selective attention in neurologically healthy young control participants as well as to asses potential RH functional decline in aging and alcoholic individuals. The results from the present study suggest the following: (a) The RH appears to play a more specialized role in selective attentional processing in that it has the ability to attend to a larger spatial array than does the LH and is thus less reliant on cues to direct attention; (b) there was an age-related difference in the pattern of results for the nonalcoholic participants such that older nonalcoholic participants showed a decreased ability to detect targets following LH activation, and they did not exhibit a preserved ability to attend to a larger region of the visual field following RH activation; (c) the pattern of results for the alcoholic participants was consistent with the RH model of alcoholism-related cognitive decline; and (d) the pattern of results does not support the premature aging hypothesis; in fact, they suggest that the detrimental effects of alcoholism on RH performance may decrease with age.

Although the present results support the RH hypothesis of alcoholism-related cognitive decline, it is important to note that this model cannot account for all the difficulties observed in alcoholic individuals. Thus, other views of alcoholism-related cognitive decline have been put forward. The frontal systems hypothesis has been proposed to account for findings of abstraction and problem-solving difficulties as well as personality and emotional changes observed in alcoholic participants (Oscar-Berman, 2000). Both the RH and the frontal systems hypotheses are limited in scope, however, because the former does not fully explain deficits in executive functioning and personality changes, and the latter does not explain functional changes generally associated with decline in more posterior regions of the RH (Oscar-Berman & Schendan, 2000). Thus, the diffuse brain dysfunction model has been proposed to account for the widespread deficits exhibited by alcoholic participants (Parsons, 1987; Rourke & Loberg, 1996). This model, however, lacks sufficient heuristic and empirical value to facilitate specific theoretical predictions of alcoholic participants' performance on cognitive tasks. Instead, several theoretical models are needed to completely account for the personality and cognitive changes observed in chronic alcoholic participants. This is not surprising because alcoholism is a multifaceted condition with a variety of genetic, environmental, and constitutional influences (Noronha, Eckardt, & Warren, 2000).

1 Although behavioral evidence supporting RH specialization for selective attention using cued-detection/response time tasks is mixed under conditions in which a target is presented alone in the visual field (Corbetta, Miezin, Shulman, & Petersen, 1993; Nagel-Leiby, Buchtel, & Welch, 1990; Petersen, Robinson, & Currie, 1989; Posner, Walker, Friedrich, & Rafal, 1984; Verfaellie, Bowers, & Heilman, 1988), RH specialization is evident under conditions in which the perceptual load of the task is increased (Evert, McGlinchey-Berroth, Verfaellie, & Milberg, 2000; Roy, Reuter-Lorenz, Roy, Copland, & Moscovitch, 1987). Thus, in the present study, we simultaneously presented a target in one visual field and a distractor letter in the opposing visual field to increase the perceptual load, which we expected would necessitate selective attentional processing and would thus reveal RH specialization.

2 Jonides and Mack (1984) pointed out that it is operationally difficult to create a truly neutral condition; for this reason, some researchers have abandoned cost-benefit analyses in favor of simply looking at the magnitude of the difference between valid and invalid cue trials (i.e., overall cue-validity effects). However, even if a significant visual field difference is found in the cue-validity effects, it is not clear whether a larger or a smaller cue-validity effect represents greater hemispheric specialization. Furthermore, one would not be able to determine the locus of this effect (e.g., the engagement of selective attention, the disengagement and moving of attention, or some combination thereof). Thus, it is not clear how one could interpret a visual field difference in overall cue-validity effects without taking the neutral cue condition into consideration. In the present study, we felt that it was important to consider the data by including the neutral cue condition and keeping in mind its potential difficulties.

3 Criteria for classifying someone as alcoholic vary (see Abel, Kruger, & Friedl, 1999; Eckardt et al., 1998). The amount and duration of ethanol consumption that characterize heavy drinking, alcohol abuse, and alcohol dependence depends on many factors including age, gender, ethnicity, nutrition, physical condition, and health status as well as social factors (see Oscar-Berman & Evert, 1997). A minimum criterion of at least 5 years of drinking 21 or more drinks per week is common among studies assessing alcohol-related cognitive decline (e.g., see Eckardt et al., 1998; Gansler et al., 2000; Hutner & Oscar-Berman, 1996). It is important to note, however, that problem-based criteria, not quantity and frequency of consumption, make up a definition for alcohol-use disorders (e.g., see Dawson, 1994, 2000).

4 Each participant was given two practice blocks at the beginning of the testing session. On the basis of each individual participant's overall error rate on the second block, we adjusted the duration of the test display so as to avoid both ceiling and floor effects. The participant was then given two experimental blocks. Because performance could vary depending on the cue-target condition, we performed preliminary data analysis on the first two experimental blocks and administered an unrelated task to the participant. On the basis of this analysis, we made further adjustments to the test display duration, if necessary, and administered six more blocks of trials to the participant to be used in the final data analysis.

5 Trials in which the participant gave no response were excluded from the data analysis (this was less than 0.20% for both the controls and the alcoholic participants). Trials in which a particular participant's response times were more than 2 standard deviations beyond his or her overall mean response time were excluded. This procedure resulted in eliminating, on average, 16 data points for each of the control participants and 14 data points for each of the alcoholic participants.

6 Because the neutral cue condition is a baseline from which benefit and cost effects are calculated to determine whether there are hemispheric asymmetries, we conducted analyses to see whether the results might be due to artifacts associated with the neutral cue condition. After averaging the d′ values for the LVF and the RVF neutral conditions, we found that the magnitude of the benefits did not significantly differ following RH (0.31) and LH (0.39) activation, F(1, 22) = 0.62, but there was still a hemispheric asymmetry for cost effects, F(1, 22) = 4.61, p < .05. As before, when the invalid cue activated the LH, there was a cost effect (0.07), but when the cue activated the RH, processing was enhanced relative to the neutral condition (−0.15). Consistent with the original data, these analyses confirm that the RH has an advantage in that it can attend to a wider spatial region and thus is not as affected as the LH by the invalidity of the cue.
