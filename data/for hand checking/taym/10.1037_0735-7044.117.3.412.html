Olfaction is known to be important for social behavior (Cheal & Sprott, 1971; Engelmann, Wotjak, & Landgraf, 1995; Schultz & Tapp, 1973). It is also commonly admitted that odor cues contribute to spatial orientation in rats (Buresova & Bures, 1981; Lavenex & Schenk, 1996). Vision plays a critical role in navigation in rodents (Knierim, Kudrimoti, & McNaughton, 1998; Maaswinkel & Whishaw, 1999), but the relative importance of these two sensory modalities in various conditions is not yet clearly understood.

We have designed experimental procedures aimed at comparing the contribution of olfaction and vision in order to find out in which conditions these two sets of sensory information may cooperate for place learning (Rossier & Schenk, 2000). We hypothesized that the relative weight of vision and olfaction is linked with the maturation of systems contributing to the processing of spatial information. We have conducted a series of experiments to assess the relative weight of olfactory and visual cues for spatial navigation, that is, a navigation based on a representation of space in which the goal and the position of the animal are coded (O'Keefe & Nadel, 1978). We worked in a controlled environment and compared different age groups through the life span.

Sensory Integration in Spatial Learning

A large number of studies have been designed to analyze the specific contribution of each sensory modality to spatial behavior (see Honzik, 1936a, 1936b). In adult rats, configuration of visual cues plays a major role in place learning and retention (Suzuki, Augerinos, & Black, 1980), whereas olfaction seems to play a minor role (Lavenex & Schenk, 1995). Moreover, kinesthesia is an important component for spatial learning. This sense must cooperate with all the others because it provides accurate information about self-motion (Etienne, 1992; Etienne et al., 1998; Etienne, Maurer, & Séguinot, 1996; Lalonde, 1997; Whishaw & Gorny, 1999; Whishaw & Maaswinkel, 1998). Tactile and auditory cue-based orientation is seldom assessed, though rats can rely on auditory cues for place learning in specific conditions (Rossier, Haeberli, & Schenk, 2000).

Most studies of spatial memory focus on the use of visual cues. Experimental procedures, such as the well-known water maze (Morris, 1981), encourage the use of vision to discriminate the training position. The distant landmarks are of greater importance, probably because they appear more stable to a moving subject (Cressant, Muller, & Poucet, 1999; Save & Poucet, 2000). Nevertheless, proximal visual cues are also used to solve spatial tasks when they are relevant (Brandner & Schenk, 1998; Vollmer-Conna & Lemon, 1998). Studies concerned with the processing and computation of visual cues often consider olfactory cues as a source of bias. Olfactory information is generally made irrelevant in spatial tasks, and the importance of these cues is thus not frequently studied per se.

Olfaction in Spatial Learning

Reliance on olfactory cues is mainly assessed in discrimination tasks (Lu, Slotnick, & Silberberg, 1993; Polan & Hofer, 1998; Schul, Slotnick, & Dudai, 1996). Rats are good at processing olfactory information (Staubli, Fraser, Faraday, & Lynch, 1987) and are able to use it to make route choices (Still & Macmillan, 1975) or to follow trails (Benhamou, 1989; Jamon, 1994; Means, Hardy, Gabriel, & Uphold, 1971). An experiment by Buresova and Bures (1981) revealed that allowing rats to have access to a large number of cues from different modalities improved their performance in a radial maze, whereas olfactory cues alone did not support efficient arm choice (Olton & Collison, 1979; Olton & Samuelson, 1976; Zoladek & Roberts, 1978). Lavenex and Schenk (1997) demonstrated the use of proximal olfactory cues by rats for place discrimination and memory. When the goal location was marked by a local olfactory cue, rats were faster in learning the task and had a more precise memory of the goal location, even after removal of this cue. Olfaction participates in spatial behaviors, especially when the cues are directly associated with specific places or when animals can rely on spatially distributed olfactory traces (Lavenex & Schenk, 1995, 1996, 1998).

Rats are crepuscular, and we can assume that olfaction plays a role in the dark. Indeed, Lavenex and Schenk (1996) showed that in total darkness, rats performed very accurately in a radial maze with eight different odors marking each tunnel roof. Moreover, following an interruption, they were capable of selecting individual unvisited arms in spite of maze reorganization. In an illuminated environment, however, rats did not rely on olfactory information to perform the maze task, even when vision did not provide relevant information, as in translucent tunnels. They reacted to the rearrangement of the olfactory cues, showing that they were aware of the presence of the cues and had memorized their relative position. However, they did not use them explicitly. These results suggested that light reduces the saliency of olfactory cues for arm choice. The authors concluded that, in the dark, rats are able to rely on a spatial representation based on olfactory information and movement-derived cues. Moreover, in this condition, arm choice was accurate despite maze reorganization. This suggests that in the dark, arm selection can be based on discrete olfactory cues. In illuminated environments, olfactory information is integrated in a spatial representation only if it is congruous with the otherwise available information. This suggests that a hierarchy of sensory modalities contributes to spatial memory (Knierim, Kudrimoti, & McNaughton, 1995; Maaswinkel & Whishaw, 1999; Rossier, 2000).

Ontogeny of Spatial Learning

The acquisition of spatial abilities such as map-based navigation requires a learning phase and includes the contribution of several sensory processes (Cooper, Miya, & Mizumori, 1998). The different sensory systems might develop in discrete steps through ontogeny. Immature animals seem first to be sensitive to tactile, olfactory, or gustatory stimuli (Amiri, Dark, Noce, & Kirstein, 1998; Gregory & Pfaff, 1971; Koffman, Petrov, Varlinskaya, & Smotherman, 1998; Polan & Hofer, 1999; Schapiro & Salas, 1970). In particular, the olfactory system is already partially functional before birth, but cellular proliferation continues until Day 30 postnatal (Kurcharski & Hall, 1988; Lopez-Mascaraque, De Carlos, & Valverde, 1996; Singh, 1977). The maturation of the visual system occurs later. Even though their eyes are not fully open until the age of about 15 days, immature rats start to move about at the age of 12 days. At this age, they are able to home from a distance of 20 centimeters (Bulut & Altman, 1974). Rats swim efficiently at Postnatal Day 16 (Schapiro, Salas, & Vukovich, 1970), but they need to reach 32–35 days of age to accurately achieve a place navigation task in a water maze in the absence of any intramaze cue (Schenk, 1985). This has been interpreted as a consequence of the slow maturation of the hippocampus until the 2nd month of age (Pokorny & Yamamoto, 1981a, 1981b).

Before Postnatal Day 20, rat pups behave somewhat like adult rats with a lesion of the hippocampus. Prior to the age of 25 days, rats do not learn an alternation task in a T-maze (Bronstein, Dworkin, & Bilder, 1974). The development of behavioral capacities is related to the growth of the central nervous system and to motor development. For example, exploratory behaviors change during development as animals become more active (Candland & Campbell, 1962). This has been interpreted as a sign of sustained attention to the environment (Renner, Bennett, & White, 1992). Of course, young rats' abilities depend on task conditions. Seventeen-day-old rats were able to achieve a beacon navigation task (to reach a cued place), whereas they failed in reaching the goal location when it was not cued (Rudy, Stadler-Morris, & Albert, 1987; Schenk, 1987). According to Brandeis, Brandys, and Yehuda (1989), rats are able to perform a place navigation task from about the age of 25 days. Thus, there is a dissociation between these two types of navigation abilities during ontogeny. Moreover, because there is no interocular equivalence before Postnatal Day 28 in the rat, spatial memory requires a convergence of the neural pathways originating from each eye onto common memory sites (Rudy & Paylor, 1987; Rudy & Stadler-Morris, 1987). Finally, not only learning, but also long-term memory, is affected by maturation. Indeed, long-term retention of a place is impaired in 33-day-old rats compared with adults (Brown & Kraemer, 1997; Spreng, Rossier, & Schenk, 2002).

Spatial memory relies on the integration of information from different sensory modalities, and this implies processing at multiple levels (Rossier, Haeberli & Schenk, 2000; Schenk, Grobéty, Lavenex, & Lipp, 1995). Spatial cognition in adult rats relies on several components or brain modules (Bures et al., 1997; Morris, Schenk, Tweedie, & Jarrard, 1990), which may not mature simultaneously. Comparing spatial navigation abilities at different ages in a controlled environment might reveal the development of the processes underlying spatial cognition. This should also indicate whether and how distant landmarks from different sensory modalities are used at different stages. The aim of the present study was to determine from which age rats are able to build a spatial representation based on the integration of relational olfactory and visual cues and at which age vision might become predominant. More specifically, the question was whether there are some differences between the use of olfactory and visual information in performing spatial navigation. An important question was whether simultaneously available visual and olfactory cues would be processed in an additive way or whether the integration of both types of cues would lead to a more robust representation.

To answer these questions, we trained four age groups in three main conditions. The first condition allowed rats to use a configuration of three discrete visual light cues (Rossier, Grobéty, & Schenk, 2000). The second provided a configuration of five olfactory cues that were dissociated from the five possible escape positions. In the third condition, rats were trained with both types of cues during the first 2 days. Further training was conducted following removal of the olfactory or the visual cues. This allowed an assessment of the impact of the olfactory cues on the use of the visual cues both before and after the removal of the olfactory cues.

Method

Subjects

One hundred thirty-six naive Long-Evans hooded rats (68 females and 68 males) of four age groups (24 days, 48 days, 3–6 months, and 12 months at the onset of the experiment) were used, in compliance with Swiss guidelines for the care and use of laboratory animals. The rats were housed in groups of 4 in large glass containers (40 cm × 40 cm × 50 cm) with a wooden nest box and various wooden pieces intended to encourage climbing and movement through holes. This can be considered as a semi-enriched rearing condition. Twenty-four-day-old and 48-day-old rats had a free access to food and water. For adults and 12-month-old rats, daily food rations were delivered immediately following testing and were adjusted to prevent any weight increase throughout the duration of the experiment (5 days). The rats were kept in a natural light/dark cycle and were tested in the afternoon.

Apparatus

A large circular board (170 cm diameter) with no wall was used (Rossier, Grobéty, & Schenk, 2000; Schenk, 1989). The platform was provided with five possible escape holes, as shown in Figure 1. Each hole was covered with a disk of light plastic, which was the same color as the platform and could easily be removed by the rats. The detachable fittings of the holes were interchangeable and washable. One hole only allowed passage to the subject's home cage placed under the table. The fitting for the goal was connected to a flexible tube and hidden by a light plastic disk. Dead-end holes were blocked with a thick piece of foam rubber hidden under the plastic disk. The empty home cage was placed under the table and connected to the flexible tube, which was curved in order to minimize its slope. The extremity of the tube (length 50 cm) was simply deposited in the open cage. The home cage and the connected tube could not be seen from the surface of the open field. Two infrared projectors lit the homing board (Infralite IR-RS 4060, narrow-band light emission, wavelength 880 nm; Institute of Physiology, Lausanne, Switzerland). An infrared video camera placed directly above the center of the table allowed recording of each session. An observer was able to view the rat's movements through an infrared viewer (Find-R-Scope 85100; Cotraco Establishment, Vaduz, Liechtenstein).

Behavioral Procedure

In a room separate from the testing room, all the rats were pretrained on a small platform to enter a covered hole connected to a tunnel until they removed the cover and spontaneously entered the hole. Daily sessions on the homing board consisted of five training trials. Pseudorandom sequences of five different starting positions were used in each training session. Individual rats remained on the table until they found the escape hole, at a fixed spatial location throughout training, or until 120 s had elapsed. Rats not able to locate the escape hole in the allotted time were gently guided. During the initial trials of the first session, the connected hole was only partially masked by the cover, which the rats rapidly learned to push aside to reach the home cage. By the end of the first session, the connected hole was completely covered.

A drop of condensed milk was available in the home cage to maintain a stable escape motivation. After the rat had reached the cage, it was placed in a closed bucket for the 2-min intertrial interval. The position of the bucket was varied from trial to trial so that it did not represent a fixed spatial reference position. After each trial, the escape hole and covering were replaced by clean ones. For conditions with no olfactory cues, the table was rotated between trials in a nonsystematic fashion. The intertrial rotation did not affect the configuration between the cues and the escape location but made olfactory traces inconsistent. For conditions with olfactory cues, all plastic disks were replaced with clean ones between trials in order to control the olfactory traces. Access to visual cues was controlled by conducting all experiments in total darkness.

Probe trials were conducted to assess the spatial bias toward the training position in the different conditions. In these trials, the hole on the training position was not connected to the home cage and, therefore, was not different from the other holes. After 72 s, the hole on the training position was connected and the rat was allowed access to its home cage. An additional reinforced trial was given immediately following the probe trial, to avoid any extinction effects.

All trials were videotaped and analyzed later. The latency to uncover the escape hole (escape latency) was recorded. The distance covered to reach the correct hole (escape distance) was calculated for Sessions 2 and 5. An XY video tracker (Kukam, Lausanne, Switzerland) analyzed all the probe trials (period of 72 s), and the time spent in each hole sector (20 cm diameter) during the probe trials was calculated.

Experimental Conditions

Three main conditions were created, depending on whether the rats were trained in total darkness with visual cues and/or olfactory cues:

  1. In the Visual Cues condition, rats were allowed to see three light cues. Each cue was identical and made of three light emitting diodes. The cues were placed 50 cm from the table limit and at the platform level. These cues were placed in three different positions at the same distance and height of the table. The escape hole was defined only by distant visuospatial cues; no relevant olfactory cues were available (Rossier, Grobéty & Schenk, 2000; Rossier, Kaminsky, Schenk, & Bures, 2000).
  2. In the Olfactory Cues condition, rats could use five distant olfactory cues made of scented Velcro tapes placed on the table (blackberry, caramel, apricot, anise, and tangerine). Each tape was placed in a closed bottle containing 2 ml of an alimentary aroma during the hour preceding the test. Clean plastic disks masked the olfactory traces left in the hole areas. In that condition, no visual cue was available and the training hole was only related to distant olfactory cues.
  3. In the V+O Cues then V Cues only condition, rats could use the three light cues and the five olfactory cues during the first 2 days. Afterward, only the visual cues were available.

In order to assess whether immature rats of 24 days of age at the onset of the experiment were able to perceive the visual cues and to be sure that they were not able to take advantage of a transitory training with both types of cues to use the visual one (see the Results section), we used two additional conditions:

  1. In the Beacon Navigation condition, rats could benefit from a unique visual cue situated just behind the goal location, a condition studied in the water maze task (Rossier, Haeberli, & Schenk, 2000).
  2. In the Prolonged V+O Cues then V Cues only condition, the three light cues and the five olfactory cues were permanently available during the first 5 days. Afterward, only the visual cues were available during 5 more days.

The results of the 3–6-month-old rats in the V+O Cues then V Cues only condition showed clearly that olfactory cues increase the accuracy in using visual cues for place navigation in total darkness (see the Results section). To study whether prior training with visual and olfactory cues could also increase the accuracy in using olfactory cues, we added the following condition:

  1. In the V+O Cues then O Cues only condition, the three light cues and the five olfactory cues were available during the first 2 days. Only the olfactory cues remained available during further training.

Experimental Design

Twelve experimental groups (four ages × three conditions) and three additional groups were trained according to the conditions described above. All groups were composed of an equivalent number of males and females in order to evaluate the impact of this variable on spatial abilities. The 48-day-old and 3–6-month-old groups were composed of 8 rats, whereas the 24-day-old and 12-month-old groups were composed of 10 rats in order to decrease the impact of the possible larger dispersion of the results of youngest and oldest age groups. All the groups were trained for 5 daily sessions with 5 training trials each day (25 training trials). They were given two standard probe trials (T1 and T2) with the cue condition unchanged relative to the training trials conducted just before at the end of the 2nd and 4th session (see Table 1). A third probe trial was conducted at the end of the fifth session, following a +90° rotation of the visual or olfactory cues (T3R). The Prolonged V+O Cues then V Cues only group was given 5 additional training sessions (for a total of 50 training trials) and three additional probe trials after the 7th, 9th, and 10th session (two standard trials, and the last with rotation [T4, T5, T6R]).

Results

24-Day-Old Rats

The escape latency of the three experimental groups decreased throughout training, indicating that all groups learned to escape through the connected hole (see Figure 2). Nevertheless, rats that could use olfactory cues were clearly quicker. The mean latency of the V+O then V Cues only group increased significantly after removal of the olfactory cues, F(1, 9) = 18.9, p =.002. A three-way repeated measures analysis of variance (ANOVA) was conducted over the five training sessions, with condition and sex as variables. There was a significant condition effect, F(2, 24) = 8.3, p =.002, and a significant decrease in latency over training, F(4, 96) = 16.0, p <.001. The interactions Condition × Sex, F(2, 24) = 4.0, p =.03, and Training Sessions × Condition, F(8, 96) = 3.8, p =.001, were also significant. The sex variable and all other interactions were nonsignificant.

Figure 2. Mean (±SEM) escape latency through training sessions on the hole board for all age groups (one five-trial session per day). V = visual; O = olfactory

Analyses of the escape distances during Days 2 and 5 confirmed the feature provided by the latencies. A three-way repeated measures ANOVA was conducted on Sessions 2 and 5 with condition and sex as variables. The training sessions, F(1, 24) = 11.2, p =.003, and the interaction Training Sessions × Condition, F(2, 24) = 6.3, p =.006, were significant. All other variables or interactions were nonsignificant.

During the standard probe trials, the rats were left on the table for 72 s while the escape hole was not connected. Three such probe trials were conducted, at the end of Sessions 2, 4, and 5. Statistical analyses were based on mean results. T1 and T2 were combined for the Visual Cues and Olfactory Cues groups, and T2 and T3R were combined for the V+O then V Cues only group, because there was no significant difference between the discrimination in both probe trials. Figure 3 shows that during T1 and T2, rats in the Visual Cues condition were not able to discriminate the correct location, confirmed by a two-way repeated measures ANOVA with sex as a variable. For this age group, the sex factor and the interaction of sex and sectors were never significant. For this reason, we will not specify it anymore. A two-way ANOVA indicated that rats in the Visual Cues condition were unable to discriminate the correct location at the end of training during T3R. On the other hand, rats in the Olfactory Cues condition accurately discriminated the correct sector during T1 and T2. A two-way repeated measures ANOVA with sex as a variable confirmed the bias toward the correct location, F(4, 32) = 9.4, p <.001. Rats with only olfactory cues followed the rotation during T3R and had a strong bias in favor of the olfactory defined sector, F(4, 32) = 16.3, p <.001. The rats in the V+O then V Cues only condition were good in discriminating the correct location in the presence of the olfactory cues during Probe Trial 1, F(4, 32) = 9.9, p <.001. After deletion of the olfactory cues (Probe Trials 2 and 3R), this group did not discriminate the goal location anymore.

The discrimination of the training sector in the different groups is based on separate ANOVAs of the absolute time in each hole sector (* p <.05, ** p <.01)

To summarize, 24-day-old rats were able to achieve a spatial navigation task in total darkness in the presence of olfactory cues. However, the configuration of three visual cues did not allow them to accurately discriminate the training location. The association of both types of cues during 2 days did not increase their ability to use the configuration of three visual cues after removal of the olfactory cues.

24-Day-Old Rats: Additional Conditions

The Beacon Navigation condition clearly showed that 24-day-old rats were able to perceive the visual cue and to move toward it. Rats of this group were even faster then the ones in the three main conditions (mean of 16.8 s to reach the escape hole during the 5th day). A three-way repeated measures ANOVA on the five sessions with condition (Olfactory Cues vs. Beacon Navigation) and sex as variables confirmed that the Beacon Navigation group was faster than the Olfactory Cues group, F(1, 16) = 7.6, p =.014. Both groups learned the task, as shown by the repeated measures effect, F(4, 64) = 2.3, p <.001. The sex variable and all interactions were nonsignificant.

Rats in the Beacon Navigation condition discriminated accurately the training sector during T1 and T2, as confirmed by a two-way repeated measures ANOVA with sex as a variable. There was a significant bias toward the training sector, F(4, 32) = 15.7, p <.001. During T3R, rats correctly discriminated the sector defined by the visual cue situated just behind the goal location, F(4, 32) = 11.4, p <.001. The sex variable and all interactions were nonsignificant.

Rats in the Prolonged V+O Cues then V Cues only condition were trained for 5 days with the two type of cues and 5 more days with only a configuration of three visual cues not directly associated with the goal location. The latency during the first 5 days did not differ from the latency of the Olfactory Cues condition. A three-way repeated measures ANOVA with condition and sex as variables on the first 5 days showed no condition and no sex effect, but a significant Condition × Sex interaction, F(1, 16) = 10.2, p =.006. Indeed, males were a little quicker during the last 3 days than females. The repeated measures were highly significant, F(4, 64) = 24.5, p <.001, whereas all other interactions were nonsignificant. Deletion of the olfactory cues (Day 6) did not induce a significant increase of the latency, as confirmed by a two-way repeated measures ANOVA on Days 5 and 6. The latencies remained stable between Days 6 and 10, as confirmed by a two-way repeated measures ANOVA with sex as a variable. There was no effect due to sex, repeated measures, or interactions.

T1 and T2 (see Figure 4) indicated that rats were accurate in discriminating the correct sector in the presence of both types of cues. A two-way repeated measures ANOVA with sex as a variable on the sectors revealed a significant effect of sectors, F(4, 32) = 11.4, but no sex or interaction effect. T3R dissociated the olfactory defined sector (T−1) and the visual cues-defined sector (T+1). Twenty-four-day-old rats clearly took into account the olfactory cues and denied the visual cues. A two-way repeated measures ANOVA with sex as a variable on the sectors of T3R revealed a significant effect of sectors, F(4, 32) = 12.3, p <.001, with no sex or interaction effect. During T4, T5, and T6R after deletion of the olfactory cues, rats were no longer able to discriminate the correct sector, as confirmed by a two-way ANOVA that showed no sector, sex, or interaction effects.

To summarize, a single visual cue can be used for beacon navigation. Twenty-four-day-old rats perceived the visual cue and were able to move toward it. Olfactory cues support place learning but are unable to promote the use of a configuration visual cues even after prolonged training in the presence of both types of cues.

48-Day-Old Rats

Figure 2 illustrates that, if the latencies of all three groups decreased throughout the training, there was a clear difference in the shape of the three learning curves. Rats trained in the Olfactory Cues condition or in the V+O Cues then V Cues only condition were faster to escape than those trained in the Visual Cues condition. A three-way repeated measures ANOVA on the training sessions with condition and sex as variables confirmed the significant effect of the conditions, F(2, 18) = 8.6, p =.002, of the repeated measures, F(4, 72) = 35.2, p <.001, and of the Condition × Session interaction, F(8, 72) = 2.3, p =.03. The sex variable and all other interactions were nonsignificant. Rats in the Visual Cues condition were significantly slower than rats in the Olfactory Cues condition, F(1, 12) = 10.6, p =.007, or the V+O Cues then V Cues only conditions, F(1, 12) = 13.0, p =.004. The latencies of the rats in the two latter conditions were not different from each other. In contrast, with the 24-day-old rats, the deletion of the olfactory cues in the V+O Cues then V Cues only condition did not induce an increase of the latencies. During Sessions 2 and 3, the latencies continued to decrease significantly, as shown by a two-way repeated measures ANOVA with sex as a variable, F(1, 6) = 62.3, p <.001. The sex variable and the interaction were nonsignificant. After deletion of the olfactory cues, when rats could only rely on the visual cues, they remained faster than the rats with access to only visual cues throughout the training. A three-way repeated measures ANOVA on Sessions 3–5 with sex and condition (Visual Cues vs. V+O Cues then V Cues only) as variables indicated that the condition, F(1, 12) = 4.9, p =.046, and the sessions, F(2, 24) = 4.5, p =.02, variables were significant. The sex variable and all interactions were nonsignificant.

Escape distances confirmed the results obtained from the latencies. Thus, a three-way repeated measures ANOVA with condition and sex as variables showed that the condition variable, F(2, 18) = 8.0, p =.003, and the repeated measures F(1, 18) = 32.7, p <.001, were significant but that the sex variable and all interactions were nonsignificant. Path lengths of the V+O Cues then V Cues only group were significantly shorter than the path lengths of the Visual Cues group, F(1, 12) = 15.6, p =.002, but not different from the path lengths of the Olfactory Cues group.

During T1 and T2, the Visual Cues group did not show any bias toward the correct sector. The sex variable and the Sector × Sex interaction were nonsignificant for all probe trials in that age group. For this reason, we will not specify it anymore. At the end of training, after the fifth session, during T3R, rats in the Visual Cues condition showed a slight bias in favor of the correct sector. A two-way repeated measures ANOVA with sex as a variable indicated a significant effect of the sectors, F(4, 24) = 6.8, p <.001. More precisely, rats spent more time in the correct sector (Trn) than in the sectors T−2, T+1, and T+2 ( Figure 3). Rats in the Olfactory Cues condition were accurate in discriminating the correct area. A two-way repeated measures ANOVA with sex as a variable indicated that rats significantly discriminated the target during T1 and T2, F(4, 24) = 13.6, p <.001. During T3R, rats spent significantly more time in the olfactory defined position. A two-way ANOVA with sex as a variable showed that sector variable had a significant impact, F(4, 24) = 8.8, p <.001. In the presence of visual and olfactory cues, rats were good in discriminating the correct area as shown by T1. A two-way ANOVA on the sectors with sex as a variable indicated that the sectors had a significant impact, F(4, 24) = 50.7, p <.001. After the deletion of the olfactory cues, therefore in the presence of only visual cues, rats were still accurate in discriminating the correct sector. Indeed, the two-way ANOVA with sex as a variable indicated a clear sector bias, F(4, 24) = 21.4, p <.001.

To summarize, 48-day-old rats were accurate in achieving a spatial navigation task and in discriminating a target area on the basis of olfactory cues. Visual cues did not support accurate navigation and place discrimination. However, the association of olfactory and visual cues seemed to promote an accurate use of these visual cues even after deletion of the olfactory cues.

12-Month-Old Rats

All three groups learned to reach the escape hole and showed a decrease in latency. A three-way repeated measures ANOVA with condition and sex as variables indicated that the condition variable was significant, F(2, 24) = 6.0, p =.008, that the sex variable was not, but that the Sex × Condition interaction was significant, F(2, 24) = 5.1, p =.01. The repeated measures were significant, F(4, 96) = 40.9, p <.001, but all interactions with the repeated measures were not. The Sex × Condition interaction was significant because the females were much faster than males in the V+O Cues then V Cues only condition. In the other two conditions, there was no difference between sexes.

The escape distances measured on Days 2 and 5 confirmed the results obtained from the latencies. A three-way repeated measures ANOVA with condition and sex as variables indicated that the condition variable had an effect, F(2, 24) = 5.6, p =.01, that the sex variable was nonsignificant, but the Sex × Condition interaction was significant, F(2, 24) = 4.4, p =.02. The repeated measures were significant, F(1, 24) = 69, p <.001, as well as the interactions Condition × Session, F(2, 24) = 8.5, p =.002, and Sex × Session, F(1, 24) = 5.2, p =.03. The Condition × Sex × Session interaction was nonsignificant. Females took longer paths than males to reach the escape hole in the Visual Cues and Olfactory Cues conditions but took shorter paths than males in the V+O Cues then V Cues only condition. During the second session in the V+O Cues then V Cues only condition, rats took globally shorter paths, possibly because it is easier to plan a route with a larger amount of relevant spatial information. Actually, the sex and condition effects are exclusively due to the differences observed at Session 2. There was no difference at the end of acquisition (Session 5).

During the T1 and T2, the group in the Visual Cues condition discriminated accurately the correct sector. A two-way repeated measures ANOVA with sex as a variable showed a significant effect of sectors, F(4, 32) = 14.8, p <.001, but no effect of sex or the interaction. After the +90° rotation, rats still discriminated the sector defined by the visual cues. A two-way repeated measures ANOVA with sex as a variable revealed a significant effect of sectors, F(4, 32) = 3.5, p =.02, but no effect of sex or the interaction. During the two first probe trials, rats in the Olfactory Cues condition were not able to clearly discriminate the reinforced location. A two-way repeated measures ANOVA with sex as a variable showed no significant effect. However, at the end of acquisition during T3R, rats did discriminate the correct olfactory defined spot. A two-way repeated measures ANOVA with sex as a variable revealed a significant effect of sectors, F(4, 32) = 6.8, p <.001, but no effect of sex or the interaction. The V+O Cues then V Cues only group did not show a significant spatial bias toward the correct location during the first probe trial. A two-way repeated measures ANOVA with sex as a variable showed an effect of sex, F(1, 8) = 5.6, p =.045, but no effect of sectors or the interaction. Females spent more time in the five sectors than males (an average of 55.6 s for females and 44.1 s for males). After removal of the olfactory cues, during T2 and T3R, rats showed a significant bias toward the correct location defined by the visual cues. A two-way repeated measures ANOVA with sex as a variable revealed a significant effect of sectors, F(4, 32) = 10.7, p <.001, but no effect of sex or the interaction.

To summarize, 12-month-old rats were successful in performing the task at the end of training in all conditions. The differences between conditions during the acquisition were no more significant at the end of training. Rats in the Visual Cues condition were able to distinguish the correct location already after 2 days, whereas rats in the Olfactory Cues and in the V+O Cues then V Cues only conditions were able to distinguish the correct location only at the end of training. Rats in the V+O Cues then V Cues only condition did show a correct spatial bias after removal of the olfactory cues.

Discussion

Spatial navigation abilities of rats changed drastically throughout the first 2 months of life, until early adulthood. Moreover, a slow evolution appeared to continue over the 1st year of life (see Table 2). The youngest rats relied mainly on olfaction to find the escape hole, whereas 12-month-old rats were more accurate with visual cues. These differences are not likely to be detected in a rich landscape of complex cues such as ordinary laboratory environments. In controlled conditions, however, it was possible to exactly evaluate the contribution of local or distributed olfactory and visual cues for place navigation at different ages. The sensory preference changed through development, and we observed a cooperation between vision and olfaction to achieve a place navigation task in total darkness in 48-day-old and 3-month-old rats. For the 48-day-old rats, this cooperation was a prerequisite when the target position was related to that of three discrete visual cues. Thus, different sensory systems are involved at different ages in spatial behavior and cognition. Olfaction is used first and appears to enable the use of visual information. This maturation requires more than the ability to detect cues in a given sensory modality. It affects the capacity to integrate specific information for place learning and not for simple guidance. We found different steps for the integration of olfactory and visual information in a spatial representation.

Summary of Results

In our experimental situation, we did not control the contribution of kinesthesia, which must have been important, as the homing task was presented on a solid ground (Cooper, Manka, & Mizumori, 2001; Etienne et al., 1996; Schenk, 1989). Kinesthesia is important to allow the integration of visual information in a spatial representation (Rossier, Grobéty, & Schenk, 2000) and might also contribute to the integration of olfactory information. Indeed, animals do not use only one type of information, but they take advantage of a wide variety of sensory information (Rossier, Haeberli, & Schenk, 2000).

Early Preference for Distributed Olfactory Cues

Twenty-four-day-old rats were particularly efficient in using olfactory information to navigate in the dark. However, they were slow to reach the escape hole and were unable to discriminate the correct location when only three discrete visual cues were available. In addition, the deletion of the olfactory cues in the V+O Cues then V Cues only condition induced a dramatic decrease in performance, confirming that rats of this age could not orient relative to a set of three discrete light cues. Two 24-day-old groups were trained in additional conditions to ensure that rats of that age could perceive the visual cues and had enough time to learn the spatial properties of the configuration of visual and olfactory cues. In the Beacon Navigation task, one cue was directly associated with the escape hole. In this condition, the rats had to detect the cue and to move toward it. They were particularly efficient in achieving this simple task, suggesting that the poor performance with the configuration of visual cues was not due to a problem of perception. Another group of 24-day-old rats were given access to the olfactory and visual cues during 5 days before removal of the distributed olfactory cues (Prolonged V+O Cues then V Cues only condition). These rats were unable to transfer the acquired ability to the visual only condition in spite of prolonged training.

Taken together, these results suggest that a configuration of cues distant from the target location can be processed and used by 24-day-old rats when the cues are olfactory, but not when they are visual. Olfaction is sufficient to define places and to support navigation in total darkness at an early age, when reliance on a configuration of limited visual information seems impossible. The fact that young animals were particularly accurate in distinguishing the olfactory defined sector during probe trials confirms that they can perform better than adults in some specific situations (Spear & Molina, 1987). Olfaction is clearly the sensory modality that allows early navigational abilities.

Like younger rats, 48-day-old rats did not show a clear discrimination of the goal sector with three visual cues. They were faster in reaching the escape hole and did discriminate the training area during probe trials when trained with the configuration of olfactory cues. However, unlike 24-day-old rats, they remained spatially oriented in spite of the removal of the olfactory cues following training in the V+O then V Cues only condition. They were able to transfer their ability to rapidly reach the escape hole and to maintain a correct discrimination of the training area in the visual-only condition. Thus, whereas the 48-day-old rats were not able to learn the position beginning with visual cues only, the transitory presence of olfactory information allowed for an efficient use of the set of visual cues. In summary, rats of this age could rely on discrete visual cues for orientation if allowed to calibrate them relative to olfactory cues during 2 days.

Late Development of Orientation Abilities With the Minimal Visual Configuration

As expected, adult rats were able to achieve the homing task based on unimodal visual or olfactory cues (Cressant et al., 1999; Lavenex & Schenk, 1996, 1997). It is noteworthy that adult rats were able to show a significant discrimination in the difficult condition with only three identical visual cues. This is a minimal cue condition that forces animals to use a spatial navigation strategy (Rossier, Grobéty, & Schenk, 2000). Spatial abilities based on visual information seem to develop throughout early adulthood. In the two conditions in which the rats could rely on both types of cues during 2 days, they were quicker, made more direct paths to reach the correct hole, and spent more time in the correct area. They were therefore more accurate in achieving the task in the presence of both types of cues and remained accurate after removal of either type of cue. Both olfactory and visual cues appeared to potentiate the use of the other modality. Females seemed a little more accurate in discriminating the correct location during the two first probe trials with olfactory cues only.

Twelve-month-old rats were better in achieving the task with visual rather than olfactory cues. The group with visual cues was faster in reaching the escape hole and discriminated the correct location from the first probe trial. The rats with olfactory cues were slower in reaching the target hole during the training trials and expressed little discrimination of the correct location during the two first probe trials. During the third probe trial, however, they showed a significant bias toward the olfactory defined location. Thus, 12-month-old rats were good at performing the task with visual cues, but they needed more time when given only olfactory cues. Like the younger adult rats, they were faster when provided with combined cues. This was particularly evident in females, which seemed better in taking advantage of olfactory information at that age. Deletion of the olfactory cues had no differential effect in males and females. Thus, as they age, rats appear less prone to rely on olfactory information in an orientation task, though they still benefit from their combination with visual cues.

Sensory Specialization or Difficulty Due to Minimal Cue Condition?

Young subjects are good in perceiving olfactory cues (Polan & Hofer, 1998; Wilson & Sullivan, 1994). In our task, they were able to use them for spatial navigation. In contrast, they were not able to use discrete visual cues to achieve the task when these were not directly associated with the target (Salazar, Rossier, & Schenk, 2000).

However, the difficulty in relying on the visual cues appears to contradict the results of Brown and Whishaw (2000). These authors proposed that place and cue navigation develop simultaneously, at a very early age. They suggested that both 19- and 20-day-old rats learned the place and the cued tasks equally well. However, their results indicate that in spite of a significant discrimination of the target during the probe trial, the rats in the place navigation task were much slower in escape than those in the cued task (15–20 s vs. approximately 8 s). Accurate navigation should allow for much lower latencies than were found by Brown and Whishaw (2000). The problem of the place navigation task is that one usually does not know which strategies are used for reaching the target and for expressing place preference. When the environment provides a rich complex of visual cues, it is difficult to know which of these cues are used to achieve the task and how they are processed. In particular, the spatial abilities of very young subjects depend on the experimental situation, and they can rely on visual cues, as shown by Carman and Mactutus (2001). The discrepancies in the literature concerning the ontogeny of spatial navigation (Brown & Whishaw, 2000; Carman & Mactutus, 2001; Rudy et al., 1987; Schenk, 1985) suggest that it is only possible to compare immature rats' performances with that of adults in controlled conditions.

A Maturation in Two Steps?

We consider that the olfactory cues placed on the table were “relational” because none of them was directly associated with the escape hole. The availability of olfactory cues around the holes was controlled by changing the plastic disks before each trial (see the Method section). Consequently, rats aged 24 days were able to learn the relation among the olfactory cues in order to navigate. These cues were distributed over the whole table and might have formed complex gradients more likely to guide young subjects. In comparison, there were only three visual cues, and the rats had to remember the escape position relative to the geometrical configuration of cues.

Thus, the deficit in processing discrete visual information does not imply that immature animals are not able to rely on vision for spatial orientation. It is really related to experimental conditions. The fact that rats were able to process and use relational or dissociated olfactory information suggests that in our visual condition, the difficulty of the visual cue task was due to the effort of processing the particular feature provided by the relative position of three identical cues.

Thus, in contrast with the processing of distributed olfactory cues, orientation based on the three visual cues might require a sketch map constructed from an arrangement of positional cues, as in a topographic map. This processing relies on a phylogenetically more recent development of the hippocampal formation (Jacobs & Schenk, in press). We suggest that the youngest rats relied on their relatively mature bearing map based on the integration of salient distributed cues, such as odor gradients emanating from the scented strips in the present work or rich visual cues such as in Carman and Mactutus (2001). As the sketch map matured, the rats began to rely preferentially on discrete distant visual cues. Our results also suggest that the sketch map is progressively calibrated by the bearing map during the 2nd postnatal month and may not become autonomous until the 3rd month, coinciding with the ontogeny of hippocampal metabolism (Meibach, Ross, Cox, & Glick, 1980). This hypothesis is also in agreement with recent findings from Martin and Berthoz (2002) that the locational firing of hippocampal place cells is less specific and stable in rats under the age of 50 days.

A Supramodal Representation

The importance of the olfactory information for young animals in total darkness confirms the results of Lavenex and Schenk (1996), and the electrophysiological data showing that place cells encode olfactory information in light (Shapiro, Tanila, & Eichenbaum, 1997) and particularly in total darkness (Poucet, Save, & Lenck-Santini, 2000). Thus, olfactory information contributes to the elaboration of spatial representation. In fact, a hierarchy of sensory modalities participates in spatial memory (Cooper et al., 1998). But this hierarchy changes through development. Olfaction may participate in the calibration of the spatial information processing and may therefore enable the integration of visual information. Olfaction not only allows beacon navigation but also supports place or spatial navigation that relies on the processing of configurational information such as our set of olfactory cues. Vision, however, provides accurate information from the changing visual landscape during locomotion and might contribute to the learning of nearby visual configurations.

To conclude, it is clear that spatial cognition does involve several components and relies on different sensory modalities. These modalities do not develop simultaneously, and each modality makes a specific contribution to spatial cognition. Young rats are good at using olfaction but are less efficient in using vision, whereas older rats are good at using vision, but not very accurate in using olfaction. We observed an interesting interaction between sensory modalities, which was reciprocal in adults. Olfaction enables the use of visual information in 48-day-old rats. Animals can rely simultaneously on information from different sensory modalities, which indicates that the spatial representation is supramodal, as suggested by Swanson (1983). It is thus important to study how this hierarchy changes during aging.
