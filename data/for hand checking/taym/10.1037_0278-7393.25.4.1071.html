The Mix-Up Regarding Mixed and Unmixed Lists in Spacing-Effect Research

By: Thomas C. Toppino
Department of Psychology, Villanova University;
Mark A. Schneider
Department of Psychology, Loyola University of Chicago

Acknowledgement:

The spacing effect refers to the finding that items with repetitions that are separated by time or other events are remembered better than items with repetitions that are massed, occurring in immediate succession. Many researchers would consider this effect to be among the best established phenomena in the area of learning and memory (e.g., Dempster, 1988). Unfortunately, theoretical agreement has been harder to achieve.

Although a variety of theoretical mechanisms have been proposed to account for the spacing effect, most accounts have been based on some variation of either a deficient-processing or an encoding-variability mechanism. According to deficient-processing theories (e.g., Challis, 1993; Jacoby, 1978; Rose & Rowe, 1976; Shaughnessy, Zimmerman, & Underwood, 1972), learners do not study fully one presentation of a massed repetition (usually the second), whereas they are more likely to study fully both presentations of a spaced repetition. Proponents of these theories attribute the superiority of spaced repetitions to greater total study time and/or quality. According to encoding-variability theories (e.g., Bower, 1972; Glenberg, 1976, 1979; Madigan, 1969), massed repetitions are likely to be encoded similarly, whereas spaced repetitions are likely to be encoded differently, enabling a greater number of effective retrieval cues. Thus, proponents of these theories attribute the superiority of spaced repetitions to the greater accessibility of differentially encoded information.

The lack of a theoretical consensus does not reflect a lack of empirical effort. The spacing-effect literature is voluminous. Countless variables have been manipulated in the course of investigating the spacing effect in a wide variety of memory tasks (for early, but still relevant, reviews, see Crowder, 1976, and Hintzman, 1974). However, the basic phenomenon has been replicated repeatedly, but the boundaries of the spacing effect have proved to be exceedingly difficult to identify, so no existing theory can account for all of the relevant findings.

Why should the spacing effect be so resistant to explanation? Most likely, the phenomenon is simply much more complex than it appears on the surface, and/or those of us who have worked on the effect are simply too entrenched in our particular theoretical ruts to see an alternative way of organizing and conceptualizing the existing data. A much more worrisome scenario, however, is that the existing data we seek to explain may be contaminated in some way.

The latter possibility was raised by Hall (1992), who concluded that intentional free-recall studies in which spacing is manipulated as a mixed-list (within-list) variable “are virtually useless in addressing theoretical questions concerning spaced versus massed study” (p. 613). The reason, Hall claimed, is that such studies are inevitably confounded by participants' displaced-rehearsal strategies.

Hall (1992) proposed that participants in a mixed-list experiment adopt a differential-rehearsal strategy in which they stop processing a massed repetition and devote the time to studying items presented earlier in the list. He argued that this should benefit spaced repetitions in particular because of the way in which lists are often constructed. That is, in mixed lists containing both massed and spaced repetitions, researchers usually equate the two types of experimental items with respect to the serial positions of their final occurrences in order to hold retention interval relatively constant. As a result, from any given point in the list, there will tend to be more preceding items from the spaced-repetition condition than from the massed-repetition condition. Thus, preceding spaced-repetition items will be more likely than preceding massed-repetition items to benefit from the extra study time afforded to participants when they stop studying a current massed repetition. In short, to the extent that participants use Hall's proposed differential-rehearsal strategy, the spacing effect they produce will be at least partially an artifact of list structure.

The potential severity of the problem this poses for the existing data on spaced repetitions stems from the fact that the vast majority of experiments on the spacing effect have employed a mixed-list procedure. Furthermore, although Hall focused his discussion primarily on intentional free-recall experiments, it seems that displaced-rehearsal strategies could contaminate the results of mixed-list spacing experiments involving other memory tasks as well. Thus, a significant proportion of the spaced-repetition literature could be contaminated. Some apparent spacing effects actually might be displaced-rehearsal effects, and some variables that seem to moderate the spacing effect actually might influence rehearsal strategies. In the present article, we reexamine the empirical basis for Hall's (1992) hypothesis to determine the degree to which there may be cause for concern.

Hall (1992) argued that the confounding inherent in mixed-list designs could be eliminated by using unmixed lists containing only massed repetitions or only spaced repetitions. Any displaced rehearsals would be devoted to earlier items of the same type. He predicted that overall performance on unmixed massed-repetition lists would not differ from that on unmixed spaced-repetition lists.

The preexisting literature was of little help to Hall (1992) in evaluating his prediction. Only three previous articles had investigated the spacing effect in free recall using unmixed lists, and their results were contradictory. Waugh (1970) found no overall spacing effect with unmixed lists, whereas Underwood (1969, 1970) found strong spacing effects. Although Underwood's findings were inconsistent with Hall's displaced-rehearsal hypothesis, Hall questioned the generality of the findings. He noted that Underwood had used slow presentation rates in conjunction with long lists. He suggested that these conditions may have strained participants' ability to maintain attention. They may have opted to rest, rather than study, during massed repetitions, thereby producing a spacing effect that would not be obtained under more usual conditions. Although this account of Underwood's findings was never evaluated empirically, it provided a justification for further investigation of the spacing effect in the context of unmixed lists.

Hall (1992) conducted three experiments in an attempt to test predictions derived from his displaced-rehearsal hypothesis. In his first two experiments, he did not use pure unmixed lists consisting of only massed or only spaced repetitions. Rather, he used mixed lists containing one type of repeated item (either massed or spaced repetitions) and once-presented items. His results indicated no overall difference in recall between massed- and spaced-repetition lists. Spaced-repetition items were recalled better than massed-repetition items, but this difference was offset by superior recall of once-presented items in massed-repetition lists in comparison with spaced-repetition lists. Unfortunately, interpretation of these results was equivocal. Although the superiority of once-presented items in the massed-repetition lists might have been due to displaced rehearsal of once-presented items during the occurrence of massed repetitions, Hall acknowledged that the results could be explained equally well by a list-strength effect (e.g., Ratcliff, Clark, & Shiffrin, 1990) in which stronger items inhibit retrieval of weaker items. That is, recall of once-presented items may have been inhibited more by stronger spaced-repetition items than by weaker massed-repetition items.

In his Experiment 3, Hall (1992) compared free recall of lists in which all items were massed repetitions with that of lists in which all items were spaced repetitions. By using true unmixed lists that eliminated the possibility of a list-strength effect, Hall hoped to provide a critical test of his displaced-rehearsal hypothesis. He predicted that a different pattern of results would be obtained on the first and second half of these lists. In the first half, massed repetitions should be recalled better than spaced repetitions because only the former would benefit from displaced rehearsals during the presentation of later massed repetitions. However, in the second half, massed repetitions should be recalled more poorly than spaced repetitions because participants would have devoted less study time to the massed repetitions presented late in the list. Hall expected the results in each half of the list to balance one another so that no overall spacing effect would be obtained. His results were in accordance with his predictions. Massed repetitions were recalled better than spaced repetitions in the first half of the lists, whereas spaced repetitions were recalled better in the second half. Consequently, there was no overall spacing effect.

Although the results of Hall's (1992) Experiment 3 seem to support his differential-rehearsal hypothesis, there are reasons to question the results and their implications. One concern is that Hall's conclusions do not follow directly from his experimental design. It is critical for Hall's interpretation of his data that performance on the first half of his lists be caused by the type of item in the second half of the lists. Thus, Hall attributed better recall of massed repetitions than spaced repetitions in the first half of a list to the fact that only the former are followed by massed repetitions. Unfortunately, such a conclusion is complicated by the fact that the relevant comparison is inherently confounded. Massed items and spaced items in the first half of unmixed lists differ not only in the type of item they precede, but also in the type of item that they themselves are.

Further concerns stem from the fact that Hall (1992) did not include primacy and recency buffers in his lists. It is standard procedure in the spacing-effect literature to minimize serial-position effects by excluding data for the first and last several items from analysis. Researchers cannot tell the degree to which Hall's findings may be unduly influenced by performance on early and/or late occurring items. If displaced rehearsal is taking place as Hall hypothesized, its effect may be limited to items that normally would occur in the primacy buffer, leaving data from the rest of the list relatively unaffected. Also, it is possible that serial position interacts with the type of repeated item in a way that is particularly beneficial to the recall of massed-repetition items. For example, if the first several items are massed repetitions, they may be especially distinctive and/or may receive more processing than typical massed repetitions.

We designed the present experiment to examine more closely the bases for Hall's (1992) results. We replicated Hall's use of pure unmixed lists consisting of only massed repetitions and only spaced repetitions, respectively. However, we also included lists containing both kinds of items such that one type of item was presented in the first half of the list (either massed or spaced) and the other type of item was presented in the second half of the list (either spaced or massed). Together, our lists yielded a factorial design in which the type of item in the first half of the list and the type of item in the second half were manipulated independently. Thus, it was possible to determine whether performance on items in the first half of a list was primarily the result of the type of item in that half or of the type of item in the second half of the list. In addition, we analyzed data separately for each quarter of the list. Thus, for example, it was possible to determine whether effects in the first half of the list were primarily attributable to items in the first quarter (comparable to the usual primacy buffer) or whether the effects extended over the entire half of the list.

Method

Participants and Design

Participants were 32 introductory psychology students at Villanova University. We presented each participant with all four conditions produced by crossing type of repetition (massed vs. spaced) with half of the list (first vs. second half). Thus, the experiment used a 2 × 2 within-subjects factorial design. In the case of massed repetitions, the second occurrence was presented immediately after its first occurrence. The occurrences of spaced repetitions were separated by spacings of three, four, five, or six intervening items.

Materials

We selected a pool of 96 common nouns from the norms of Paivio, Yuille, and Madigan (1968). All nouns contained five to eight letters and had a high imagery value (at least 5.6 on a 7-point scale), a high concreteness value (at least 5.4 on a 7-point scale), and a high Thorndike–Lorge frequency (50 or more occurrences per 1 million words).

We then divided the pool into four sets of 24 items. We assigned items from the pool randomly to each set with the restriction that words having obvious semantic associations could not belong to the same set. The 24-item sets were each used to create lists in which each word appeared twice for a total of 48 presentations.

We actually used each set of 24 items to create four lists in each of the four experimental conditions. Across the resulting 16 lists for each experimental condition, each of the 96 words occurred equally often in each quadrant of the list. We assigned lists to participants such that, for a given participant, the list serving in each experimental condition involved a different set of 24 words and that, across participants, each of the 16 lists for each condition was presented equally often. In addition, the order in which the four experimental conditions was presented was counterbalanced across participants. As a consequence of the counterbalancing, each word occurred equally often in each list quadrant for all experimental conditions. This served to avoid confounds between items (including item difficulty), the type of repetition (massed vs. spaced), and the quadrant of the list.

Procedure

Participants were instructed that they were participating in a free-recall test of memory. Following Hall (1992), we presented words by a microcomputer at the presentation rate of one item every 2.5 s. The word appeared on the screen for 2.0 s, and then the screen became blank for 0.5 s until the next item was presented. We showed each participant four lists, one representing each of the four experimental conditions. After each study list, a set of six digits was displayed for 3 s. We instructed participants to memorize the digits and write them down on answer sheets. Then, we gave them a 2-min recall period to write, in any order, as many words as they were able to remember from the immediately preceding list.

Results and Discussion

We submitted the results to a series of analyses of variance (ANOVAs) with the alpha level set at .05 for all tests. We actually conducted analyses on an arcsine transformation of the proportions of items recalled, which is often suggested for percentage and proportion data in order to improve the fit with assumptions underlying the ANOVA (e.g., Guilford, 1965; Rosenthal & Rosnow, 1984). The means, however, are reported as percentages to allow convenient comparisons.

When our pure lists (all-massed vs. all-spaced items) are considered alone, our experiment constitutes a replication of Hall's (1992) Experiment 3. Our first analysis compared the recall performance of the all-massed and all-spaced lists in order to determine whether our results were similar to those obtained in Hall's third experiment. A 2 × 4 ANOVA (Type of List × List Quadrant) with repeated measures on both factors revealed significant main effects of spacing, F(1, 31) = 6.75, MSE = 0.08, and quadrant, F(3, 93) = 7.51, MSE = 0.09. More important, however, there was a significant Type of List × List Quadrant interaction, F(3, 93) = 3.02, MSE = 0.10, reflecting the fact that massed repetitions were recalled better in Quadrant 1, whereas the opposite was true in Quadrants 2, 3, and 4. This interaction constitutes a replication of the critical interaction reported in Hall's (1992) Experiment 3. However, with our more sensitive design in which effects can be analyzed by list quadrant rather than merely by list half, it is clear that recall is better for massed- as opposed to spaced-repetition items only in Quadrant 1.

When we temporarily put aside the question of what factor accounts for the improved recall of early massed-repetition items in our experiment and, presumably, in Hall's (1992) study, our findings suggest that this unidentified factor is unlikely to have influenced the outcome of many spaced-repetition experiments. The benefit for massed-repetition items is limited to a region of the list that, in most experiments, is occupied primarily by primacy buffers that routinely are excluded from analysis. When we excluded data from the first and last quadrants of the list (i.e., the first and last six items, respectively) as we normally would do with primacy- and recency-buffer items, a 2 × 2 repeated-measures ANOVA (Type of List × List Half, involving only Quadrants 2 and 3) revealed a main effect of spacing such that spaced repetitions were recalled better than massed repetitions, F(1, 31) = 4.65, MSE = 0.09. Quadrant 3 items were recalled better than Quadrant 2 items, but the effect did not reach conventional levels of significance, F(1, 31) = 3.96, MSE = 0.11. In addition, there was no hint of an interaction, F < 1.00, MSE = 0.10. Thus, when we followed the standard practice of excluding primacy and recency buffers from analysis, we obtained a clear spacing effect that was unqualified by the recall advantage enjoyed by early-occurring massed repetitions.

In contrast with our findings, Hall (1992) predicted and obtained a null effect of spacing using unmixed lists. He predicted that, if learners were using his hypothesized displaced-rehearsal strategy, they would spend less time processing a current massed-repetition item but would spend that time rehearsing previously presented items of the same type so that overall recall of massed repetitions would not suffer. The fact that our results reveal a strong, reliable overall spacing effect with unmixed lists clearly indicates that Hall's null effect lacks generality and raises serious questions about the viability of his displaced-rehearsal hypothesis.

A remaining question, however, is why Hall (1992, Experiment 3) obtained no overall spacing effect whereas we did. It is not clear that this question can be answered definitively. Part of the explanation may lie in the fact that our lists were several items longer than Hall's lists. Items falling in the primacy region of a shorter list would constitute a greater proportion of the entire list and of the first half in particular. Thus, in one of Hall's lists, the benefits accruing to massed repetitions in the primacy region would carry relatively greater weight in balancing the poorer recall of massed repetitions in other parts of the list. However, the different results obtained in the two experiments also seem attributable to the fact that spaced-repetition superiority in the second half of the lists was greater in our study than in Hall's. Why this occurred is unclear but is most likely attributable to one or more seemingly minor differences in methodology. For example, the lags separating occurrences of spaced-repetition items ranged from two to six intervening items in our experiment, whereas Hall's lags ranged from two to only four intervening items. Although this difference seems unlikely to have had much effect, it is at least possible that our procedure constituted a stronger manipulation of the spacing variable, thereby contributing to the greater spaced-repetition advantage in our experiment.

To adequately address the issue of what underlies the improved recall of early-occurring massed repetitions, we had to combine the data from our pure unmixed lists with data from our mixed lists in which each half of a list consisted of a different type of item (massed or spaced repetitions). A 2 × 2 × 2 ANOVA with repeated measures on all factors examined the recall of items in the first half of the list as a function of the type of item in the first half of the list (massed vs. spaced), the type of item in the second half of the list (massed vs. spaced), and the quadrant of the list (first vs. second). The relevant means are presented in Table 2.

Although there was a weak tendency for first-half items to be recalled slightly better when they were located before second-half massed repetitions rather than before second-half spaced repetitions, the effect did not approach significance, F < 1.00, MSE = 0.12. Furthermore, there were no significant interactive effects involving second-half item type, all F s(1, 31) < 1.00. We obtained a significant main effect of quadrant, indicating a primacy effect such that items in Quadrant 1 were recalled better than items in Quadrant 2, F(1, 31) = 26.03, MSE = 0.12. Although the main effect of type of first-half item was not reliable—F (1, 31) = 1.19, MSE = 0.10—there was a significant two-way interaction involving first-half item type and quadrant, F(1, 31) = 5.48, MSE = 0.07. A probe of simple main effects revealed that spaced repetitions were recalled significantly better than massed repetitions in Quadrant 2 of the list, F(1, 62) = 4.54, MSE = 0.05. However, a larger primacy effect for massed- than for spaced-repetition items eliminated the advantage of spaced repetitions in Quadrant 1 and actually produced a reverse spacing effect, although the difference was not reliable, F < 1.00, MSE = 0.06.

The above findings do not support Hall's (1992) displaced-rehearsal hypothesis. The effect of second-half item type on the recall of first-half items was small and unreliable. We computed the partial omega squared for the effect and found it to be small and negative (ω
  = −.0005), suggesting that we would have been unlikely to have obtained a significant effect even with greater power. The apparent advantage of massed-repetition items in the first half of the list was limited to Quadrant 1 and was attributable to an interaction between quadrant and type of first-half item. Massed repetitions apparently benefited more from being in the primacy region of the list than did spaced repetitions. Why this occurred is unclear. Possibly, massed repetitions enjoy enhanced distinctiveness when they are the first items presented, or perhaps they benefit differentially from participants' rehearsal patterns. The important point here is that our results clearly indicate that their advantage is related primarily to their own item type and not to the type of item that followed them.

To complete the account of our data, we performed another 2 × 2 × 2 ANOVA with repeated measures on all factors that examined the recall of second-half items as a function of type of first-half item (massed vs. spaced), type of second-half item (massed vs. spaced), and quadrant (third vs. fourth). The relevant means are presented in Table 3. The main effect of quadrant was significant, indicating a recency effect such that items in Quadrant 4 were recalled better than items in Quadrant 3, F = 5.83, MSE = 0.06. In addition, there was a reliable main effect of second-half item type, with spaced repetitions being recalled better than massed repetitions, F = 12.42, MSE = 0.12, but neither the type of first-half item nor any interactive effects approached significance, F s(1, 31) < 1.00. In short, recall of second-half items revealed a spacing effect that was unqualified by any other factor.

Two of the conditions involved in our last two analyses were mixed lists in which half of the items were spaced repetitions and half were massed. This raises the question of whether a list-strength effect in which stronger items are recalled at the expense of weaker items could have influenced our results (Ratcliff et al., 1990; Rose & Sutton, 1996). This prospect seems unlikely. Spaced repetitions were clearly “stronger” in the second half of lists in the sense that they were recalled better than second-half massed repetitions. However, type of second-half item did not significantly affect recall of items from the first half of the list. Another way to address the issue is to compare performance on the pure lists (massed–massed and spaced–spaced) with performance on the same type of items from mixed lists (e.g., recall of massed items from the first half of massed–spaced lists averaged together with recall of massed items from the second half of spaced–massed lists). To the extent that there is a list-strength effect at work, items from the pure massed list should be recalled better than massed items in the mixed lists, and items from the pure spaced list should be recalled worse than spaced items from the mixed lists. Our data, however, show that recall from pure and mixed lists was virtually identical. The overall percentage of massed items recalled from the pure list was 50.5%, whereas the overall percentage of massed items recalled from the mixed lists was 49.8%. The comparable numbers for spaced repetitions were 57.2% for the pure list and 58.2% for the mixed lists. Clearly, any list-strength effects were small and relatively inconsequential.

Conclusions

We found no evidence for the displaced-rehearsal strategy that was hypothesized by Hall (1992) to contaminate mixed-list experiments on the spacing effect. We obtained an overall spacing effect with unmixed lists, a finding that is inconsistent with the hypothesis that learners generally use the displaced-rehearsal strategy proposed by Hall (1992). Furthermore, we obtained evidence indicating that the findings he interpreted to support the displaced-rehearsal strategy were mainly attributable to a primacy effect that differentially influences massed and spaced repetitions. One practical implication of our results is that primacy and recency buffers should be used in spaced-repetition studies regardless of whether mixed or unmixed lists are used. Beyond that, our data provide some degree of reassurance about the state of the existing data related to the spacing effect. These findings obviously should not be interpreted to mean that Hall's hypothesized displaced-rehearsal strategy is never used or that we can be completely confident that the existing spaced-repetition data are uncontaminated. Nevertheless, our findings suggest that what appeared to be solid evidence for Hall's hypothesized displaced-rehearsal strategy was primarily the artifactual result of a confounded experiment. Without evidence for a contaminating factor, there is little reason to question the validity of the data obtained from mixed-list experiments, and it seems appropriate for researchers to continue to use the efficient mixed-list methodology. There is, however, a warning implicit in Hall's (1992) article that researchers would be well-advised to heed. By relying almost exclusively on the mixed-list methodology, we run the risk of mistaking effects that are specific to mixed-list conditions for effects that are generally true of spacing repetitions. It would be wise to include unmixed-list conditions more often in our experiments on the spacing effect.
