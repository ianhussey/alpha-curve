Clinical experience suggests that ingestion of a small amount of a formerly abused drug greatly increases the likelihood of relapse in currently abstinent drug or alcohol abusers. For this reason, self-help groups such as Alcoholics Anonymous and Narcotics Anonymous advocate drug abstinence as part of their treatment process; in many substance abuse treatment programs abstinence is their primary goal in order to decrease the probability of relapse. In fact, Hall and Havessy (1986) demonstrated that tobacco, alcohol, and heroin abusers whose goal was total abstention from drug use during a 12-week treatment period were less likely to relapse than patients whose goal was partial abstinence during the 12 weeks (37% vs. 70% relapse).

The relapse phenomenon has been examined using laboratory models of drug taking in animals (reinstatement studies) and in humans (priming or preload studies). In these studies, drug-free subjects are presented with a drug that previously served as a reinforcer (i.e., was previously self-administered). Then, typically, investigators measure subsequent drug-taking behavior and, in humans, self-reported desire for more drug. In animals, these studies have demonstrated that experimenter-delivered administration of a drug increases the likelihood of reinitiating drug-taking behavior after a drug-free period (e.g., de Wit & Stewart, 1981, 1983; Stretch & Gerber, 1973). In humans, priming studies have produced inconsistent results. Some studies have demonstrated that, in alcoholics, ingesting alcohol does not increase ethanol (ETOH) consumption (Kaplan, Meyer, & Stroebel, 1983; Marlatt, Demming, & Reid, 1973; Mello & Mendelson, 1972). Others studies have demonstrated that alcohol increases measures of alcohol consumption and self-reported desire for alcohol (Bigelow, Griffiths, & Liebson, 1977; Hodgson, Rankin, & Stockwell, 1979; Laberg & Ellertsen, 1987; Stockwell, Hodgson, Rankin, & Taylor, 1982). For example, Bigelow et al. (1977) required alcoholic subjects to perform a task (i.e., spend time on an exercise bicycle) or undergo a period of social isolation in order to obtain an alcoholic beverage. The subjects expended more effort or endured more social isolation to obtain ETOH after they had ingested preload beverages of ETOH. Hodgson et al. (1979) and Stockwell et al. (1982) examined the speed of consuming an alcoholic beverage and self-reported “desire for a drink” in moderately and severely dependent alcoholics who had consumed ETOH preload beverages. They found that ETOH preloads increased self-reported desire for, and rate of consumption of an alcoholic drink in severely, but not moderately, dependent subjects.

A more recent study also demonstrated a priming effect with normal social drinkers (de Wit & Chutuape, 1993). In a double-blind study, nonproblem drinkers (4–18 alcoholic drinks per week) were more likely to choose a previously sampled ETOH-containing beverage over a placebo beverage after they consumed either 0.25 or 0.5 g/kg ETOH preloads. Although this result suggested dose dependent effects of ETOH preloads on ETOH preference, the strength of these effects was difficult to determine using the all-or-none measure of choice of the ETOH-containing beverage over the placebo beverage.

In this study we used a procedure that provides a quantitative measure of the strength of subjects' preference for ETOH. Concurrent random-ratio (RR) schedules were used to quantify preference for an ETOH beverage, relative to money. Concurrent schedules have been used in previous studies to compare the ability of a reinforcer to maintain responding relative to an alternative reinforcer (e.g., Bickel, Hughes, DeGrandpre, Higgins, & Rizzuto, 1992; Carroll & Rodefer, 1993; Herrnstein & Loveland, 1975; Lappalainen & Epstein, 1990; Vuchinich & Tucker, 1983; Zacny, Divane, & de Wit, 1992). It is assumed that the distribution of responding between concurrent schedules is a product of factors inherent to each reinforcer and environmental factors, such as the relative probability of earning the reinforcer (Catania, 1963; Rachlin, 1971). In our study, the probability of earning ETOH remained constant and the probability of earning the alternative reinforcer was varied. Subjects performed on the concurrent schedules after preloads of ETOH (0.25 and 0.5 g/kg) or placebo. By recording responding on a range of concurrent RR schedules, we expected to get a sensitive measure of relative preference for ETOH. The primary dependent measure was the number of responses on the ETOH schedule following ETOH preloads. We hypothesized that subjects would respond more on the ETOH schedule following the ETOH preloads than following the placebo preload and that this increased responding would be dose dependent. We also hypothesized that subjects would report parallel increases in their ratings of desire for ETOH.

Method

Subjects

Seven men and 4 women, 21–35 years old (M = 24.5, SD = 4.6), participated in the study. Subjects were recruited from a university community by advertisements in the student newspaper, posters on campus, and word-of-mouth referrals. During screening, candidates completed a questionnaire concerning their recreational drug use (current and lifetime) as well as a standard psychiatric questionnaire, the Symptom Checklist-90–R (Derogatis, 1983). They were interviewed by a psychiatric social worker and examined by a physician. Subjects were not accepted into the study if they had any current medical problems or any major psychiatric disorders defined by Axis I criteria from the revised third edition of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (American Psychiatric Association, 1987). In addition, subjects had to meet the criterion of drinking at least four alcoholic drinks on one occasion (e.g., in one evening) at least twice a month. Candidates with any history of drug abuse or dependence, including alcohol abuse or dependence, were not accepted.

Subjects consumed an average of 9.3 alcoholic drinks per week (SD = 6.4, range = 3–24) and an average maximum of 4.1 drinks per occasion (SD = 1.3, range = 3–6). No subject reported current use of hallucinogens, opiates, stimulants, tranquilizers or sedatives. Four subjects reported using marijuana within the past month, and 1 subject had never tried marijuana. Past recreational drug use included 8 subjects who had used hallucinogens, 6 who had used stimulants, 3 who had used sedatives or tranquilizers, and 3 who had used opiates at least once in their lifetime.

Before participating in the study, subjects gave written informed consent. The consent form stated that they might receive a stimulant, a sedative, an antihistamine, alcohol, or a placebo and informed them of the possible side effects of these drugs. The procedures for this study were approved by the Institutional Review Board at the University of Chicago hospitals. Subjects were paid after completing the experiment.

Procedure

The study consisted of five sessions, conducted one or two evenings per week, from 5:00 p.m. to 9:00 p.m. Sessions were conducted in a comfortable laboratory-based recreational environment. When experimental measures were not being obtained, subjects were encouraged to engage in leisure activities of their choice (TV, movies, radios, magazines, and games were available). They were not allowed to work or study because these activities were considered incompatible with consumption of recreational drugs. Subjects were tested alone (n = 5) or in pairs (n = 6).

Session 1 was the orientation and sampling session. Orientation began at 5:00 p.m.: Subjects read the instructions for the study, gave informed consent, and practiced the computer task (described shortly). As an incentive for learning the task, subjects were allowed to exchange all of the points earned on the computer task for money after completing the task. At 7:00 p.m., breath alcohol levels (BALs) were obtained to verify that subjects were ETOH-free. They then completed a self-report mood questionnaire (Profile of Mood States [POMS]; McNair, Lorr, & Droppelman, 1971). Subjects sampled an ETOH-containing beverage (0.8 g/kg; described later) in four equal portions (0.2 g/kg each). The portions were administered at 7:00, 7:15, 7:30, and 7:45 p.m., and each portion was consumed within 5 min. Subjects were informed that this beverage would be referred to as Substance A in subsequent sessions. At 8:00 p.m., the expected peak time of effects, BALs were measured and subjects completed the POMS. They also completed a drug-liking visual analog scale (VAS) at this time. At 9:00 p.m., subjects were allowed to leave the laboratory.

Sessions 2–5 were preload sessions. At the beginning of these sessions, BALs were determined and subjects completed the POMS. They also rated their desire for Substance A using a desire-for-drug VAS. Following these measures, subjects consumed a preload consisting of a beverage–capsule combination. On Session 2, the preload always contained placebo. This session served as a practice session, and data from this session were not analyzed. In Sessions 3–5, the preload beverages contained placebo, 0.25 g/kg, or 0.5 g/kg ETOH, presented in counterbalanced order. Capsules always contained placebo. Subjects were instructed to consume the preload beverage over a 20-min period. They rated their desire for Substance A again 30 and 60 min after beginning to consume the beverage. At 60 min, subjects also identified the type of drug believed to be contained in the preload beverage–capsule combination and completed the POMS. At 65 min after finishing the preload beverage, subjects began the computer task. After completing the task, subjects exchanged the points earned for Substance A, money, or both. They were require to consume any Substance A earned and remain in the laboratory until 9:00 p.m. At the end of each session, subjects were taken home.

A debriefing interview was conducted on completion of the experiment. During this interview, subjects were informed of the purpose of the experiment and paid for their participation.

Computer task

This task was a modified version of the Applepicker Task (Lappalainen & Epstein, 1990; Norman & Jongerius, 1985). Subjects were presented with two concurrent RR schedules, which were associated with two computer screens. One screen was designated the money screen and points earned on it were exchangeable for money. The other screen was designated the Substance A screen, and points earned on it were exchangeable for Substance A. Each screen consisted of a 10 × 10 array of symbols (Xs or Os). Subjects responded by clicking the mouse while the cursor was postitioned over a symbol. The symbol would either remain on the screen, indicating that no point was earned by that response, or the symbol would briefly be replaced by the letter A and the computer would emit a beep indicating that 1 point had been earned. Subjects could switch between screens at any time by pressing the escape key.

During each session, subjects completed 10 trials. Between each trial, a message on the computer screen informed subjects that the trial was complete and that they could press any key to continue on to the next trial. Thus, the time between trials was subject controlled. Each trial consisted of a practice period, during which subjects allocated 40 responses to each schedule in order to determine the probabilities of the two reinforcers, and a test period, during which subjects could allocate 50 responses to the Substance A, money, or both schedules. Subjects were not informed of the actual RR schedules in effect. Across the 10 trials, the probability of earning points for money systematically increased (.08, .16, .32, .48, and .64) and then decreased (.64, .48, .32, .16, and .08). The probability of earning points for ETOH remained constant at .32.

The number of points earned on each screen (within each trial and across all trials) and the number of responses remaining within the trial were continuously displayed below the screens. Points earned on each of the concurrent RR schedules could be exchanged only in increments of 24: Every 24 points earned on the Substance A schedule yielded 0.2 g/kg ETOH, and every 24 points earned on the money schedule yielded 60¢. If, for example, a subject earned between 24 and 47 points on the money schedule, he or she would receive 60¢ after completing the task, but 48 points would be exchangeable for $1.20. The number of responses for ETOH on each of the 10 successive trials was recorded.

Beverage preparation

The dose of ETOH on the sampling session (Substance A) was 0.8 g/kg. The beverage consisted of a 16% ETOH solution by volume, with tonic and lime mix. The placebo preload contained tonic and lime mix with 1% ETOH added to serve as a taste mask. The ETOH preloads were prepared using 5% (0.25-g/kg dose) or 10% (0.5-g/kg dose) ETOH solution with tonic and lime mix. All beverages were prepared in a volume of 450 ml for a 70-kg person with volumetric adjustments for body weight. The beverages were served cold in styrofoam cups with lids and consumed through straws.

Dependent Measures

Preference

The primary dependent measure was the number of responses subjects made on the Substance A schedule across each of the 10 successive trials. The number of ETOH responses at each probability (sum of the two trials at each probability) was compared across the three preload conditions. The total amounts of Substance A and money earned were also recorded and analyzed.

Mood and subjective drug effects

A number of measures were taken throughout the sampling and preload sessions to assess subjects' mood and the subjective effects of the substances ingested. Mood was measured using an experimental version of the POMS (Johanson & Uhlenhuth, 1980), consisting of 72 adjectives commonly used to describe momentary mood states. Subjects indicated how they felt at the moment in relation to each of the 72 adjectives on a 5-point scale ranging from not at all (0) to extremely (4). Eight scales have been empirically derived using factor analysis (Anxiety, Depression, Anger, Vigor, Fatigue, Confusion, Friendliness, and Elation). Two additional subscales have been derived on an intuitive basis from the other scales: Arousal = (Anxiety + Vigor) − (Fatigue + Confusion), and Positive Mood = Elation − Depression.

A desire-for-drug VAS was completed by subjects during preload sessions (Sessions 2–5). This questionnaire pertained to the effects of preloads by asking subjects whether they felt any drug effects (“feel preload drug”), whether they liked the effects they were experiencing (“like preload effects”), and whether they were high (“feel high”). Subjects also rated their current desire for ETOH (“desire for Substance A”). Subjects placed a vertical mark through a 100-mm line that corresponded to how they were currently feeling on these measures. The left side of each line was labeled none at all and the right side was labeled very much.

A drug identification form was also completed during preload sessions. Subjects indicated what type of drug they believed was contained in the preload: antihistamine, stimulant, tranquilizer, alcohol, or placebo.

Data Analysis

Only data from Sessions 3–5 were analyzed. Session 2 was considered to be a practice session. Responses for ETOH at each probability of earning money (.08, .16, .32, .48, and .64) were summed across the ascending and descending series. Responding for ETOH was analyzed using two-way, within-subjects analysis of variance (ANOVA). The two repeated variables were preload condition (placebo, 0.25, and 0.5 g/kg ETOH) and probability of earning money. Subjective effects of preloads were analyzed using two-way ANOVAs with repeated measures on preload condition and hour. Changes on each of the 10 POMS scales were evaluated prior to and 60 min after receiving the preload. Changes on “feel preload drug” effects, “like preload effects,” and “feel high” were evaluated prior to, 30 min, and 60 min after receiving the preload. Changes on “desire for Substance A” were analyzed as change scores. Ratings made at the beginning of the session (before subjects ingested the preload) were subtracted from ratings of desire for Substance A recorded 30 and 60 min after ingesting the preload. Thus, positive change scores reflected an increase in desire for Substance A following preload, whereas negative values reflected a decrease in subjects' desire for Substance A. Tukey post hoc analyses were conducted on measures with significant values on F tests.

Results

Ethanol Responding

The number of responses on the Substance A (ETOH) schedule varied as a function of the RR schedule in effect (i.e., subjects responded more on the Substance A schedule when the probability of earning money was low), F(4, 40) = 20.0, p < .0001. In addition, at the lower probabilities of earning money, the number of responses on the Substance A schedule was significantly higher after both ETOH preload doses compared with placebo: Preload Condition × Probability, F(8, 80) = 2.8, p < .02 (see Figure 1). Tukey's post hoc tests revealed that at the .08 probability of earning money, responses on the Substance A schedule were higher than placebo after both the 0.25-g/kg and the 0.5-g/kg ETOH preloads and that at the .16 probability, mean responses on the Substance A schedule were higher than placebo following the 0.5-g/kg ETOH preload. Neither the total number of responses across probabilities nor the total amount of ETOH earned differed significantly across the preload conditions: responses for ETOH by preload condition, F(2, 20) = 1.5, ns; amount of ETOH by preload condition, F(2, 20) < 1, ns.

Responses on the ethanol schedule were measured following preloads containing 0, 0.25, and 0.5 g/kg ethanol. Subjective Effects of Preloads

There was a statistically significant increase in the ratings of “desire for Substance A” following both ETOH preload conditions (0.25 and 0.5 g/kg), compared with the placebo preload condition: Preload Condition × Hour, F(4, 40) = 6.6, p < .0003 (see Figure 2). This increase in desire was observed 30 min following the 0.25-g/kg ETOH preload and 30 and 60 min following the 0.5-g/kg ETOH preload.

Figure 2. Mean ratings of “desire for Substance A” following preloads containing 0, 0.25, and 0.5 g/kg ethanol. Scores are presented as a change from before the preload was administered (pre) to 30 and 60 min after preload. Tukey's post hoc tests revealed significant differences between means: *p < .05, **p < .01, compared with the 0-g/kg ethanol preload; +p < .05, ++p < .01, compared with the 0.25-g/kg ethanol preload

There were also significant main effects of preload condition, F(2, 20) = 9.7, p < .0027, and hour, F(2, 20) = 6.5, p < .007. Scores on “feel preload drug” showed a significant Preload Condition × Hour interaction: Relative to before the preload was administered, scores increased 30 and 60 min following both ETOH preloads, F(4, 40) = 11.6, p < .0001 (see Figure 3). There were also significant main effects of both preload condition. F(2, 20) = 18.9, p < .0001, and hour, F(2, 20) = 26.3, p < .0001. Scores on “like preload effects” showed a significant Preload Condition × Hour interaction. Relative to before the preload was administered, scores increased 30 and 60 min following the 0.5-g/kg ETOH preload only, F(4, 40) = 8.2, p < .0001. There were also significant main effects for both preload condition, F(2, 20) = 11.8, p < .0004, and hour, F(2, 20) = 6.8, p < .02. Similarly, scores on “feel high” showed a significant Preload Condition × Hour interaction. Relative to before the preload was administered, scores increased 30 and 60 min following the 0.5-g/kg ETOH preload only, F(4, 40) = 9.5, p < .0001. In addition, there were significant main effects of preload condition, F(2, 20) = 14.6, p < .0001, and hour, F(2, 20) = 13.0, p < .002. There were no significant changes on any of the POMS scales following any of the preloads.

Preload Identification

Results from preload identification, assessed 1 hr after preload, are presented in Table 1. Overall, half of the subjects correctly identified the placebo preload as placebo. Approximately equal numbers of subjects identified the 0.25-g/kg ETOH preload as either ETOH, placebo, or a sedative or tranquilizer, whereas most subjects correctly identified the 0.5-g/kg ETOH preload as ETOH.

 

Discussion

The main findings from this study support our hypothesis that in normal social drinkers, ETOH preloads increase responding for ETOH, compared with a placebo preload. Furthermore, the magnitude of this effect varied as a function of the ETOH preload dose and the availability of the alternative reinforcer, money; 0.25 g/kg ETOH increased responding when the probability of earning money was .08, and 0.5 g/kg ETOH increased responding when the probabilities of earning money were .08 and .16. Subjective reports of desire for ETOH were consistent with the behavioral results: Desire for ETOH increased in a dose-dependent manner following both ETOH preloads compared with placebo preload.

These results are consistent with those reported by de Wit and Chutuape (1993). In that experiment, preference for ETOH in normal social drinkers was measured by an all-or-none choice between ETOH and money. By contrast, the current design allowed subjects to choose repeatedly between ETOH and money across a range of different probabilities (trials) of monetary payoff. Thus, this procedure provided a graded or quantitative measure of preference for ETOH versus an alternative nondrug reinforcer and allowed us to demonstrate that the strength of ETOH priming is dose dependent. These results are also consistent with findings that ETOH preloads increase responding for ETOH in alcoholics (e.g., Bigelow et al., 1977). In a more general sense, our findings are consistent with the observation that a brief presentation of a positive reinforcer increases responding for the reinforcer. For example, various stimuli such as food (e.g., Cornell, Rodin, & Weingarten, 1989; Eiserer, 1978; Pavlov, 1957), water (Van der Kooy & Hogan, 1978), and brain stimulation (Reid, Hunsicker, Kent, Lindsay, & Gallistel, 1973) have also been shown to prime specific, goal-directed responding.

These results also demonstrate a robust effect of a concurrently available nondrug reinforcer (money) on responding for ETOH. Regardless of the preload condition, responding for ETOH decreased as the probability of monetary reinforcement increased. This result is consistent with numerous studies that demonstrate similar effects of alternative nondrug reinforcers on drug choice in animals (e.g., Carroll, Carmona, & May, 1991; Carroll & Rodefer, 1993; Nader & Woolverton, 1991) and humans (e.g., de Wit & Chutuape, 1993; Griffiths, Troisi, Silverman & Mumford, 1993; Zacny et al., 1992). Moreover, such principles have been applied to clinical settings to successfully decrease the amount of drug taking in drug treatment programs (e.g., Higgins et al., 1991).

In addition to the main effect of the probability of earning money on responding for ETOH, there was an interaction between the probability of earning money and the preload dose of ETOH (see Figure 1). Both ETOH preloads increased responding on the ETOH schedule when the probability of earning money was low. Moreover, the 0.5-g/kg ETOH preload increased responding more under low monetary reinforcement conditions than did the 0.25-g/kg ETOH preload. Thus, we would predict that, when an alternative reinforcer is available, preloads will result in increased drug consumption only when the availability of the alternative reinforcer is low. Furthermore, the likelihood of observing a priming effect under these conditions varies with the priming dose consumed. Previous studies have not systematically examined these variables (i.e., varying priming doses or the availability of an alternative reinforcer), which may account for the inability of some researchers to observe a priming effect (e.g., Engle & Williams, 1972; Kaplan et al., 1983; Marlatt et al., 1973).

The strength of the priming effect depends on the ETOH preload dose. This is demonstrated by both the interaction of the probability of earning money with preload dose on responding for ETOH and the effects of the perload on the subjective ratings of desire for ETOH. In both instances, the higher preload dose generally produced a stronger effect than the lower dose. Whether still higher preload doses would produce an even greater effect is not known. However, it is unlikely that doses much higher than 0.5 g/kg would produce further increases in preference for ETOH in this population of subjects. These subjects normally consumed an average of 4.1 drinks per occasion (e.g., over the course of an evening), which is equivalent to a dose only slightly higher than the 0.5 g/kg administered in a single preload dose in our study. Thus, higher preload doses of ETOH would probably “satiate” the subjects, decreasing their desire to consume more. The relationship of the priming effect to the dose administered in the preload and to subjects' habitual alcohol use is an important topic for future research.

The strength of subjects' self-reported desire for drug varied as a function of time since the preload was consumed (see Figure 2). In this study, subjects began responding on the concurrent schedules 65 min following the preload, whereas the effect of ETOH preload on ratings of desire appeared to peak 30 min following the preloads. It is not known whether responding on the computer task covaried perfectly with self-reported desire for ETOH. Responding for ETOH may also vary according to the time at which subjects began responding on the concurrent schedules.

In addition to demonstrating a dose-dependent priming effect of ETOH, the results indicate that concurrent RR schedules can be a sensitive measure for quantifying preference for drugs. We have demonstrated their usefulness to quantify the priming effect of ETOH. They may also be useful to identify conditions under which preference for drugs is altered by other pharmacological (i.e., agonist or antagonist) pretreatments. Future research may also indicate whether the procedure is useful to identify the most effective alternative nondrug reinforcers (e.g., money or goods) that decrease drug-seeking behavior.
