Review By: Peter R. Grant

Review of: Relative Deprivation and Social Comparison: The Ontario Symposium, Volume 4
By: J. M. Olson, C. P. Herman, and M. P. Zanna, (Eds.)Hillsdale, New Jersey: Erlbaum, 1986 (259 pages; $32.50)

Biographical Information for Authors: James M. Olson is Associate Professor of psychology at the University of Western Ontario. He is a social psychologist with a particular interest in attitudes and interpersonal relations.

C. Peter Herman is a professor of psychology at the University of Toronto.

Mark P. Zanna is a professor of psychology at the University of Waterloo. He is a social psychologist whose work on attitude change and the link between attitudes and behaviour is internationally recognized for its quality.

Peter R. Grant is associate professor of psychology, Unversity of Saskatchewan. He is a social psychologist whose research interests are in the areas of intergroup conflict and social gerontology.

This volume addresses two very important and interrelated topics in social psychology through a collection of relatively independent papers. Following an introductory chapter, the authors of chapters 2 through 5 discuss various theoretical approaches to the determinants of resentment engendered by relative deprivation and recent research designed to address this theorizing. Then, chapters 6 through 8 discuss recent work on social comparison processes. Finally, chapters 9 through 12 use the concepts of relative deprivation and social comparison to suggest ways to understand four important social issues: minority-majority relations, dissatisfaction in organizational settings, social protest, and tolerance of injustice.

The first section on the determinants of feelings of resentment in response to deprivation is very uneven and, for me, disappointing. In chapter 2, Crosby, Muehrer, and Loewenstein present a secondary analysis of survey data focussing on grievances about work and home life. Using a regression analysis, they compare six models of relative deprivation and find that none of them is clearly superior in predictive power. On this basis, the authors suggest that the models are not of much value. However, just because the models account for the same percentage of the variance, does not mean they have equal explanatory power. The ability of a particular model to elucidate the causal determinants of relative deprivation as opposed to identifying plausible correlates must be taken into account.

By far the best chapter in this section is the one by Folger (chapter 3). His explication of a referent cognitions theory of relative deprivation is very clear and the evidence that supports it convincing. Simply put, this theory suggests that a deprived individual will feel resentment and moral outrage if he/she can easily imagine experiencing a better outcome, but feels that this alternative is unlikely and that this situation is unjust. A strength of this theory is that it deals with the distinction between egotistical and fraternal relative deprivation. Further, Folger is the only author in the book to make the point that soical comparison information need not necessarily be a determinant of a sense of relative deprivation, although it often is.

Chapter 4 by Olson, describes a series of five studies which examine the impact of qualifications on feelings of deprivation. While interesting the results are in no way definitive: the results from two studies investigating actual deprivation differ from those that use role play and neither set of studies fully supports Olson’s hypothesis. Because Folger’s "likelihood of future outcomes" variable seems very similar to Olson’s "resource abundance" variable, I would have liked to see a consideration of the role of qualifications from the perspective of referent cognitions theory in this chapter.

Starting with a brief review of the determinants of well-being from a social indicators perspective, Ross, Eyman, and Kishchuk (chapter 5) present the results of three studies in which respondents are asked to rate and explain their present level of happiness or life satisfaction. Speculation about the determinants of well-being is based on a content analysis of these explanations. Unfortunately, Kozma and Stones’ work (e.g. Kozma & Stones, 1980, Kozma & Stones, 1983; Stones & Kozma, 1986) on the measurement of happiness and the role of affect in determining happiness is not discussed even though it is directly relevant to Ross et al.’s results. Further, the "surprising" finding that social comparison explanations for well-being comprise only 5% to 13% of all responses is hard to interpret because the authors do not report the percentage of respondents who gave such an explanation. Indeed, when explicitly asked, most respondents stated they did make social comparisons with particular individuals whom they were able to name.

The second section on social comparisons is both interesting and informative. Suls (chapter 6) theorizes that there are developmental trends in the types of social comparisons that individuals make. Some evidence is presented that suggests that very young children do not make social comparisons and 5 to 9 year olds make indiscriminant ones. Thus, Festinger’s (1954) theory applies only to the behaviour of older children, who make comparisons with similar others. While the evidence is very scarce, Suls also argues that people do not confine their social comparisons to similar others in middle age, and in old age they tend to make comparisons with themselves at an earlier age.

Atkinson’s chapter is also primarily theoretical and based upon Tajfel and Turner’s (1979) social identity theory and Tversky’s (1977) feature-matching model of similarity judgements. In essence, he suggests that the characteristics of a certain outgroup and whether or not the person is asked to make similarity or difference judgements will influence the type of social comparison made. For example, salient outgroup characteristics may cause ingroup members to focus on particular aspects of their group’s identity.

The fact that individuals often overestimate the proportion of people agreeing with their opinions (false consensus) forms the basis of a discussion of self-serving social comparisons in chapter 8. Goethals describes an interesting set of studies in which subjects are presented with an accurate and vivid portrayal of the actual distribution of opinions on a particular issue. The results show that the false consensus effect is still obtained in the face of this information. Then, he presents a second set of studies in which subjects are asked to make a decision. Results show that the false consensus effect is reduced when people believe their decision is the socially desirable one, but exaggerated when they believe it is not. Because motivational rather than cognitive explanations for these results are more plausible, Goethals suggests that researchers study self-serving motives for social comparisons which create feelings of deprivation, although he does not elaborate on how this might be done.

The final section of this book is, in my view, the best one. This is partly because the distinction between egotistical and fraternal relative deprivation is discussed in detail, and partly because relative deprivation theory is examined in the context of important social problems.

Chapter 9 by Dion is my favorite. First, he reviews his research program on the phenomenology of being a victim of prejudice. This work demonstrates that the belief that personal failure at an experimental task is due to discrimination heightens group identity and protects self-esteem even though it is stressful. Then, Dion examines survey research which shows a consistent relationship between fraternal relative deprivation and indices of negative inter-group relations, and no such relationship between egotistical relative deprivation and these indices. A model is proposed which links different reactions to fraternal relative deprivation with feelings of personal control and feelings of control over the social system. The importance of examining the conditions under which a person experiencing a personal deprivation will infer that a group deprivation (discrimination) has occurred is also discussed.

Organizations are complex entities, and in chapter 10, de Carufel deals with some of the implications of this fact for relative deprivation theory. For example, he points out that, in organizations, it is important that procedures as well as actual resource allocations be fair, that organizations provide opportunity for multiple social comparisons over time, and that employees will choose to work in an organization in spite of some deprivations provided other aspects of the job are fulfilling. After a brief review of some pertinent studies conducted in organizational settings, the author concludes by discussing a study which tested a model of organizational behaviour to help clarify the role played by relative deprivation in an organizational setting.

Dube and Guimond (Chapter 11) present evidence from two studies which show that fraternal and not egotistical relative deprivation is associated with sociopolitical attitudes favouring the Quebec Nationalist movement. More specifically, the evidence suggests that it is the affective (group discontent) rather than the cognitive component (perception of group inequity) of fraternal relative deprivation which seems to be a direct determinant of these attitudes. Further, a third study obtained a strong association between group discontent and the protest behaviour of students at the University of Montreal.

The final chapter by Martin deals with tolerance of injustice. She presents evidence from three role play experiments in which she demonstrates that the larger the pay inequity between two groups, the more the subjects felt personal and group deprivation. However, in contrast to the Dube and Guimond’s findings (Chapter 11), no relationship was found between deprivation and likelihood of engaging in social protest. Martin speculates that people follow a sequential, contingency model when responding to deprivation in which they pragmatically go through different behavioural alternatives starting with the one that involves the least risk and effort.

All in all, I highly recommend this book to anyone wishing to update themselves on current research trends in this area. Two of the three sections are excellent and third is of uneven rather than poor quality. Further, the regrettable focus on egotistical rather than fraternal relative deprivation in the social psychology literature is not as prevalent in this book as I feared it might be. The editors are to be congratulated on achieving such broad coverage of this important research area.
