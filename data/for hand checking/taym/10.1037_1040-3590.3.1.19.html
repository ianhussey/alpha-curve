For decades, “organic amnesia” was thought to be a single disorder regardless of the etiology or location of the brain damage (Goldstein, 1939; Halstead, 1947; Head, 1920; Lashley, 1950; Warrington & Weiskrantz, 1973; Wechsler, 1945). Within this conceptual framework, clinical memory tests were designed to assess the presence or absence of memory dysfunction per se. This goal was accomplished by providing standardized scores only for the level of correct recall or recognition (Babcock & Levy, 1940; Denman, 1984; Graham & Kendall, 1960; Rey, 1964; Talland, 1965; Warrington, 1984; Wechsler, 1945, 1987). In the past 15 years, however, this unitary view of amnesia has been refuted. A wealth of data from cognitive–neuropsychological research has documented qualitatively distinct memory disorders associated with different neurological and psychiatric populations (Butters & Cermak, 1980; Butters & Stuss, 1989; Damasio, Tranel, & Damasio, 1989; Delis & Ober, 1986; Levin, 1989; Squire, 1987). These disorders are best characterized in terms of the spared and impaired components of memory. For example, patients with Alzheimer's disease (AD) and Huntington's disease (HD) often show the same level of recall on explicit memory tests, but differ in terms of their forgetting rates, vulnerability to proactive interference, encoding versus retrieval problems, intrusion rates, and recognition discriminability (Butters, Martone, White, Granholm, & Wolfe, 1986; Kramer et al., 1988). Because most clinical memory tests assess only the level of correct recall or recognition, they are insufficient for identifying and distinguishing between these memory disorders (for reviews, see Butters, 1984; Delis, 1989; Loring & Papanicolaou, 1987; Mayes, 1986).

The California Verbal Learning Test (CVLT; Delis, Kramer, Kaplan, & Ober, 1987) was developed to enhance clinicians' accuracy in identifying and characterizing different memory disorders. The CVLT differs from traditional memory tests in that its scoring system goes well beyond the conventional measure of the total number of correct words recalled or recognized. The CVLT quantifies and provides normative data for numerous additional indices derived from cognitive psychology, including (a) semantic clustering; (b) primacy-recency effects; (c) rate of acquisition across trials; (d) consistency of item recall across trials; (e) measures of vulnerability to interference; (f) retention over short and longer delays; (g) intrusion and perseveration rates; and (h) signal detection parameters (discriminability and response bias) of recognition performance.

Clinical investigations have found the CVLT to be useful in characterizing the memory deficits associated with a number of neurological and psychiatric disorders, including head injury (Crosson, Novack, Trenerry, & Craig, 1988), multiple sclerosis (Kessler, Lauer, & Kausch, 1985), temporal-lobe epilepsy (Hermann, Wyler, Richey, & Rea, 1987; Hermann, Wyler, Steenman, & Richey, 1988), chronic alcoholism (Kramer, Blusewicz, & Preston, 1989), human immunodeficiency virus (Saykin et al., in press), Alzheimer's disease (Kramer et al., 1988; Kramer, Levin, Brandt, & Delis, 1989), Parkinson's disease (Kramer et al., 1989; Massman, Delis, Butters, Levin, & Salmon, 1990), Huntington's disease (Kramer et al., 1988; Massman et al., 1990; Strauss, Brandt, Hale, Delis, & Folstein, 1989), schizophrenia (Authelet & Raymond, 1987; Cullum et al., 1990), the affective state in premenstrual syndrome (Keenan, Stern, & Janowsky, 1990), major affective disorders (Dupont et al., 1990), language-impaired children (Shear, Tallal, & Delis, in press), and children with attention deficit disorder (Loge, Staton, & Beatty, in press). In addition, factor-analytic studies of the CVLT have found that its expanded scoring system yields a number of theoretically meaningful component factors beyond the single factor found when only correct recall is scored (Delis, Freeland, Kramer, & Kaplan, 1988; Schear & Craft, 1989).

To date, most of the clinical studies of the CVLT have focused on individual patient groups. Only three studies have compared the CVLT performances of different clinical populations directly (Kramer et al., 1988; 1989; Massman et al., 1990), and two of these studies examined primarily error types (Kramer et al., 1988; 1989). Further research thus is needed to determine the utility of the CVLT for identifying and discriminating between the memory disorders of different patient groups.

In the present study, we directly compare the CVLT profiles associated with three neurological disorders: a prototypical “cortical” dementia, AD; a prototypical “subcortical” dementia, HD; and a prototypical amnesic syndrome, alcoholic Korsakoff syndrome (KS). These three groups were chosen because their memory disorders have been researched extensively by cognitive neuropsychologists. Consistent patterns of spared and impaired memory functions have been associated with the groups. This corpus of data affords an important test of the construct validity of the CVLT. Specifically, we investigated whether or not the CVLT profiles of these patient groups are consistent with findings from past experimental studies of their memory disorders.

In the experimental studies, AD and KS patients have been found to display strikingly similar memory deficits. Both AD and KS patients evince limited immediate recall spans, failure to show learning on repeated trials of supra-span material, severely impaired retention over time, high intrusion rates, increased sensitivity to proactive interference, and poor recognition performance characterized by high false-positive rates (Butters & Cermak, 1980; Butters, Granholm, Salmon, Grant, & Wolfe, 1987; Corkin, 1982; Fuld, 1976; Kaszniak, Garron, & Fox, 1979; Kramer et al., 1988; Meudell, Butters, & Montgomery, 1978; Morris & Baddeley, 1988; Moss, Albert, Butters, & Payne, 1986; Ober, Koss, Friedland, & Delis, 1985; Post, 1975; Wilson, Bacon, Fox, & Kaszniak, 1983). In light of these findings, several investigators have proposed a common, neurocognitive mechanism underlying the memory deficits of AD and KS patients (Butters, 1985; Kopelman, 1987; Salmon & Butters, 1987; Squire, 1987). The patients' severely deficient free recall and recognition performances suggest predominantly an encoding and storage impairment. Damage to the hippocampus in AD (Hyman, Van Hoesen, Damasio, & Barnes, 1984) and to the dorsomedial nucleus of the thalamus in KS (Victor, Adams, & Collins, 1971) may disrupt the same memory system at different sites, resulting in similar explicit memory deficits (Butters & Stuss, 1989; Mishkin, 1982; Squire, 1987; there is also evidence of damage to the medial diencephalon in AD, McDuff & Sumi, 1985). In addition, compromise of the basal forebrain with its cholinergic input to the cortex has been reported in both AD and KS (Arendt, Bigl, Arendt, & Tennstedt, 1983) and may contribute to the similarities in memory deficits of the two groups (Butters, 1985; Salmon & Butters, 1987).

As just noted, HD patients show a qualitatively different pattern of memory deficits. These patients often display levels of immediate recall similar to those of AD and KS patients, but they typically show better retention over time, make fewer intrusion errors, are less vulnerable to interference effects, and show greater improvement on recognition testing relative to free recall (suggesting predominantly a retrieval deficit; Butters et al., 1987; Butters et al., 1988; Butters, Wolfe, Granholm, & Martone, 1986; Butters, Wolfe, Martone, Granholm, & Cermak, 1985; Caine, Ebert, & Weingartner, 1977; Kramer et al., 1989; Meudell et al., 1978). This pattern of spared and impaired memory functions has been associated with neostriatal damage, the primary area of pathology in HD (Cummings & Benson, 1983). A similar memory disorder has been reported in other patient groups with predominantly subcortical damage (e.g., Parkinson's disease; Massman et al., 1990).

On the basis of these findings from experimental studies, we predicted that the AD and KS patients would display a similar CVLT profile and that this profile would be qualitatively different from that of the HD patients. Specifically, we hypothesized that the HD patients would show levels of immediate recall comparable to those of the AD and KS patients, but better retention over time, lower intrusion rates, less vulnerability to interference, and better recognition performance. In addition, we predicted that a discriminant function analysis using key CVLT variables would clearly differentiate the AD and HD patients and that this function would classify the KS patients as AD rather than as HD patients.

Method

Subjects

Twenty AD patients (13 men and 7 women) and 20 HD patients (14 men and 6 women), who were recruited from the Alzheimer's Disease Research Center at the University of California, San Diego, participated in the study. Subjects were recruited without regard to ethnicity or race. All AD patients were diagnosed by a senior staff neurologist according to the criteria developed by the NINCDS–ADRDA Work Group (McKhann et al., 1984). Extensive laboratory testing was performed to rule out other possible causes of dementia. The HD patients had been diagnosed by a senior staff neurologist on the basis of a positive family history of HD, the presence of involuntary choreiform movements, and the presence of dementia. They had been experiencing motor symptoms for a mean of approximately 4.5 years.

CVLT protocols of 8 alcoholic KS patients (all men) were obtained from the Memory Disorders Research Center at Boston University and from the San Diego Veterans Administration Medical Center. The KS patients were diagnosed according to the criteria established by Butters and Cermak (1980). The patients' mean Full Scale IQ on the WAIS–R was 100.5 (SD = 11.18) , their mean General Memory Index on the Wechsler Memory Scale–Revised (WMS–R; Wechsler, 1987) was 76.13 (SD = 12.48) , and their mean Delayed Recall Index on the WMS–R was 55.63. The mean difference between their WAIS–R IQs and WMS–R Delayed Recall Index scores was 44.88 (SD = 7.37) ; this finding depicts the severity of their anterograde amnesia relative to their average level of intellectual functioning.

Table 1 shows demographic data and mean scores on the Dementia Rating Scale (DRS; Mattis, 1976) for the three patient groups. The three groups did not differ in terms of years of education. Due to differences in the average age of onset of the different disorders (Cummings & Benson, 1983), the HD patients were significantly younger than the KS and AD patients, and the AD patients were significantly older than the KS patients. To control for differences in age and sex distributions across the groups, standardized scores from the CVLT, which are stratified by age and sex, were used in most of the analyses. For those variables analyzed in the present study that do not have standardized scores on the CVLT, ratio scores were computed that correct for level of memory performance (as discussed here later).

 

As expected, the KS patients had significantly higher DRS scores than the two groups with progressive dementing disorders, whereas the AD and HD groups' DRS scores were not significantly different (p > .50) . The three groups, however, did not differ in their level of free recall on the CVLT learning trials (as discussed here later). Thus, any differential patterns of spared and impaired components of memory found in the patient groups could not be attributed to differences in their global level of recall performance.

Procedure

The CVLT was administered by trained psychological technicians as part of a larger set of neuropsychological tests. The test consists of the oral presentation of a “shopping” list of 16 words (List A) for five, immediate-recall trials. The list comprises four items from each of four categories. Adjacent words on the list are from different categories in order to assess the degree to which an examinee uses the active learning strategy of recalling the words in semantic clusters. Following the five learning trials, a second interference list (List B) is presented for one trial. Free- and category-cued-recall trials of List A are then administered (the “short delay” trials). After a 20-minute interval in which nonverbal testing occurs (the “long delay”), free recall, cued recall, and recognition of List A are assessed. The yes/no recognition test contains the 16 List A target items and 28 distracters. The subjects' paper-and-pencil protocols were scored using the CVLT scoring software (Fridlund & Delis, 1987). Results

Results for the three patient groups on 15 standardized CVLT indices are shown in Table 3. The numbers in the tables are standard deviation scores corrected for age and sex (e.g., a mean score of −1.63 indicates that the subjects in the group scored an average of 1.63 standard deviations below persons of their age and sex in the CVLT normative sample). The data were analyzed using ANOVA with orthogonal planned comparisons tests for all of the variables except for the List A Trial 5 and Long Delay Cued Recall measures; these latter variables were analyzed using nonparametric Mann-Whitney tests because of heterogeneity of variance across groups. The planned comparisons were formulated in accordance with the two key questions under investigation: (a) Do AD and KS patients differ on any of the CVLT indices? and (b) Do HD patients (who have prominent retrieval deficits) differ from the combined group of AD and KS patients (who have prominent encoding and storage deficits) on the predicted CVLT measures but not on the other indices? Although these analyses do not permit an examination of all possible pairwise comparisons, they involve a lower probability of Type I error than performing t tests between all groups, and they are more powerful for detecting differences than performing ANOVA with post-hoc comparisons tests (e.g., Tukey).

 

The results indicated that the AD and KS patients failed to differ significantly on any of the 15 variables (p > .10 for all comparisons; median p value was .52). Thus, the AD and KS patients showed comparable levels of immediate free recall across multiple trials, learning slope, semantic clustering, primacy–recency effects, recall consistency, delayed free and cued recall, recognition discriminability, false positives, and response bias. The HD group failed to differ significantly from the combined AD and KS groups in their levels of immediate recall, learning slope across trials, semantic clustering, recall consistency, primacy and recency effects, and response bias. The HD patients, however, showed substantially better recognition discriminability, t (45) = 4.72, p < .001, for the planned comparison, lower false-positive rates, t (45) = −4.37, p < .001, for the planned comparison, and higher difference scores between recognition discriminability and Trial 5 recall, t (45) = 5.35, p < .001, for the planned comparison, than did the AD and KS patients.

Table 4 shows the delayed-recall saving scores and error-type analyses for the three patient groups. As previously noted here, because these variables do not have standardized scores on the CVLT, ratio measures were computed that correct for number of responses given (see descriptions in Table 2). Results for the Percent Long Delay Saving variable were analyzed using ANOVA with orthogonal planned comparisons tests; data for the other variables were analyzed using Mann-Whitney tests because of heterogeneity of variance across groups.

 

The results indicated that the AD and KS patients again failed to differ significantly on any of these measures (p > .30 for all comparisons; median p value was .89). Thus, the AD and KS patients displayed comparable delayed-recall saving scores, perseveration rates, free- and cued-recall intrusion rates, and intrusion type (i.e., percentage of words recalled on the List B trial that were List A words). In contrast, the HD patients (a) had better delayed-recall saving scores than did the AD and KS patients, t (45) = 4.87, p < .001, for the planned comparison; (b) generated lower percentages of intrusion errors on the free- and cued-recall trials than did the AD and KS patients (p < .001 for both Mann-Whitney tests); (c) displayed a trend to produce lower percentages of intrusions of List A words on the List B trial compared with the AD and KS patients (p = .058 for the Mann-Whitney test); and (d) generated higher percentages of perseverative errors than did the AD and KS patients (p < .01 for the Mann-Whitney test).

The foregoing results indicate that the AD and KS patients performed similarly on all of the CVLT variables, and were markedly different from the HD patients on several key measures. To conclude that AD and KS patients show the same CVLT profile, however, would be tantamount to accepting the null hypothesis. To examine the apparent similarity between the AD and KS patients' performances more thoroughly, discriminant function analyses were performed. The two CVLT variables on which the AD and HD groups appeared to differ most strongly were selected for the analysis: Percent Cued Recall Intrusions and Recognition Discriminability versus List A Trial 5. Because the relatively small sample sizes of the groups do not permit independent-sample cross-validation, jackknife analyses, in which each subject is classified on the basis of the discriminant function derived from the remaining (n − 1) subjects, were performed. The analysis yielded a Wilks's lambda of .35 (p < .001) , and the jackknife discriminant functions correctly classified 85.0% (17 out of 20) of the AD patients and 84.2% (16 out of 19) of the HD patients (results from 1 HD subject could not be computed because he gave no responses on the cued recall trials). The same discriminant function was then applied to the KS group: The analysis classified 100% (all 8) of the KS patients as AD patients. These findings buttress the proposition that the CVLT results of the KS patients resemble those of the AD patients but not those of the HD patients.

Discussion

In this study, the construct validity of the CVLT was investigated by examining whether or not the CVLT profiles of AD, HD, and KS patients were consistent with findings from past experimental studies of their memory disorders. A striking finding was that the AD and KS patients failed to differ significantly on any of the 20 CVLT indices analyzed (p > .10 for all planned comparisons and Mann-Whitney tests; median p value was .60). One would have expected at least one of the 20 variables to be significant by chance. Thus, both the AD and KS groups displayed severely impaired immediate recall, flat learning rates across trials, inconsistent recall across trials, ineffective use of semantic clustering, a tendency to recall words passively from the recency region of the list, poor retention over delay intervals, high intrusion rates, poor recognition discriminability, high false positive rates, a mild “Yes” response bias, and no improvement on recognition testing relative to free recall (suggesting predominantly an encoding and storage impairment). The similar amnesic profiles of the AD and KS patients is consonant with findings of past experimental studies of the explicit memory deficits of the two groups (for reviews, see Butters, 1985; Kopelman, 1987; Salmon & Butters, 1987).

The HD patients displayed levels of free- and cued-recall comparable to those of the AD and KS patients. The HD patients also showed a number of recall characteristics similar to those of the AD and KS patients, including flat learning rates, inconsistent recall, large recency effect, and ineffective use of semantic clustering. These findings are consistent with those of past experimental studies reporting a severe recall impairment in HD patients (Butters et al., 1985, 1986; Caine et al., 1977).

In contrast, the HD patients surpassed the AD and KS patients on the predicted CVLT measures. The HD group displayed (a) better retention on delayed recall; (b) lower intrusion rates on the free- and cued-recall trials; (c) a trend to recall a lower percentage of List A words on the List B Trial (suggesting less vulnerability to interference); (d) lower false-positive rates on recognition testing; (e) higher recognition discriminability; and (f) greater improvement on recognition testing relative to free recall (implicating a prominent retrieval impairment). These CVLT results are consonant with past experimental studies that have documented a qualitatively distinct memory disorder in HD patients compared with AD and KS patients (Butters et al., 1976, 1985, 1986). The findings indicate that the CVLT is useful in identifying the type of memory disorder associated with a prototypical subcortical dementia (i.e., HD) and with a prototypical cortical dementia (i.e., AD).

Discriminant function analyses using key CVLT indices correctly classified 85.0% of the AD patients and 84.2% of the HD patients. As predicted, when the discriminant function was applied to the KS patients, all eight (100%) were classified as AD patients. These findings provide further support for the construct validity of the CVLT in identifying and distinguishing between the memory disorders of these patient groups.

An unexpected finding was that the HD patients generated a higher percentage of perseverative responses than did the AD and KS patients. A recent study also reported a high perseveration rate in a different sample of HD patients (Kramer et al., 1989). These findings are intriguing because they suggest a double dissociation between type of memory disorder and errors. That is, HD patients may generate more perseverations, whereas AD and KS patients may produce more intrusions.

Several clinical implications can be drawn from the present results. First, our findings illustrate a difficulty in making the differential diagnosis between early AD and KS, particularly with AD patients who have a history of alcohol abuse. Severe amnesia is the hallmark of KS (Butters & Cermak, 1980), and it is typically the first finding in early AD (Katzman, 1981). The present results indicate that the assessment of new learning and memory, though helpful in narrowing the diagnostic possibilities, will be of little value in distinguishing between AD and KS. Documentation of deficits in other cognitive domains such as naming, praxis, and visuospatial function is needed to implicate an Alzheimer's process (Cummings & Benson, 1983; Delis, 1989; Lezak, 1983). In most cases of early AD, a reevaluation 6 to 12 months following baseline testing is necessary to confirm a progressive decline in cognitive functions.

A second implication concerns a shortcoming in most clinical memory tests. Because most tests assess either recall or recognition, they are likely to misdiagnose the nature and extent of memory dysfunction in patients with subcortical pathology (e.g., HD, Parkinson's disease, infarctions in the neostriatum and surrounding white matter; Cummings & Benson, 1983). On the one hand, tests that measure recall only (e.g., the Logical Memory and Visual Reproduction subtests of the WMS–R, Wechsler, 1987; the selective reminding procedure, Buschke, 1973; the verbal memory subtests of the Denman Neuropsychology Memory Scale, Denman, 1984; the list and story recall tasks of the Randt Memory Test, Randt & Brown, 1983) are likely to exaggerate the severity of amnesia in patients with subcortical disorders. These patients often display levels of recall comparable to those of patients with damage to mesial temporal–diencephalic structures (e.g., AD and KS patients) but show superior recognition performance. Thus, recognition testing is needed to demonstrate that subcortical patients typically have better encoding and storage than mesial temporal–diencephalic patients. On the other hand, tests that assess recognition only (e.g., the Figural Memory subtest of the WMS–R; the Recognition Memory Test, Warrington, 1984; the Memory for Human Faces subtest, Denman, 1984; the Picture Recognition subtest, Randt & Brown, 1983) are likely to minimize the memory deficits of subcortical patients, because recall testing is needed to detect the severe retrieval impairment characteristic of these patients. The optimal clinical testing method is to assess memory for the same target stimuli using both recall and recognition procedures. In this way, both the prominent retrieval deficit of subcortical patients and the encoding and storage impairment of mesial temporal–diencephalic patients can be identified. This combined recall-recognition procedure is used on the Rey Auditory Verbal Learning Test (RAVLT, Lezak, 1983; Rey, 1964), Kaplan's adaptation of the WMS (Milberg, Hebben, & Kaplan, 1986), a recent adaptation of the selective reminding procedure (Masur et al., 1989), the Biber Figure Learning Test (Glosser, Goodglass, & Biber, 1989), the Fuld Object-Memory Test (Fuld, 1981), and the CVLT.

A third implication is the importance of scoring and providing normative data for error types. The combination of high rates of intrusions in recall and false positives in recognition is characteristic of patients with mesial temporal–diencephalic damage, as in AD and KS (Butters et al., 1987; Fuld, Katzman, Davies, & Terry, 1982; Kramer et al., 1988). Perseverative errors are important in identifying the memory disorders of frontal-lobe patients (Luria, 1981) and, as suggested in this study, in HD patients (see also Kramer et al., 1989). On a children's version of the CVLT (Delis et al., in press), language-impaired children also have been found to generate high rates of perseverative errors (Shear et al., in press). The Benton Visual Retention Test (Benton, 1974) and the CVLT are the only standardized memory instruments that provide normative data for error types.

In summary, the congruency between the CVLT profiles of AD, HD, and KS patients and findings from past experimental studies of their memory deficits provides further support for the construct validity of the CVLT. The results underscore the importance of assessing the spared and impaired components of different memory disorders.
