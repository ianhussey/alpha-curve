There continues to be considerable debate as to whether or not executive functions decline with age. Some authors (Daigneault & Braun, 1993; Malloy & Richardson, 1994) have contended that frontal lobe or executive functioning declines with age and may even be the first to deteriorate (Daigneault & Braun, 1993). On the face of it, this appears correct because there is considerable evidence of poorer performance by older adults compared with younger adults on a variety of executive function tasks (Ardila & Rosselli, 1989; Arenberg, 1982; Davis & Bernstein, 1992; Flicker, Ferris, Crook, Bartus, & Reisberg, 1986; Parkin & Lawrence, 1994; Parkin & Walter, 1991; Reese & Rodeheaver, 1985; Stuss, Stethem, & Poirier, 1987). Other authors (Boone, Miller, Lesser, Hill, & D'Elia, 1990; Kramer, Humphrey, Larish, Logan, & Strayer, 1994), however, have had variable results, with significant age differences on some, but not all, executive function tests.

Some of the variability may be due to dilemmas in aging research in general, such as inclusion–exclusion criteria in sample selection (Ardila & Rosselli, 1989; Howieson, Holm, Kaye, Oken, & Howieson, 1993), cohort effects (Bordens & Abbot, 1991; Drachman, 1986; Lezak, 1995), skewed attrition (Drachman, 1986; Zelinski, Gilewski, & Schaie, 1993), and differential impact of emotional factors (Deptula, Singh, & Pomara, 1993), including depression (Raskin, 1986). In addition, attention, memory, and task familiarity (Reese & Rodeheaver, 1985) may have a disproportionately adverse effect on the performance of older adults. On timed tests, central slowing (Salthouse, 1989), a decline in effortful processes (Hasher & Zacks, 1979), mental slowing (particularly with increasing task complexity; Stuss, Stethem, Picton, Leech, & Pelchat, 1989), greater response time with more operations (Nebes & Madden, 1988), and slower perceptual speed (Schaie, 1989) are particularly consequential.

The term executive(or frontal lobe) functionsdescribes a wide range of abilities that integrate, organize, and maintain other cognitive processes, such as memory and attention. Although there is little consensus on the definition of executive functions, some agreement exists that executive functions include abilities such as self-regulation, sequencing of behavior, flexibility, response inhibition, planning, and organization of behavior (Eslinger, 1996). Varying definitions make operationalization difficult (Borkowki & Burke, 1996; Lyon, 1996). A test may be considered a measure of attention by one researcher and a measure of executive functions by another. For example, both the Trail Making Test, originally from the Army Individual Test Battery (as cited in Lezak, 1983), and the Stroop Color–Word Test (Stroop, 1935/1992) are often used as attention measures and as executive function measures (Morris, 1996). Both tests require at least two types of executive ability: mental flexibility and the capacity to inhibit overlearned responses in favor of performing novel behaviors. As such, they correspond to the first level of frontal lobe functions as formulated by Stuss and Benson (1986), which include sequencing and set formation, which requires the control of interference and an ability to identify and extract relevant information. Failures in these types of complex tasks may be due to an inability to render independent responses rather than stimulus-bound or stereotypic responses. These tasks are similar to those developed and administered by Luria (1973) in his seminal frontal lobe studies, such as requesting the patient to lift a fist when the examiner lifts a finger and vice versa.

The complexity of executive functions makes it difficult to distinguish what actual cognitive processes are involved (Stuss, 1993). Moreover, tests that are depicted as assessing executive functions actually include various nonexecutive component functions. For example, although the Stroop Color–Word Test is commonly perceived of as an executive function test, it also relies on immediate memory to remember that the task requires naming the color of the ink (Hayes, Gifford, & Ruckstuhl, 1996). Therefore, when test performance is summarized by a single score, it can be particularly misleading. To counteract this problem, some researchers (Owen, Downes, Sahakian, Polkey, & Robbins, 1990) have quantified the individual components of a complex executive function task, and then, to delineate the source of group differences, they have attempted to control for performance on these component aspects (Baddeley, Bressi, Della Sala, Logie, & Spinnler, 1991; Baddeley, Logie, Bressi, Della Sala, & Spinnler, 1986).

As early as 1969, Lindsey and Copppinger occupied themselves with the question of whether age-related declines in the simple capabilities that are required for the performance of the Trail Making Test account for age declines on the more complex Trail Making Test, Part B (as cited in Lezak, 1983). They compared performances by age on the Trail Making Test, Part B after they partialed out six simple skills: saying numbers capability, visual search capability, finding next letter capability, simultaneous number capability, simultaneous number–letter capability, and Trail Making, Part A. They concluded that after the simple capability tasks were partialed out, age was not significantly correlated with Trail Making Test, Part B. Although independence of variables is, by definition, unattainable, the interdependence of the predictor and criterion variables in their study may have skewed the results. All the simple skill tasks were significantly correlated with the Trail Making Test, Part B. The simultaneous number–letter capability task, which required participants “to say the alphabet and count at the same time” (p. 157), was very similar to the Trail Making Test, Part B, except without the scanning and motor components. In fact, Lindsey and Coppinger (1969) conceded that the high correlation between several tasks and the Trail Making Test, Part B suggest that the processes that were intended to be simple, component skills were actually rather complex, making it unlikely that there would be any further effects for age on Trail Making Test, Part B.

Typically, however, Part A of the Trail Making Test (as cited in Lezak, 1983) is used as the exclusive component, and when controlled for performance on Part B, age is found to be a significant factor (Koss et al., 1991; Salthouse, Fristoe, & Rhee, 1996; Stanton, Jenkins, Savageau, & Zyzanski, 1984).

Similarly, with another executive function test, the Stroop Color–Word Test, color naming and word reading are used as nonexecutive component, or baseline, measures for the more complicated interference section. Older adults in several studies have consistently demonstrated slower color naming in the interference condition, implying a larger Stroop effect (Cohn, Dustman, & Bradford, 1984; Daigneault, Braun, & Whitaker, 1992; Hartley, 1993). Panek, Rush, and Slade (1983) found that the degree to which reading words dominated naming colors did not predict performance on the interference task for older adults, however. A meta-analysis conducted by Verhaeghen and De Meersman (1998) of 20 Stroop Color–Word Test studies revealed no significant difference between younger and older adults on the interference condition when performances on baseline skills (color naming, symbols, or word reading) were accounted for.

Another method of judging performance on executive function tasks, besides by time to completion, is by analysis of errors. For example, on the Trail Making Test, Part B, errors may indicate impairment in the mental flexibility required to maintain attention to the two sequences simultaneously. The relationship between age and error patterns remains unclear. Recently, Lowe and Reynolds (1999) reported no significant age differences on three types of error scores on a verbal sorting task in an older sample of 54- to 89-year-olds. Boone et al. (1990) reported age differences on total errors on another sorting test but found nonsignificant age differences on overall performance, perseverative responses, perseverative errors, and failure to maintain set. However, when Daigneault et al. (1992) contrasted a subsample of younger participants (20- to 34-years-old) to the Boone and colleague's youngest group (50- to 59-year-olds), the older group exhibited a relative decline on perseverative errors. Stanton et al. (1984) found that the number of errors on Part B of the Trail Making Test was not significantly correlated with age. Consequently, further research, including broad age ranges and various types of executive function tasks, is needed to clarify these inconsistencies.

The present study was intended to clarify the aforementioned and other previous contradictory findings of age effect and executive function performance. Performance on versions of the Trail Making Test and Stroop Color–Word Test were chosen as prototypical tests of an aspect of executive function, namely set maintenance. Set maintenance necessitates mental flexibility to purposefully maintain attention to one or more stimuli or task parameter while inhibiting inappropriate responses. Set maintenance has two aspects, the control of attention and inhibition, that is, attending to one or more stimuli while inhibiting attention to others. It also encompasses the ability to shift attention between two or more stimuli, such as parallel sequences, and the ability to inhibit overlearned responses in favor of originating novel behavior. Needless to say, set maintenance, especially related to complex and dual tasks, is often discussed in terms of executive, or frontal lobe, function, although it is alternatively classified as an attentional concept. Paradoxically, attentional processes are sometimes included as executive functions (Denckla, 1994).

The two tests used in the current study were versions of the California Trail Making Test and the California Stroop Test, subtests of a pilot version of the Delis–Kaplan Executive Function Scale (D-KEFS; Delis, Kaplan, & Kramer, in press). Each is composed of sections that allow for measuring both the relevant nonexecutive component skills and the complex executive functions. It was hypothesized that increased age would be associated with declines on complex set-maintenance tasks that could not be accounted for by differences in performance on the component skills. In addition to time to completion on scores for both tests, sequence loss and set-shifting errors for performances on the California Trail Making Test were analyzed.

Method

Participants

The sample for this study included 55 female and 57 male community-residing volunteers between the ages of 20 and 79 years. Individuals were excluded on the basis of self-reported acute or chronic psychiatric disorders and non-English fluency. Medical conditions were not exclusionary. The sample was drawn to match the U.S. census in terms of race, gender, age, and level of education. The mean age was 50.37 years (SD= 16.74), and the mean level of education was 12.8 years (SD= 2.01, range 8–17 years). Twelve participants (11%) were African American, 9 (8%) were Hispanic, and 91 (81%) were Caucasian. There were no significant differences in demographic features across the age range. In addition, there were no age differences on the Vocabulary subtest of the Wechsler Abbreviated Scale of Intelligence (Wechsler, 1999).

Materials and Procedure

Participants were administered a version of the California Trail Making Test and the California Stroop Test as 2 of the 11 subtests of a pilot version of the D-KEFS. This version of the California Trail Making Test included four sections that measure component skills (visual search, motor speed, number sequencing, and letter sequencing) and one section of increased complexity (number–letter switching). Number sequencing is similar to the original Trail Making Test, Part A, except that letters also appear on the page as distractors. Number–letter switching is comparable with Part B of the original Trail Making Test, requiring maintaining and alternating attention between numerical and alphabetical sequences. Visual search requires finding and marking numbers and letters on the page with the distinguishing quality of having curved parts. Motor speed requires tracing, or drawing over, an existing dotted line. Letter sequencing is similar to number sequencing, where both letters and numbers are printed on the page, but only letters are to be connected. Time to completion was obtained for each section. During test administration, errors are pointed out to the examinee and the examinee is instructed to return to and proceed from the last correct number or letter. For the number–letter sequencing section, errors are totaled and categorized as either failure-to-switch or sequence loss errors. Failure-to-switch errors are defined as when the examinee connects two numbers or two letters in sequence rather than alternating between the two sequences. Sequence loss errors occur when the examinee has alternated to the correct sequence of number or letter but connects the wrong number or letter. Thus, the California Trail Making Test expands the original Trail Making Test, allowing the quantification of more aspects of component skills, namely motoric speed, scanning speed, and alphabetical sequencing speed, embedded in the more complex task, and error analysis.

The pilot version of the California Stroop Test included two sections that measure component skills (color naming and word reading) and a section measuring complex skills (interference). The interference section requires inhibiting an automatic, overlearned response of reading words to respond in a novel way by naming the color of the ink in which words are printed. Time to completion was obtained for each section.

Results

Regression Analyses: Time to Completion

Preliminary analyses by Pearson product–moment correlation coefficients revealed that age was significantly correlated with performance, as measured by time to completion on most of the component sections (California Trail Making Test: visual search, number sequencing, letter sequencing; California Stroop Test: color naming) and with the executive function tasks (California Trail Making Test: switching; California Stroop Test; interference). Having established that there was an age effect on these tasks, our purpose in the current study was to determine whether an age effect remained with the executive function task after the component skills were partialed out. To address this question, we used a series of forced-entry multiple regression analyses. First, we entered scores for time to completion of the component sections of each of the executive function tests into the regression equation as a group, and then, we entered age. The dependent variable was the executive function score. For example, it was hypothesized that there was an age effect on the California Trail Making Test switching task time to completion when component skills (motor speed, visual search, number sequencing, and letter sequencing times) were controlled. Analysis revealed that the component skills explained approximately 60% of the variance and that age did not explain any additional variance. The results of the analysis are presented in Table 1.

Next, it was hypothesized that there was an age effect on the California Stroop Test interference task time to completion when component skills (color naming and word reading times) were controlled. In this case, component skills explained approximately 40% of the variance, and the inclusion of age accounted for an additional 5% of the variance (p< .005). In summary, analyses revealed that age was not a significant contributor to the variance after component skills were accounted for on the California Trail Making Test time to completion. However, age did account for a significant portion of the variance of the California Stroop Test interference task time to completion.

Error Analyses

On the California Trail Making Test switching section, few errors were made overall. Five participants (3%) committed three or more failure-to-switch errors, and 14 (13%) committed two or more such errors. Because the data were not normally distributed, we used the nonparametric Spearman's rho correlation test. There was no significant relationship between age and failure-to-switch errors on the California Trail Making Test switching section (Spearman's rho = .13, p= .18).

In regard to sequence loss errors on the California Trail Making Test, only 11 participants (10%) made three or more errors, but 20 (18%) committed two or more errors. In contrast to failure-to-switch errors, sequence loss errors were significantly correlated with age (Spearman's rho = .20, p< .05). In addition, correlation analysis revealed that age was not significantly correlated with errors on the California Stroop Test interference section (Spearman's rho = −.12, p= .23).

Discussion

This study investigated the effect of age across the adult life span on executive functions, specifically the ability to maintain set. Although executive functions draw on more fundamental cognitive functions, many executive function tests do not clearly delineate which of the many processes are being assessed. This is particularly problematic on timed tests when comparisons across the life span are sought because most studies have found older adults to be slower on almost all tasks. Consequently, if there is a decline in performance with age on complex executive function tests, it is difficult to determine whether the decline is due to qualitative changes in executive function abilities or the cumulative effect of performance on the simpler component skills (Nebes & Madden, 1988).

In the current study, age was found to account for a significant portion of the variance on the California Stroop Test, but not on the California Trail Making Test, when component skills that are embedded in complex executive function tasks were controlled by regression analysis. These disparate findings suggest that the California Stroop Test and the California Trail Making Test measure at least somewhat different abilities and that the effect of age is task specific. Although both tests require mental flexibility, the executive function aspects of the original Trail Making Test are thought to require an ability to shift course and to attend to more than one stimulus at a time (Spreen & Strauss, 1991). In contrast, the Stroop Color–Word Test places more emphasis on the ability to inhibit a more habituated response to produce a less automatic response (Lezak, 1983, 1995). In factor analysis, some authors have found the Trail Making Test to load on the same factor as the Stroop Test, whereas others have not. This discrepancy may be due, in part, to different versions of the tests being used (see Barkley, 1994; Shum, McFarland, & Bain, 1990).

With regard to the finding of no age effect on the Trail Making Test once component skills are accounted for, the current study is consistent with the research of Lindsey and Coppinger (1969), despite their inclusion of somewhat different component skills. The finding of an age effect on the California Stroop Test after partialing out performance on component skills is consistent with some reports of a larger Stroop effect for older adults (Cohn et al., 1984; Hartley, 1993; Rush, Panek, & Russell, 1990) but not with others who found no significant age effect (Boone et al., 1990; Verhaeghen & De Meersman, 1998). Uttl and Graf (1997) found only a very small effect size in a structural equation model analysis after color naming and word reading were fitted into the model. With a small effect size, discrepant findings can result from design variability, such as sampling differences (e.g., the range of ages included) and inclusion–exclusion criteria (particularly illnesses such as dementia that covary with age).

Methods of analysis for making age comparisons that attempt to account for component skills in the performance of executive functions vary greatly. Some studies have used simple subtraction formulas, whereas others have used ratios, structural equation models (Uttl & Graf, 1997), or analysis of variance (Cohn et al., 1984). Verhaeghen and De Meersman (1998) made a strong case for using regression analyses, as we did in the current study, by entering baseline data first and then, in the next step, entering data from the executive function condition, such as from the interference condition in the Stroop test. They argued that this method is superior in estimating the additional variance explained by the executive function performance over and above the baseline condition.

In the current study, sequence loss errors on the California Trail Making Test were associated with older age. Any generalization from these findings must be made with caution, however, as few errors were committed in general in this study. Nevertheless, error scores enhance understanding of performance and are particularly useful in making age comparisons because they are less compromised by the general slowing factor. The present findings argue that age effects in certain types of executive skills are small but real and are revealed most accurately when factoring out component parts. Possible differences between various types of executive functions require further study.
