Thirst, the sensation associated with the desire to ingest water, provokes drinking—a particularly well-defined and extensively studied goal-directed behavior. During dehydration there are coordinate autonomic and endocrine responses attendant with drinking behavior, all of which are aimed at returning the osmolality and volume of the extracellular fluid to within preset limits. Thirst can be stimulated by two separate physiological mechanisms: cellular dehydration, where water is drawn out of the intracellular compartment by an increasing osmolality differential between the extracellular and intracellular compartments, and extracellular dehydration, where isotonic fluid is lost exclusively from the extracellular compartment (see Grossman, 1990, for review). Cellular dehydration is characterized by increasing plasma osmolality, whereas extracellular dehydration is accompanied by increased hematocrit and plasma protein concentration. Although the stimulus usually associated with thirst—prolonged water deprivation—provokes both types of dehydration in the rat, the ensuing drinking behavior is thought to arise primarily from cellular dehydration (Ramsey, Rolls, & Wood, 1977).

To direct and coordinate the homeostatic response to dehydration, the forebrain contains an interactive network of cell groups (see Loewy, 1990, for review). Prominent within this network is the paraventricular nucleus of the hypothalamus (PVH), which contains discrete functional compartments subserving the behavioral, neuroendocrine, and autonomic aspects of the response (Swanson & Sawchenko, 1983). Further contributions to all aspects of homeostasis are made by a series of forebrain cell groups that include the supraoptic nucleus (SO), lateral hypothalamic area (LHA), bed nuclei of the stria terminalis (BST), and the central nucleus of the amygdala (CEA). Neurons in all of these cell groups synthesize an array of neuropeptides central to their signaling functions. These peptides are released either by neurosecretion at vascular synapses or by conventional neurotransmission at central cellular synapses. Stimulus-specific alterations to the amounts of neuropeptides synthesized for release by these two mechanisms would support the hypothesis that the appropriate response by the animal to distinct and specific stimuli is effected by modifying the chemically coded signals contained within neurons. It is this hypothesis that we address here by measuring how peptidergic messenger RNA (mRNA) levels are influenced in different forebrain regions by a physiologically well-characterized dehydration stimulus.

During dehydration, the peptidergic component of the neuroendocrine response is best exemplified by vasopressin. Increases in plasma osmolality or decreases in extracellular fluid volume trigger robust increases in vasopressin secretion from the terminals of magnocellular neurosecretory neurons in the posterior pituitary (Share, 1974; Verney, 1947; Wade, Keil, & Ramsey, 1983), which are accompanied by increased gene expression and mRNA levels (Lightman & Young, 1987; Majzoub, Rich, van Boom, & Habener, 1983; Sherman, McKelvey, & Watson, 1986). Increased plasma vasopressin then effects increased water absorption by the kidney tubules.

With regard to the behavioral and autonomic components, the function of centrally acting peptides is less clear, because the detailed organization of the underlying circuitry is not well understood. With a view to clarifying the role of centrally acting neuropeptides during the response to dehydration, we have already reported that in the lateral part of the central nucleus of the amygdala (CEAl) and the LHA, overnight ingestion of hypertonic saline differentially affects the cellular content of the mRNAs encoding two neuropeptides: corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) and neurotensin (Watts, 1992b). CRH has been associated functionally with a number of behavioral and physiological processes altered during dehydration (Fisher, 1989; Koob et al., 1993; Tarjan, Denton, & Weisinger, 1991). Furthermore, these two neuropeptides are frequently found both individually and colocalized in forebrain neurons (Moga & Gray, 1985; Moga, Saper, & Gray, 1989; Moga, Saper, & Gray, 1990; Sawchenko, Swanson, & Vale, 1984; Shimada et al., 1989). Collectively, these observations are consistent with the hypothesis that alterations in neuropeptide mRNAs form part of the coordinate response to alterations in fluid homeostasis and that these specific alterations in the synthesis of neuropeptides contribute to the overall architecture of the behavior and physiology required for fluid homeostasis.

In the present study, we have tested this hypothesis by investigating the responses of CRH and neurotensin/neuromedin N (NT/N) mRNAs throughout the forebrain as rats begin and then continue to drink hypertonic saline over a 5-day period. The study uses semiquantitative in situ hybridization and has two aims. The first aim is to identify those forebrain regions that exhibit altered levels of these mRNAs and to determine the temporal aspect of their response with a view to characterizing the extent of neuropeptide involvement in the homeostatic response. Second, by correlating these specific mRNA changes and their temporal profile with the alterations in body weight, fluid intake, plasma osmolality, and hematocrit arising from continued ingestion of hypertonic saline, we aim to identify the physiological stimuli responsible for driving the changes in peptide mRNAs.

Some aspects of this study have previously been reported in abstract form (Kelly & Watts, 1993).

Method

Subjects

Four groups of 5 male Sprague-Dawley rats (280–320 g of body weight at the beginning of the experiment) were maintained on a 12-hr light–dark cycle (lights on at 7 a.m.) with unrestricted access to water and rat chow. Seven days before the change to hypertonic saline, all rats were individually caged. Beginning at 8 a.m. 2 days before the change to hypertonic saline and continuing for the remainder of the experiment, all rats were weighed and the volume of fluid drunk in the previous 24 hr was measured. Beginning at 12 noon on Day 0, 3 groups of rats were offered only 2.5% saline (wt/vol) to drink for 1, 3, or 5 days. In the remaining group, rats were allowed unrestricted access to drinking water.

At the appropriate time, all rats were anesthetized with tribromoethanol and perfused through the ascending aorta with 4% paraformaldehyde solution in 0.1 M borate buffer at pH 9.5. Brains were frozen in powdered dry ice and either sectioned immediately or stored at −70 °C until sectioned at a later date. Eight consecutive series of 15-μm-thick coronal sections were cut through the forebrain to sample the BST, hypothalamus, and amygdala and were saved in ice-cold potassium phosphate buffered saline containing 0.25% paraformaldehyde. Sections were mounted the same day on poly-l-lysine coated gelatin-subbed slides, vacuum desiccated overnight, and then stored at −70 °C in airtight containers containing silica-gel desiccant until hybridization at a later date. Serial sections were saved for thionin staining.

A single 1–1.5 ml blood sample was taken into a heparinized syringe from the external jugular vein immediately before the perfusion cannula was introduced into the heart. After removing blood to measure hematocrit, we centrifuged the samples at 4 °C and separated and stored the plasma at −20 °C until measurement of plasma osmolality using an Advanced Instruments freezing point osmometer (Model 3W2, Needham Heights, MA). The plasma osmolalities of all samples were measured in a single assay run.

In Situ Hybridization

In situ hybridization was performed according to the protocol originally described by Cox, DeLeon, Angerer, and Angerer (1984) and modified in this laboratory by Watts and Sanchez-Watts (1995a). Sections were hybridized with 35 S-uridine 5`[α-thio]triphosphate-labeled complementary RNA (cRNA) probes synthesized from complementary DNA (cDNA) fragments coding for part of the mRNAs encoding prepro(pp)corticotropin-releasing hormone (396 bp) or pp-neurotensin/neuromedin N (336 bp) using the Promega Gemini kit (Promega Inc., Madison, WI) and the appropriate RNA polymerase (Watts & Sanchez-Watts, 1995a). Details of the probes and appropriate controls have been given in previous publications (Watts, 1992a; Watts & Sanchez-Watts, 1995a; Watts & Swanson, 1989).

Briefly, sections were prehybridized, hybridized for 18–20 hr at 60 °C using a probe concentration of either 5 × 106 or 107 cpm/ml of hybridization solution, treated posthybridization with RNase and washes of decreasing concentrations of saline sodium citrate (SSC) from 4× to 0.1× at 65 °C, and dehydrated in alcohols. Sections were exposed to Cronex Microvision X-ray film (Dupont, Wilmington, DE) for varying periods (between 6 and 18 days for CRH mRNA hybridization and 3 and 6 days for NT/N mRNA) and then were dipped in nuclear track emulsion (Kodak NTB-2 New Haven, CT; diluted 1:1 with distilled water), exposed for 3–18 days, and developed and counterstained with thionin.

Semi-Quantitation

Both the mean gray level (MGL) of the hybridization signal and the area occupied by labeled cells in anatomically selected areas were measured as previously described (Watts & Sanchez-Watts, 1995a). The MGL was calculated within the anatomically selected area on a signal segmented at 3 SD above the mean background value. Background measurements were taken from adjacent areas showing no labeling. Segmentation of the image at 3 SD above the mean background value excluded >98% of background pixels. The mean background value was subtracted to give the MGL value used in subsequent calculations. For the purposes of comparison, data were normalized to the mean value found in euhydrated controls. We have assumed that the MGL is representative of the amount of hybridized mRNA present per cell, whereas the area occupied by the signal after segmenting the image at 3 SD is representative of the number of cells containing the mRNA. All photomicrographs were obtained using computer-linked video equipment previously described (Watts & Sanchez-Watts, 1995a, 1995b). Images were processed (landmark enhancement, minor blemish removal, and contrast matching) and printed using Adobe Photoshop (Version 2.5.1; Adobe Systems Inc., Mountain View, CA) and Adobe Illustrator (Version 5.0) software. The data content of the photomicrographs was not altered in any way. For clarity, dark-field photomicrographs were inverted so that image white becomes printed black. The anatomical nomenclature of Swanson (1992) has been followed throughout. The following studies were used to determine nuclear organization: for the PVH, Swanson & Simmons (1989), and Watts (1992a); for the BST, Ju & Swanson (1989); and for the CEA, McDonald (1982) and Swanson (1992).

Statistical Analyses

Analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by Fisher's least squares difference test (differences in hematocrit, osmolality, body weight, or fluid consumed) or Dunnett's one-tailed test (to compare data from rats drinking hypertonic saline with euhydrated controls') were used to evaluate statistical differences between groups. All statistical analyses were performed using Microsoft Excel (Version 4.0) or Systat (Version 5.2) software for the Macintosh computer.

Results

Effects of Saline Ingestion on Fluid Intake, Body Weight, Plasma Osmolality, and Hematocrit

Fluid Intake

When expressed as group means, rats given access to water throughout the experiment showed no significant difference in the volume of fluid ingested every 24 hr throughout the experiment (see Figure 1A). The rats that were allowed to drink 2.5% saline only, showed no significant difference from controls in the mean volume of fluid ingested at any stage of the experiment (see Figure 1A). However, the extremely large standard errors of the mean, evident from Day 2 to Day 4 of saline ingestion, does reflect a very large range of values. When expressed as individual cumulative fluid intakes (see Figure 1B), the wide range in drinking behavior became apparent; some rats drank very large volumes of hypertonic saline, others drank equivalent fluid volumes as controls, and others greatly reduced their fluid intake.

Body Weight

Rats drinking only water throughout the experiment increased their body weight by approximately 1.8% every day (see Figure 1C), whereas rats drinking 2.5% saline lost approximately 7% of their body weight each day for the first 3 days. This loss then stabilized, and there was no significant difference in the changes in body weight from Day 3 to 5 (see Figure 1C).

Plasma Osmolality and Hematocrit

There was no significant difference between the plasma osmolalities and hematocrits in control and experimental rats Day 1 after drinking hypertonic saline (see Figure 1D); but by Day 3, both parameters were significantly increased above controls. By Day 5, these were approximately 6.5% (osmolality) and 18% (hematocrit) above controls. The increased hematocrit represented a 15.4% reduction in plasma volume (Stricker, 1968).

Effects of Saline Ingestion on Peptide mRNA Levels in the Bed Nuclei of the Stria Terminalis and Amygdala

Both CRH and NT/N mRNAs were found in the oval subdivision of the BST (BSTov) and the CEAl, whereas only CRH mRNA was seen in the fusiform subdivision of the BST (BSTfu). The effects of hypertonic saline ingestion were measured in both regions.

CRH mRNA

Bed nuclei of the stria terminals—oval and fusiform nuclei (see Table 1, Figure 2)

Both the number of cells detected (area) and the amount of hybridized CRH mRNA (MGL) decreased significantly in the BSTov after overnight ingestion of hypertonic saline, while the number of detectable cells in the BSTfu was significantly increased. By Day 3, both parameters in both BST regions were significantly different from euhydrated rats. However, by Day 5 in the BSTov, CRH mRNA values were no longer significantly lower than those in euhydrated controls, while the number of detectable cells in the BSTfu was still significantly increased.

Central nucleus of the amygdala (see Table 1, Figure 3)

The manner in which CRH mRNA in the CEAl responded to hypertonic saline ingestion was identical to that in the BSTov—levels were reduced by Days 1 and 3 but had returned to control values by Day 5.

NT/N mRNA

Bed nuclei of the stria terminalis—oval nucleus (see Table 1)

Significant amounts of NT/N mRNA were detected in the BSTov of control rats. However, unlike CRH mRNA in the BSTov and NT/N in the CEA, ingestion of hypertonic saline had no effect on the numbers of cells or the signal intensity. No NT/N was seen in the BSTfu.

Central nucleus of the amygdala (see Table 1)

The profile of NT/N mRNA responses in the CEAl was similar to CRH mRNA. Levels were significantly reduced by Day 3 of ingestion and were tending to return to control values by Day 5.

Effects of Saline Ingestion on Peptide mRNA Levels in the Hypothalamus

CRH mRNA

Paraventricular and supraoptic nuclei (see Table 2, Figure 4)

In the PVH, saline ingestion significantly reduced the levels of CRH mRNA in the dorsal part of the medial parvicellular subdivision (PVHmpd). The hybridization signal was unchanged 1 day after saline ingestion but was approximately 45% lower than euhydrated controls by 3 and 5 days. CRH mRNA was detectable in the SO and in magnocellular parts of the PVH after overnight saline ingestion, and continued to be detectable as the stimulus was maintained.

Periventricular nucleus (see Table 2)

Few cells in the periventricular nucleus (PV) expressed CRH mRNA in control rats or overnight saline rats. However, 3 and 5 days after drinking hypertonic saline, cells with hybridized CRH mRNA were seen in the PV between the caudal parts of the PVH and the arcuate nucleus (ARH).

Lateral hypothalamic area (see Table 3, Figure 5)

Very few CRH mRNA-containing cells were seen in any region of the LHA of control rats. After overnight saline ingestion, however, appreciable numbers of cells contained CRH mRNA in the perifornical region of the LHA. These cells were distributed primarily in a cluster located dorsolateral to the fornix approximately between the most caudal levels of the PVH and the rostral part of the dorsomedial nucleus of the hypothalamus (Levels 27–28 in Swanson, 1992). Fewer CRH mRNA-containing cells were seen ventral to the fornix, and very few medial to the fornix (see also Watts, 1992b). As the stimulus was maintained, labeled cells in this region became more numerous and contained increasing amounts of mRNA.

Retrochiasmatic area (see Table 3, Figure 5)

Levels of CRH mRNA were measured cumulatively in the retrochiasmatic area (RCH) in sections from the caudal aspect of the suprachiasmatic nucleus to the rostral tip of the arcuate nucleus (Levels 24–26 in Swanson, 1992). Cells containing CRH mRNA in the RCH were not seen in control rats but, like those in the LHA, appeared after overnight saline ingestion; and their numbers and labeling intensity continued to increase as long as the stimulus was maintained.

NT/N mRNA

Arcuate nucleus (see Table 2, Figure 6)

Levels of NT/N mRNA were measured cumulatively in ARH neurons in sections from the rostral tip to mid levels of the ARH (Levels 26–29 in Swanson, 1992). Although overnight saline ingestion did not alter NT/N mRNA levels, these levels were significantly reduced by Days 3 and 5.

Periventricular nucleus (see Table 2)

In controls or rats drinking hypertonic saline overnight, very few cells were visible in the PV that expressed NT/N mRNA. However, by Days 3 and 5 of rats drinking hypertonic saline, substantial numbers of cells with hybridized NT/N mRNA could be seen in the PV at the same rostrocaudal levels as those containing CRH mRNA.

Lateral hypothalamic area (see Table 3)

Substantial numbers of cells contained NT/N mRNA in the LHA of control rats. Compared with cells containing CRH mRNA, those cells with NT/N mRNA were more widespread. They were located in the same regions as CRH mRNA-containing cells, but their numbers were always much greater; cells were also seen medial to the fornix (see also Watts, 1992b). As early as 24 h after saline ingestion, the amount of NT/N mRNA in these cells was significantly increased; and as the stimulus was maintained, it increased further. However unlike those containing CRH mRNA, the numbers of cells containing NT/N mRNA remained stable.

Retrochiasmatic area (see Table 3, Figure 5)

Levels of NT/N mRNA were measured in the same region as CRH mRNA (see earlier discussion). Like those in the LHA, cells containing NT/N mRNA in the RCH were seen in control rats and did not change after overnight saline ingestion. Both the number and the levels of NT/N mRNA were significantly increased after 3 and 5 days of saline ingestion.

Discussion

Our data demonstrate that the response pattern of CRH and NT/N mRNAs in the forebrain to prolonged hypertonic saline ingestion is both complex and extensive. The response is cell-specific in that either gene when expressed in different cell groups behaves divergently in the face of the same stimulus, emphasizing the presence of cell-specific regulatory mechanisms available to control mRNA levels and peptide translation (see also Watts & Sanchez-Watts, 1995a).

Before discussing the implications of our findings, it is useful to consider how prolonged ingestion of hypertonic saline affects the fluid compartments of the body and how the rat might compensate for these changes. On the 1st night of drinking saline, the elevated osmolality of the extracellular fluid (ECF) compartment draws water from the intracellular fluid (ICF) compartment and expands the ECF. The initial response of the rat to this purely cellular dehydrating stimulus is to excrete the excess sodium and water by increased renal activity and urination; in our data, this is perhaps correlated with the significant loss of body weight seen on Day 1 (see Figure 1 C). By the following morning, homeostatic mechanisms have been able to maintain hematocrit and plasma osmolality at values no different from control rats (see also Watts, 1992a). Mechanisms maintaining drinking behavior clearly still operate in all rats during this period, because there is no significant difference in the volume of fluid consumed between rats drinking hypertonic saline and those drinking water.

By the 3rd morning, persistent hypertonic saline ingestion and the accompanying sodium load placed on the ECF make it increasingly difficult for the rat to regulate ECF osmolality effectively by a continued loss of water from the ICF. As a consequence, the reduction in body weight is restrained from Day 3 onwards, but now the drinking behavior of rats ranges from hyperdipsia to adipsia (see Figure 1 B). But regardless of the choice the rat makes, the overall dehydration is still exacerbated. When compared with euhydrated rats or rats drinking hypertonic saline for only 1 night, all rats having access to hypertonic saline for more than 3 days have a contracted ECF volume, which is evident as significant increases in hematocrit, and all show elevated plasma osmolality (see Figure 1 D).

Our data thus reveal two distinct phases in the physiological and behavioral response to hypertonic saline ingestion. The first phase occurs during the 1st night and is characterized by the consumption of normal volumes of fluid and the exclusive presence of cellular dehydration, which is accompanied by a successful homeostatic response. The second phase is apparent by the 3rd morning and is characterized by a persistent but relatively stable degree of cellular dehydration, which is now accompanied by gradually increasing extracellular dehydration. In our model, this second phase is maintained as long as the rat continues to have access only to hypertonic saline.

Considering these phases, we conclude that the changes in CRH and NT/N mRNA levels in the SO, BSTov, BSTfu, LHA, and CEAl after overnight saline ingestion must occur solely as a consequence of cellular dehydration (see also Watts, 1992a). This conclusion is supported by the fact that in rats having continuous access to water, extracellular dehydration maintained for 20 hr by subcutaneous injections of colloidal polyethylene glycol does not increase CRH and NT/N mRNA levels in the LHA or CRH mRNA levels in the RCH and SO (Sanchez-Watts, Tanimura, & Watts, 1993; Watts Sanchez-Watts unpublished observations). Furthermore, although the same treatment increases CRH, NT/N, and proenkephalin mRNAs in the PVHmpd (Watts & Sanchez-Watts, 1995a), we have never seen such changes after ingestion of hypertonic saline (present study; Watts, 1992a; Watts Kelly Sanchez-Watts unpublished observations). Collectively these observations suggest that separate and distinct patterns of neuropeptide gene expression are associated with cellular and extracellular dehydration.

With regard to afferent mechanisms, the area of the LHA that contains CRH and NT/N neurons receives a direct projection from cells in the median preoptic nucleus and subfornical organ (Miselis, 1981; Swanson & Lind, 1986; Kelly & Watts, 1993, 1994), regions thought to contain sensory elements critical for the behavioral and endocrine responses to elevated plasma osmolality and angiotensin II (A-II) concentrations (Grossman, 1990). We have recently shown in a double-labeling experiment that a significant proportion of neurons in the median preoptic nucleus and subfornical organ that project to the LHA also show elevated c-fos mRNA levels in response to increased plasma osmolality (Kelly & Watts, 1994). Furthermore, in rats drinking hypertonic saline, a unilateral section of rostral afferents into the LHA specifically blocks increased CRH and NT/N mRNA responses on the lesioned side of the LHA (Kelly & Watts, 1994). Collectively, these data suggest that the LHA receives a direct projection from osmosensitive cell groups rostral to the knife cut and that these connections are critical for increasing mRNA levels during cellular dehydration. Comparable results were obtained by Kovács and Sawchenko (1993) regarding the increases in CRH mRNA in hypothalamic magnocellular neurosecretory neurons after hypertonic saline ingestion.

Unlike the LHA, the BST and CEAl do not appear to receive a direct projection from central osmoreceptors, and alternative mechanisms must operate. Both the BSTov and CEAl receive strong projections from those parts of parabrachial nucleus that, because of their pattern of afferents from the nucleus of the solitary tract, are thought to be concerned with processing visceral and chemosensory information (Alden, Besson, & Bernard, 1994; Bernard, Alden, & Besson, 1993). This route could conceivably operate to regulate gene expression in forebrain regions by communicating visceral sensory information from, for example, the area postrema (Cunningham, Miselis, & Sawchenko, 1994; Kadachian & Pickel, 1993; Shapiro & Miselis, 1985; van der Kooy & Koda, 1983), peripheral osmoreceptors (King & Baertschi, 1991), and the kidney (Stella & Zanchetti, 1991).

A number of workers have noted anatomical similarities between the CEAl and the BSTov (see Alheid, de Olmos, & Beltramino, 1995, for review). Both cell groups are cytoarchitectonically similar and show similar patterns of peptide colocalization (Shimada et al., 1989). However, other observations point to important differences in the mechanisms of peptide gene regulation and in physiological function. We have recently shown that although both cell groups express CRH and NT/N mRNAs, they differ in their sensitivity to circulating corticosterone (Watts & Sanchez-Watts, 1995b). Furthermore, the CEA and BST appear to play different roles with regard to the secretion of hormones (Gray et al., 1993; van der Kar, Piechowski, Rittenhouse, & Gray, 1991). Our observations here show that CRH and NT/N mRNAs in CEAl neurons are more sensitive to the ingestion of hypertonic saline than when expressed in the BSTov. This is reminiscent of their response to corticosterone and further emphasizes the differences between these two cell groups.

Increased plasma corticosterone concentrations feature in the coordinate endocrine response seen during the first few days of drinking hypertonic saline or of water deprivation (Aguilera, Lightman, & Kiss, 1993; Watts, 1992b) and after extracellular dehydration (Stricker, Vagnucci, McDonald, & Leenan, 1979). A number of observations support the hypothesis that corticosterone participates in regulating gene expression in some but not all cell groups during dehydration and that as hypertonic saline ingestion continues, the degree and the manner of this action depend on the cell type targeted. Thus, corticosterone may have no effect, or it may either amplify or counteract the actions of cellular dehydration. First, when plasma corticosterone is persistently elevated in euhydrated rats within the limits of the diurnal range, it can significantly alter CRH and NT/N mRNA levels in many of the regions identified in the present study (Makino, Gold, & Schulkin, 1994a, 1994b; Swanson & Simmons, 1989; Watts & Sanchez-Watts, 1995b). Second, the areas where CRH and NT/N mRNA levels remain modified for the whole dehydration period—the SO, RCH, LHA, and BSTfu—are ones that are rather insensitive to the effects of corticosterone (Swanson & Simmons, 1989; Watts & Sanchez-Watts, 1995b), suggesting that osmosensitive but corticosterone-independent mechanisms act rapidly here to modify gene expression (Kovács & Sawchenko, 1993). This conclusion is supported by the fact that adrenalectomy has no effect on increased CRH mRNA in the SO after water deprivation (Watts & Sanchez-Watts, 1995c). Finally, dehydration-associated alterations to mRNA levels in the ARH (NT/N), PVHmpd (CRH), and PV (NT/N) are all in the same direction as those seen after elevating plasma corticosterone in euhydrated rats and are not seen for at least 3 days despite the presence of cellular dehydration from Day 1. Furthermore, because adrenalectomy abolishes the decrease in CRH mRNA seen in the PVHmpd after water deprivation, a strong argument can be made that a corticosterone-dependent mechanism participates in regulating CRH gene expression during dehydration (Watts & Sanchez-Watts, 1995c). However, we should also consider that ablation of the vascular organ of the lamina terminalis (OVLT) and adjacent region inhibits the decrease in CRH mRNA in the PVHmpd after salt ingestion (Kovács & Sawchenko, 1993), clearly showing that corticosterone is not the only factor operating here.

Our data show that extracellular dehydration becomes a prominent feature from the 3rd day and that the added effects of this stimulus may be important in directing the biphasic responses in the CEAl and BSTov. One possibility is that the increased corticosterone secretion seen during this phase (Watts, 1992a) contributes to returning mRNAs to control levels in these two areas. Certainly in euhydrated rats, corticosterone acts to change mRNAs in a direction opposite to the overnight effects of drinking hypertonic saline (Watts & Sanchez-Watts, 1995b). A second possibility is that increased plasma A-II concentrations arising from extracellular dehydration may affect neuropeptide mRNAs. Supporting this are the observations that intraventricular or intravenous injections of A-II increase c-fos protein in the BST and CEAl (Herbert, Forsling, Howes, Stacey, & Shiers, 1992; McKinley, Badoer, & Oldfield, 1992; Rowland et al., 1994), and this conceivably could alter neuropeptide function.

We postulate that alterations in neuropeptide mRNA levels represent specific adaptations in neuronal signaling mechanisms that contribute to the overall homeostatic response to hypertonic saline ingestion. Many reports have documented the effects of dehydration on neuropeptide gene expression in neurosecretory neurons in the PVH and SO. For example, hypertonic saline ingestion will decrease CRH mRNA levels in the PVHmpd (Lightman & Young, 1987; Watts, 1992a; Young, 1986), but will increase them in oxytocinergic magnocellular neurosecretory neurons (Dohanics, Kovács, & Makara, 1990; Kovács & Sawchenko, 1993; Watts, 1992a; Young, 1986). We have previously reported that timing of these two events is different (Watts, 1992a), and the data here confirm those observations; CRH mRNA in the SO increases immediately, while 3 days of hypertonic saline ingestion are required for a significant effect in the PVHmpd. We have also observed here that hypertonic saline ingestion significantly decreases NT/N mRNA levels in ventrolateral ARH neurons but increases levels in the periventricular PV. Many neurons in these two nuclei innervate the median eminence and regulate anterior pituitary function (Merchenthaler, 1991; Weigand & Price, 1980). Alexander (1993) has identified a population of NT/N mRNA-containing neurons in dorsomedial part of the ARH that, in the female rat, are estrogen-sensitive and the source of most of the neurotensin in the median eminence (Alexander, 1993; Merchenthaler & Lennard, 1991). In the present study, the dehydration-sensitive neurons are located in the ventrolateral part of the ARH and, as such, appear to be distinct from the estrogen-sensitive neurons identified by Alexander (1993). However, they are located in the same part of the ARH that contains corticosterone-sensitive NT/N mRNA-containing neurons (Watts & Sanchez-Watts, 1993). In the absence of double-labeling data, we unfortunately cannot comment on whether they are neurosecretory, or whether they may also contain tyrosine hydroxylase as do many other neurotensin-containing neurons in the ARH (Hökfelt, Everitt, Theodorsson-Norheim, & Goldstein, 1984).

With regard to behavioral and autonomic components, immunocytochemical studies show that many CRH-and NT-containing cells in the LHA project to the lateral parabrachial nucleus (Moga, Saper, & Gray, 1990), a nucleus that as part of the parabrachial nuclear complex helps regulate a range of behaviors and autonomic actions including ingestive behavior and cardiovascular and gustatory functions (Alden et al., 1994; Bernard et al., 1993; Fulwiler & Saper, 1984). In rats drinking hypertonic saline, we have recently shown with double-labeled in situ hybridization and retrograde tract-tracing that some CRH and NT/N mRNA-containing neurons in the LHA contribute to this projection (Kelly & Watts, 1993). Thus, altering the peptide content within specific efferents of the LHA during cellular dehydration might allow these neurons to modulate the neuronal activity of the parabrachial nucleus. Similarly, CRH-and NT-containing neurons in the CEAl (Sakanaka, Shibasaki, & Lederis, 1986; Moga & Gray, 1985) and BSTov (Moga et al., 1989) also project to the lateral parabrachial nucleus. Modifying the peptidergic content of these projections, may further modulate parabrachial function during dehydration. Because the LHA projects to somewhat different parts of the parabrachial nucleus than do the BST and CEA (Moga, Herbert, et al., 1990), the possibility arises that each of these forebrain regions modifies a separate aspect of parabrachial function. Finally, dehydration-induced modifications to neuropeptide mRNA levels in the LHA are not limited to CRH and NT/N. Changes in preprotachykinin A (Larsen, Jessop, Lightman, & Chowdrey, 1993) and melanin-concentrating hormone (Presse & Nahon, 1993) mRNAs have also been reported after hypertonic saline ingestion, thus expanding the range of possible functions modified after dehydration.

In summary, we have shown that ingestion of hypertonic saline over a 5-day period first stimulates cellular dehydration with the addition of extracellular dehydration as ingestion continues beyond 3 days. Associated with these two stimuli are anatomically and temporally specific alterations to the levels of CRH and NT/N mRNAs in a variety of limbic forebrain neurons associated with the neuroendocrine, behavioral, and autonomic responses to dehydration. We postulate that these stimulus-associated alterations in mRNA content are allied to the adaptations in neuronal signaling required by the rat to compensate for the imposed physiological stressors.
