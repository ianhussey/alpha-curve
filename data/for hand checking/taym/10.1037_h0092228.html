Review By: Harriette Kaley
Brooklyn Colege—City Uninversity of New York

Review of: Other Times, Other Realities: Toward a Theory of Psychoanalytic Treatment
By: Arthur H. Modell, Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1990. 180 pp

Because the psychoanalyst-author of this book is intensely aware of the existence of paradox in therapy, it is fitting that his book itself achieves something of a paradox: it offers a theoretical perspective on treatment that is both substantially novel and creative, while being integrative and synthesizing.

Drawing on recent research and theoretical developments in the neurobiology of memory, and scanning the psychoanalytic literature in depth, especially in the areas of transference, resistance, and interpretation, Modell attempts to reconsider the treatment process in light of contemporary biological knowledge. He then reformulates classical concepts in terms compatible with their current versions and with that biological knowledge. In the end, he has forged a reinterpretation of much of what we do in therapy and analysis that is clinically astute, stimulating, intellectually demanding and, most of the time, intellectually satisfying.

The avowed purpose of the book is to provide "a theory that explains how psychoanalysis works" (p. 1). That is, Modell pursues a theory of treatment that is highly psychoanalytic in its basic assumptions, and he does so with special attention to the multiple "levels of reality" (p. 4) and other paradoxical aspects of the treatment process.

In the development of his formulations, the author gives special attention to Freud’s concept of Nachträglichkeit. Modell believes the term to have been erroneously translated by Strachey as "deferred action" and in his book Modell treats the term as better translated by something like "retranscription" of memory. One of the most interesting theoretical aspects of the book is the way that Modell then draws on the work of the eminent neurobiologist Gerald Edelman to show how neural processes, according to current understanding, proceed in ways highly compatible with the sense of Nachträglichkeit. As Modell employs it, Edelman’s theory, very simply put, proposes that memory "is a dynamic reconstruction … established by means of categories" (p. 60, emphasis added). This reconstruction occurs in constant engagement with motoric activity, including affect expression, and this creates in therapy the opportunity to confirm or disconfirm previous categorical memories. The picture is quite different from that of the more static neurology of Freud’s day, which permitted Freud to describe the memories under repression in the unconscious as "timeless" (p. 82). The net result is a shift in the theoretical underpinnings of basic psychoanalytic concepts dramatic enough to call to mind Kuhn’s discussion of the effects of a radical shift in scientific paradigm.

In light of this reconceptualization of memory, Modell reformulates, within his own distinctive object—relational framework, many of the fundamental issues of treatment. He discusses such topics as the place of play and illusion in treatment, the dimensions of our experience of time, the variations possible in a patient’s subjective experience of an interpretation, and the Winnicottian variations in the ways that a patient can "use" a therapist. Particularly subtle are his discussions of the paradoxical nature of the psychoanalytic setting itself as both the "dependent/containing" frame which functions as a holding environment at the same time as it creates the more problematic "iconic/projective transference."

Modell presents his ideas as applicable to both therapy and analysis. For himself, he acknowledges (p. 154, n. 1), the differences between therapy and analysis are elusive. Nevertheless, this book assumes considerable knowledge of psychoanalysis and its various controversies, especially within the Freudian camps. Further, since it cites relatively few clinical examples (though those few are very good at illuminating the abstract concepts being discussed), it is probably also helpful to the reader to have considerable clinical experience in intensive psychotherapy or psychoanalysis. For such readers, who can be patient with a repetitious quality in the closing chapters of the book, the work provides a challenging opportunity to see a psychoanalytic scholar approach time-honored questions in the light of today.
