Review By: Thomas F. McGee
Mercy Hospital and Medical Center, San Diego, Calif.

Review of: ETHICS AND LAW IN MENTAL HEALTH ADMINISTRATION
By: Walter E. BartonGail M. Barton, . . 1984. 378 pp. $35.00. International Universities Press, New York.

In this admirable work, the Bartons exemplify and examine a wide array of ethical and legal issues currently confronting the mental health professions. Ostensibly, their work is presented with the mental health administrator in mind. It becomes readily apparent, however, that it is a vital book for all mental health professionals, particularly if one agrees with a viewpoint central to their work. That is, rather than serving as abstract codes and distant regulations, ethical and legal issues as they relate to mental health have great potential to intertwine with and profoundly influence one's daily professional activities. Thus, they espouse the viewpoint that ethics and law represent dynamic rather than static concepts which actively affect all aspects of mental health and increasingly determine where, when, and how mental health practice is conducted.

Drawing upon their earlier discussions in the area of ethics and law in psychiatry (Administration in Psychiatry, 1962) and mental health administration (Mental Health Administration: Principles and Practice, 1983), the Bartons carefully delineate the significant shifts that have occurred in the scope and focus of ethics and law in mental health during the past 25 years. In this process the societal, legal, and scientific back-ground for the rapid changes in ethics and law is examined, as well as the rapidly developing complexities of ethical and legal issues which have come inexorably to influence all aspects of contemporary mental health.

The Bartons' work is lucidly written and straightforward in its presentation. As a partial consequence it may appear deceptively simple in style and content; yet in a number of respects it probably requires considerable experience and maturity to master fully its complexities and import. The work is clearly organized, and consists of two major sections: “Ethics in Mental Health Administration,” and “Law in Mental Health Administration.” In addition, there are three appendices, two of which—on rights of patients and on informed consent and release of information from patient records—are of particular value.

The reader is provided with a fine sense of immediacy as the majority of the chapters begin with a case example, most of which appear quite typical to the day-to-day practice of mental health. Each case example contains both obvious and subtle interrelated ethical or legal issues from which the chapter develops. Each of the chapters concludes with a succinct summary and a brief set of questions based upon the chapter. While the summaries are cogent and useful, the sets of questions appear less so. Indeed, they impart an unnecessary textbook-like quality to the work.

In their effort to present a clear, concise compendium of ethical and legal issues confronting contemporary mental health, the Bartons have produced a work that attempts to be comprehensive in scope. As a consequence of the increasing vastness of the topic, a number of issues that deserve fuller treatment are dealt with much too briefly. For example, the subsection “Machines That Sustain Life” is contained within four pages, and the subsection “Electroconvulsive Therapy” is limited to a discussion of two pages. Similarly, the subsection “Prediction of Dangerousness” is limited to two pages, while the subsection “Child Abuse” is less than one page.

Despite the shortcomings noted above, the book eloquently forces one to recognize the greatly increased awareness of ethical and legal issues presently required by mental health professionals. The reader is confronted again and again with the view that a deep and consistent grasp of ethical and legal issues and their continual development is required if one is to approach effectiveness as a mental health administrator or practitioner. The brevity of some topics notwithstanding, the Bartons have presented a compelling overview of a difficult, complex, but most vital area.

I trust that an update of this work will be carried out every few years, and that it will be done with the clarity, thoughtfulness, and compassion that mark the present work. As a mental health administrator and a clinician for more than 20 years, I applaud the Bartons' efforts, and recommend their book to a wide audience of mental health administrators and professionals.
