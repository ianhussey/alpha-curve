Transitional Employment: The Swedish Experience

By: Marie Persson
Staff Worker at Fontanhuset, Malmo, Sweden

Acknowledgement: This article is taken from a presentation at the Sixth International Seminar on the Clubhouse Model, Greenville, South Carolina, 1991.

The Transitional Employment Program (TEP) has always been central to our vision of the clubhouse. When Fontanhuset began 6 years ago, we said that if we didn’t succeed in developing a TE program in five years, we didn’t have the right to be part of the clubhouse community. The 5 years have passed, and we have a TE program. Twenty-two members have completed placements in seven companies. An additional 23 members have done fill-ins and have had shorter term experiences on TE. In all, 45 members have had some kind of experience with TE.

When we began our clubhouse, we were told that TE was simply not possible in Sweden. In Sweden, we have a very well-developed social welfare system, maybe one of the best in the world. For example, everyone is guaranteed a minimum income every month, either from the health or social authorities, and we have a rent-subsidy program for those who need help with their housing costs. Health insurance is universal.

Besides all these guaranteed social benefits, there are laws that further protect the rights of employees. These laws have created an atmosphere that makes employers afraid of hiring new employees, and persons with a poor job history or very little work experience find it difficult to obtain jobs. Further, in the last few years, there has been a relatively high unemployment rate. These positive and negative aspects of Swedish society were thought to be obstacles too great to overcome. But we have proved in Malmo that it is possible. It is not an isolated case. In Denmark, Fountain House/Copenhagen has proved it is possible even in an EEC country. They have a much bigger TE program than Malmo does.

When I look back at the development of the TE program in Malmo, I really think that we made only one big mistake. We should have begun our TE program sooner. We always had TEP as a goal for our clubhouse, but in the beginning we let other matters take precedence and misplaced our energy.

Our first efforts were not very efficient. We began by telephoning various companies in the Malmo phone book. This proved to be inefficient—we interested a few employers; some even visited us, and we visited them. However, the return on our efforts was not equal to the time and energy that we spent.

We turned to our board of directors, which has a committee structure of members, staff, and board members. We aggressively pursued contacts they had. We created a 1-hour weekly TE meeting for all interested parties.

We raised many questions: What is TE? Who has a right to a TE placement? Why is it important to have a TE placement? What do I have to consider in applying for a job?

At this meeting, members could discuss their good and bad experiences on TE. They get support and feedback from others. This inspired and impressed other members, and now we have more members wanting a TE than we have placements.

I could not imagine working in a clubhouse without a TEP, or at least the very active desire to develop a program. It is the heart of the clubhouse, and the way out of the clubhouse as well. Without this connection with the wider world, we would end up as just another institutional setting, perhaps in more beautiful clothing, but an institution nonetheless. As we fight against stigma and the segregation of people with mental illness, TE offers us a marvelous vehicle to reach into society and show that people with serious mental illness can be productive citizens. Do not be afraid of TE and the hard work it takes, for it can only bring vitality and life into your clubhouse and give both members and staff the precious gift of belief in the future.
