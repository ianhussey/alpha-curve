In Parting… / EDITORIALS

By: Ellen L. Bassuk

It has been an honor to serve as Editor of the American Journal of Orthopsychiatry these last five years. I am leaving with mixed feelings: most of all, pride at the direction of the Journal and its high level of scholarship, but also relief that I will no longer be faced with the large Jiffy bags jam-packed with articles that have just been through review or revision. More than 1,500 such manuscripts have crossed my desk, and I've attempted to make helpful suggestions and had to reach difficult verdicts about their publication. Concordance between Journal reviewers has been the exception, rather than the rule; generally, they have brought their quite varied perspectives to the assessment of articles, and I have tried to reconcile their views and my own. Inevitably, it has not been possible to satisfy everyone, especially given our rejection rate of over 80%—regrettable, but not uncommon at peer-reviewed journals such as this. I would like especially to thank the Journal's Contributing Editors (named on the facing page); members of the Editorial Board; and Associate Editor Irene Shifren Levine and Research Editor John C. Buckner, for taking time out of their busy lives to help so diligently with this process.

When I began my term as Editor, I hoped to strengthen the voice of the Journal, particularly in response to the growing social problems that are undermining the fabric of our society and compromising the well-being of thousands of families and children. Despite our booming economy, these remain troubled times. The list of social ills seems little different today than it did five years ago—the inexorable growth of home-lessness; rampant and pervasive interpersonal violence; abuse of drugs; the invidious effects of poverty, racism, and sexism; the decay of inner-city neighborhoods; lack of adequate support for people with disabilities; and intolerance of people who are different. Many children are growing up in conditions of poverty that no longer allow escape. As Editor of the American Journal of Orthopsychiatry, I have tried to focus attention on these topics, in part through the development of special sections of articles that have included: welfare reform and the real lives of poor women; homelessness; issues relating to adults with severe mental illness (covered in separate sections on dual diagnoses, effects on the family, and evaluation of the Assertive Community Treatment program); education reform; issues relating to children and youth (sections on children at risk, violence among youth, and prevention research); mental health and the melting pot; and sexual orientation and family development. Developing, editing, and refining these theme sections required considerable work and persistence, especially on the part of Journal staff and guest editors. I want to thank them all.

In my role as Editor, I often wondered about the real impact of Journal articles on social issues as entrenched as those mentioned above. I believe (and hope) that we have had some real effect. The Journal has provided a multidisci-plinary forum for academicians, clinicians, and practitioners to express their views in a scholarly fashion. The written word, especially when presented in a prestigious, peer-reviewed journal, has the capacity to change attitudes, catalyze new ideas, and inform public policy debates. Although the researcher in me would like to find a way to measure the impact of a periodical such as ours, I take comfort in the fact that the Journal is continuing to thrive after 68 years of publication, has retained its unique mission, and is widely read and cited.

Much of the credit for its continued excellence belongs to the Journal staff—especially to Ernest Herman, Publications Director, and Joan Adler, Managing Editor. Even in these high-tech times, they have not relinquished their hands-on approach and have continued to help in every step of the editing process. Their skills and commitment are largely responsible for the high quality and success of the Journal. I want to thank them for their dedication to the issues, their high editorial standards, and their extensive support for the Editor's complex tasks. I will miss working with them.

In parting, I am tempted once again to give a clarion call for the concerned readers of the Journal to continue, in whatever ways they can, to fight the good fight. Community service must be part of our life's calling—we must keep on working to implement the changes that we care about. Many of the manuscripts submitted to the Journal have reflected this commitment.

I wish Carlos Sluzki the best as he ascends to the helm of this Journal's masthead. He is an excellent and worthy choice to carry on Ortho's mission of integrating social and clinical issues, within a context that values intellectual rigor and vibrant scholarship. With the support of Journal staff, 1 know that he will continue to lead the American Journal of Orthopsychiatry in directions that will make this world a better place for our children.
