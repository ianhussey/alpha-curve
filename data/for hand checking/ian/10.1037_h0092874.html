A Student’s Quandary: Historian to Archivist and Back Again

By: Jennifer L. Bazar
York University

Acknowledgement: The author would like to thank David Baker and the staff at AHAP for their invaluable support during her internship.

In the summer of 2009, I was given the opportunity to participate in the trial-run of a developing internship program at the Archives of the History of American Psychology (AHAP) in Akron, Ohio. The two month long internship provided an interesting opportunity to step beyond my typical student researcher role while getting a first-hand glimpse behind the scenes of an archive. I was assigned to process two collections: the first consisted of the papers of an individual psychologist and the second of an organization.

One of the biggest challenges during, and since, the internship was coming to the understanding that not all donated materials are or can be retained when a collection is processed. That is to say that, depending on the guidelines of the individual archive, certain items may not be preserved. I soon learned that AHAP, like other archives, has a list of items that are not typically kept as part of a collection. AHAP however is unlike many archives in that a psychologist-historian, director David Baker, comprised its list. Some examples from the list are things like envelopes, receipts, tickets (plane, train), and so forth However, the rule is not set in stone—in the former case, envelopes are kept with unsigned or undated letters if they provide the relevant information.

The idea of selecting what material should be kept within a collection was daunting for me both as a student and a researcher. How could I possibly know what someone studying the material would need? The first item I tentatively recycled was a photocopy of a draft of a paper. It was identical in every way to its original (i.e., no annotations had been added to the copy) yet I hesitated for quite some time. Did it have some “value” for the collection that I might be overlooking? But why keep a photocopy of an article to which the original is available? I finally put it in the recycle bin and yet it still raised the question - what are we losing when a collection is processed?

Archivists are bestowed with the role of predicting the future; they must anticipate research trends, questions, and needs. Information may be considered “of little value” today but may tomorrow be seen as a ’rich resource.’ A case in point from my own research experience: the historiography of asylums has changed over the years such that researchers are now focusing their attention not solely on administrative or governmental reports, but also seeking out sources such as patient records. And yet, some archives continue to dispose of these materials. The reality is that archives are limited both by their financial resources and physical space restrictions—they simply cannot keep everything. But what about that envelope, receipt, or plane ticket that provides that missing link to some historical mystery?

By the end of the summer, I had begun to see the task from the archivist’s perspective, feeling more confident in the decisions I was making about what to keep and what to throw away. However, upon returning to my role as a student and researcher of the history of psychology, the idea of archival loss continues to raise questions for me. I wonder if a more open dialogue between historians of psychology and archivists is needed? I don’t know what the answers are, but my summer at the archives has brought me to believe that we - psychologist-historians, historians of psychology, and archivists - must at least continue to explore the questions.
