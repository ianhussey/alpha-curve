Studies using the divided attention (DA) paradigm have shown that dividing participants' attention between the encoding of the information presented and performing a secondary task resulted in a clear detrimental effect on free recall, cued recall, and recognition memory performance relative to conditions where full attention is paid to encoding the items (e.g., Baddeley, Lewis, Eldridge, & Thomson, 1984; Craik, Govoni, Naveh-Benjamin, & Anderson, 1996; Fernandes & Moscovitch, 2000, 2002; Murdock, 1965; Naveh-Benjamin, Craik, Gavrilescu, & Anderson, 2000; Naveh-Benjamin, Craik, Guez, & Dori, 1998; Reinitz, Morrissey, & Demb, 1994). Divided attention at encoding was shown to have a similar effect on a variety of memory features, including memory for spatial location (Naveh-Benjamin, 1987), and memory for frequency of occurrence (Naveh-Benjamin & Jonides, 1986).

For example, Craik et al. (1996) showed that encoding single words and word pairs results in a reliable decrement to secondary task performance. Furthermore, manipulating emphasis by instructing participants to stress the memory task, the secondary task, or both tasks equally, has complementary effects on the two tasks: As attention is switched to the secondary task and away from the memory task, memory performance declines and secondary task performance improves. These results indicate that encoding processes require attention, and that the allocation of attention to encoding processes is under the participant's conscious control (see also Kellog, Cocklin, & Bourne, 1982; Szymanski & MacLeod, 1996).

Although the detrimental effects of DA at encoding on later memory performance are clear-cut, there is no consensus regarding the locus of the effects of DA at encoding, nor the processes that are hampered during encoding by the concurrent interfering activity. Several types of processes may be affected by a concurrent activity, mediating the effects of DA at encoding on later memory performance. These types include initial perceptual analysis of the stimulus, semantic processes involved in item-specific processing, and more strategic-effortful processes involved in the relational-elaborative processing of information.

One possibility is that the decrease in memory performance when attention is divided at encoding is related to a decrease in strategic-effortful processing (Hasher & Zacks, 1979). That is, while subjects are trying to perform the secondary task, they lack the resources to enable them to use strategic-effortful processes in encoding the information presented in the primary task. According to this view, those aspects of encoding that do not require strategic-effortful processes would be much less affected by the division of attention. This idea is intuitively appealing given its direct relation to the notion of limited attentional resources, which states that the fewer the resources available for encoding, the less participants can engage in strategic effortful encoding, resulting in poorer memory performance. In spite of its intuitive appeal, however, this idea has received mixed support. For example, manipulating the difficulty level of the secondary task at encoding (e.g., Anderson & Craik, 1974; Naveh-Benjamin, Craik, Perretta, & Tonev, 2000) has shown that as the difficulty of the concurrent task increases, subsequent memory performance declines. The most straightforward interpretation of these results is that the more difficult secondary task condition requires more attentional resources, leaving fewer resources available for strategic-effortful processing of the encoded words.

Note, however, that this interpretation of the results assumes that the fewer the attentional resources that are left for encoding, the lower the probability that strategic encoding processes will be used for the words. Therefore, the above results are not definitive, for they do not reflect the direct measurement or manipulation of underlying strategic-effortful processes. Other studies also providing indirect support for the claim that strategic processes are those affected by divided attention show that DA during encoding mostly reduced recollection-based, rather than familiarity-based, processing (e.g., Gardiner & Parkin, 1990; Jacoby & Kelley, 1992; Yonelinas, 2002).

Do we have any direct evidence that strategic-effortful processes are the ones affected by DA at encoding? In a series of studies conducted during the 1980s, Craik provided such evidence, showing that under DA at encoding, participants use shallower and less semantic processes to encode the information. In a typical study, Craik (1982) employed the orienting task procedure, where for each item presented, participants were asked a question relating either to the graphemic, phonemic, or semantic aspects of a word, with half of the words yielding a positive answer and the other half a negative answer. Participants performed the task paying either full attention to the words or while attending also to an auditory secondary task at the same time. Recall performance showed that the divided attention participants showed the greatest memory deficit under deeper levels of processing; this indicated that strategic-effortful semantic processes are the ones most affected under DA. Similar results, indicating a shallower level of processing under DA at encoding conditions, were reported by Craik and Byrd (1982) and Rabinowitz, Craik, and Ackerman (1982). In addition, a recent study conducted by Naveh-Benjamin, Craik, Gavrilescu, and Anderson (2000), supported the notion that DA at encoding changes the qualitative nature of encoding such that it becomes less semantic in nature. Finally, recent studies using neuroimaging techniques (e.g., Anderson et al., 2000; Fletcher et al., 1995) have indicated that DA at encoding reduces encoding-related brain activity in the left inferior prefrontal cortex, an area shown in other studies to be associated with deep strategic semantic processing (e.g., Kapur et al., 1994).

The above-mentioned studies provide supportive evidence, coinciding with our intuitions, that DA at encoding interrupts the deep-level strategic-effortful processes necessary for an adequate encoding of incoming information. However, the results of several recent studies complicate this picture. For example, recent studies conducted by Craik and Kester (2000) provide some counter results. In a typical study in this series, participants were presented with word pairs and asked to rate the elaborateness of the connection they could create for each word pair. This was done either under full attention or under DA where participants simultaneously performed an auditory digit-monitoring task. Results showed that subsequent performance on an unexpected cued recall task improved as the rated degree of elaboration increased, and that this happened for both full- and divided-attention conditions. However, in contrast to what is expected by the strategic effortful hypothesis, memory performance for the DA condition was considerably lower than under the full attention condition at each level of elaboration. That is, for the same degree of strategic semantic elaboration, fewer words were recalled after being encoded under divided attention than under full attention conditions. This implies that a different mechanism, in addition to amount of strategic elaboration, must underlie the effects of DA at encoding on later memory performance.

Likewise, recent studies in our laboratory (e.g., Naveh-Benjamin, 2002) show that the magnitude of memory performance advantage under intentional as opposed to incidental learning instructions during study is affected to the same degree in the case when attention was divided at encoding as when information was studied under full attention. If strategic/elaborative learning processes had been the only ones affected under DA, we would have expected intentional learning to be affected by DA much more than incidental learning, where no strategies were employed.

The above results by Craik and Kester (2000) and Naveh-Benjamin (2002) are not in line with the hypothesis that DA at encoding has its greatest effect on strategic-effortful processing. At the very least, these results do not support across-the-board adverse effects of DA at encoding on effortful-strategic processing; instead, it seems that DA may also affect certain types of processing other than strategic-effortful ones.

Another possibility is that the effects of DA at encoding on later memory performance are mediated by nonstrategic processes located further upstream in the continuum of processing. More specifically, it may be the case that DA interrupts the initial registration of the information. Intuitively, it seems reasonable that DA conditions may impede the participant's ability to either perceive the item to begin with or to fully perceptually register the presented event when the participant's attention is focused on the secondary task.

A study reported by Naveh-Benjamin (2002) examined this possibility. Participants were presented with lists of words, each of which was presented in one of two computer fonts, and encoded the information under conditions of either full attention or DA. Participants were forewarned about an upcoming memory test on the words. The results of a recognition test given after some interpolated activity, showed clear effects of both DA and similarity of font: Words presented at test in the same font as the one used during encoding were remembered better than those words presented in a different font from the one used at encoding. There was, however, no interaction between the two variables, implying that DA at encoding does not disrupt the initial processing of perceptual aspects, in this case font information. Such an interaction, showing a smaller advantage of same over different font recognition in the DA condition than in the full attention condition, would be expected if DA interrupted the initial perceptual registration of the information. These results are also in line with dual-process theories that distinguish between recollection-based and familiarity-based memory. Whereas font reinstatement likely affected familiarity, DA seems to have affected recollection, and the lack of interaction of the two manipulations is in line with claims regarding the independence of these processes. Furthermore, using perceptual priming procedures, Mulligan (1998) reached similar conclusions about the lack of DA effects on perceptual analysis. It should be noted, however, that this lack of support for an interruption of perceptual processes by DA at encoding is evident when implicit measures of memory are used, as was done in the above-mentioned studies. Such processes may still be interrupted by DA to result in a later decline in explicit memory performance.

To summarize, it seems that despite the strong detrimental effects that DA at encoding has on later memory performance, the results obtained in the literature do not provide clear indication regarding the identity of the processes that are interrupted during encoding by participants' occupation with the secondary task. The current research attempted to locate and identify the interfered processes more precisely using a new differential temporal interference paradigm where the study phase was interrupted at different temporal segments.

One reasonable assumption is that the encoding process is not a monolithic one, but a multiphase mental activity (e.g., Craik & Lockhart, 1972; Naveh-Benjamin & Jonides, 1984; Tulving, 2002; see also Naveh-Benjamin, Craik, Guez, & Dori, 1998, and Naveh-Benjamin & Guez, 2000 for a similar analysis for retrieval processes). Such an activity possibly includes an initial encoding phase that involves a perceptual registration mechanism, followed by lexical/semantic processes, and then a more strategic phase that includes elaboration on the information initially encoded (see Mangels, Picton, & Craik, 2001 for a similar analysis). These processes may be supplemented by an intentional encoding mode phase, which involves a mental set established to deliberately encode the information, and which is active throughout the study period. Using such a processing analysis, we wanted to evaluate whether interruption during one of these phases/processes will result in especially poor later memory performance. To do so, we conducted two experiments using a somewhat different DA paradigm from the standard one, in which words to be remembered were presented along with concurrent task interference at different temporal segments of the study phase of each word. The interference was presented during the beginning, the middle, or the end of the study phase. We assessed whether such differential interference would produce different degrees of reduction in participants' later memory performance. We compared these interference conditions to two additional ones, one where no interference was present (a full attention condition), and the other, the standard interference condition, where interference was presented throughout the study period.

In addition to looking at memory performance, we also used an online measure that provided information about the attentional resources required to execute each of the encoding phases. We did so by evaluating performance on the secondary choice reaction time (CRT) task in each of the three encoding phases relative to baseline performance when the task was performed alone.

Manipulating the interference of the secondary task at different phases of the study period, and measuring later memory performance, the attentional resources required in each of these phases, as well as the degree to which these two measures converged, could provide useful information about the encoding phases that are especially prone to interference by a concurrent task. For example, if the initial encoding phase is the one most affected by concurrent activity, we would expect to have the largest decrease in later memory performance when the secondary task is presented at an earlier phase during study. However, a more probable outcome is one where the initial encoding phase, which is at least partially mediated by data-driven/automatic encoding processes, is neither affected much by DA nor requires substantial attentional resources to be completed.

However, if the later phases of encoding, after the initial registration of information, were those most affected by concurrent activity, we would expect later memory performance to be substantially affected when concurrent activity was presented towards the middle and the later phases during study, an effect that may be accompanied by larger attentional costs during these phases. Such a pattern of results might point to more strategic encoding processes as those which are especially vulnerable to concurrent task processing.

To answer the above questions, we ran two experiments in which the temporal locus of the concurrent activity during encoding was manipulated. We measured later free recall performance as well as the attentional resources required at encoding.

Experiment 1

Method

Participants

The participants were 40 Ben-Gurion University undergraduates who participated in the experiment in exchange for course credit.

Design

One independent variable, the locus of interference of the secondary task during study, was manipulated within subjects. The concurrent task was presented on different trials during either the first third of the study period (the first 2 seconds), the second third of the study period (during the middle 2 seconds), the last third of the study period (the last 2 seconds), or not at all (the full attention condition). In addition, on other trials the secondary task was also performed alone. The dependent variables were the proportion of correctly recalled targets in the free recall task, and performance on the concurrent choice RT task.

Materials

The words used, presented auditorily, were high-frequency two- and three-syllabic words. Twelve lists were created, with 12 words in each. The length of the utterance of the recorded words via the computer was exactly 1 second, and each word was used in only one of the lists. The words in each list were not related to each other semantically, or in any other obvious way. Nine of the lists were studied under DA (three in each locus of interference condition) and three appeared under full attention. The order of the conditions and the assignment of lists to the different locus of interference conditions were counterbalanced.

The concurrent task was a visual continuous CRT task. It involved a visual display on a computer screen and a manual response on a computer keyboard (see Craik et al., 1996; Naveh-Benjamin et al., 1998). The display consisted of four boxes arranged horizontally. An asterisk appeared at random in one of the boxes and the participants' task was to press the corresponding key on the keyboard. A correct response caused the asterisk to move immediately to one of the other boxes at random.

Procedure

Participants were run individually. During the practice phase, participants received practice with the memory task, the concurrent task, and both together (DA). After the end of the practice phase, participants went through 15 trials, 12 of which were memory trials that included study and test phases, and 3 trials that involved performance on the concurrent task alone. Three replication blocks were run with five trials in each: Four memory trials, one under each of the four attention conditions, and one secondary task alone. For the memory trials, participants were told to try to learn for each list as many of the 12 words as possible and to recall as many of them as they could during the retrieval phase. For the concurrent task alone trials, they were told to respond as fast and as accurately as they could to each of the presented asterisks. In the DA trials, they were told to pay equal attention to both tasks and to try to perform on each to the best of their ability. The order of the memory trials was counterbalanced across participants. The concurrent baseline trials were inserted in, between the memory trials.

In the three full attention trials, participants studied the 12 words for 6 seconds each (for a total of 72 seconds), and after an interpolated activity of 30 seconds (counting backwards three-digit numbers starting with a given number), they attempted to orally recall as many of the words as they could for 60 seconds. Their responses were recorded by the experimenter and also tape-recorded. The same procedure was used in the DA conditions, except that participants encoded the words while performing the concurrent task. On three of the memory trials, the secondary task appeared on the screen during the first 2 seconds of the study period of a given word (during the rest of the encoding period the screen went blank). On three other memory trials, the secondary task appeared during the middle 2 seconds of the study period, and on the three remaining trials the task appeared during the last 2 seconds of the study period. A similar arrangement was used for the three levels of the secondary task baseline condition, with the asterisks appearing during the beginning, middle, or end of a given 6-second period for a total of 72 seconds for each trial.

Results and Discussion

Recall task

The mean percentage of words recalled correctly (out of 12) in the different secondary task conditions appears in Figure 1. The figure indicates that performance under full attention was superior to that under all three DA conditions, and that performance seems to show a gradient of decreasing interference as a function of the study phase temporal locus of the interference. A one-way ANOVA with the locus of interference as the independent variable showed a significant effect, F (3, 117) = 6.45, MSE = 81.1, p < .01. Specific post-hoc comparisons using the Duncan test indicated that performance under each DA condition ( M = 48.9%, 49.8%, and 52.2%, for the three encoding phases, respectively) was poorer than under the full attention condition ( M = 56.8), p < .05 in all cases. The comparisons also showed that performance was worse when the interference occurred during the first phase of study than during the third phase, and this effect approached significance, p =.10.

Concurrent CRT task

Average response times on the continuous visual CRT task were calculated for the performance in the baseline (single task) condition and in each of the different DA conditions, and can be seen in Figure 2. Since in the single task condition, there was no marker indicating when any given 6-s segment had begun, we could not assess the CRT baseline performance for each phase, but had to average them out. We were, however, able to assess separate baselines in an additional study mentioned in Footnote 1, and the results of this additional study strengthen the following reported results. Figure 2 indicates that performance in the baseline CRT task was faster than in all three DA conditions, and that performance seems to slow down the most during the initial encoding phase. A one-way ANOVA with the locus of interference as the independent variable showed a significant effect, F (3, 117) = 10.68, MSE = 1,100, p < .01. Specific post-hoc comparisons using the Duncan test indicated that performance under each DA condition ( M = 505 ms, 482 ms, and 480 ms, for the three encoding phases, respectively) was slower than under the baseline condition ( M = 463 ms), p < .01 for Phase 1, and p < .05 for Phases 2 and 3. The comparisons also showed that performance was significantly slower when the interference occurred during the first phase of encoding than during the second or the third phase, p < .05 in both cases (see also Table 1). The latter two conditions were not significantly different from each other, p > .10, ns.

Both the memory and the CRT task results replicate those reported in the literature (e.g., Craik et al., 1996; Naveh-Benjamin et al., 1998; Naveh-Benjamin et al., 1998; Naveh-Benjamin, Craik, Gavrilescu, et al., 2000; Naveh-Benjamin, Craik, Perretta, et al., 2000) in showing that DA at encoding results in a significant decrease in later memory performance (15% drop relative to the full attention condition), and that encoding processes require substantial attentional resources as reflected by the CRT task performance under DA conditions being significantly slower than in the baseline condition. Furthermore, the current results seem to indicate that all phases of encoding are sensitive to interruption by a concurrent activity. However, unexpectedly, it appears that the first phase of encoding, which is most likely associated with the initial registration of the information, is particularly vulnerable to interference, given that memory performance was poorest when interference happened during this phase. In the current experiment, the four additional seconds of processing that occurred after the interruption of the first phase were not sufficient to recover the encoding of information to the level obtained under full attention. In addition, the encoding activities during this phase seem to require more attentional resources than during the other phases.

To strengthen the obtained results we conducted a second experiment. The dual purpose of this experiment was to replicate Experiment 1 results under somewhat different conditions and to address several issues related to that experiment. One question regarding results obtained in Experiment 1 concerns whether or not the unexpected large interference effects on memory and greater secondary task costs during the first study phase reflect the interruption of early encoding/registration processes, which were presumably less mediated by strategic processes than were subsequent phases. Since the first phase in Experiment 1 encompassed two full seconds, it could have involved other processes, including more strategic ones. Similarly, the smaller amount of interference shown in the third phase of encoding in Experiment 1 might have been due to the length of the encoding phase; by the time of the third phase after 4 seconds, encoding may have been already completed. To rule out these possibilities, in Experiment 2, we cut by half the time allotted for each study phase from 2 seconds to 1 second only. This resulted in each word being studied for 3 seconds instead of the 6-second procedure implemented in Experiment 1.

Another feature of Experiment 2 was the employment of an additional DA condition where interference happened throughout the study period for a given word. Having such a condition could allow us to assess the degree to which interference during each of the phases, and especially during the first phase, was as disruptive as that throughout the whole encoding period.

Experiment 2

Method

Participants. The participants were 40 Ben-Gurion University undergraduates who participated in the experiment in exchange for course credit. None of these subjects had participated in Experiment 1.

Design

The independent variable was the temporal locus of interference of the secondary task during encoding. This variable had five levels and was manipulated within subjects, as in Experiment 1. The secondary task was presented either during the first third (the first second), the second third (the second second), the last third of the study period (the third and last second), throughout the study period (for 3 seconds), or not at all (the full attention condition). In addition, the secondary task was also performed alone either for one second at a time (as a baseline for the 1-second interfering conditions) or for 3 seconds at a time, in a continuous fashion (as a baseline for the 3-second interference condition).

Materials

Ten lists of 12 words each were created in a similar manner to that used in Experiment 1, with two replications of each of the five attention conditions. The test phase was identical to the one used in Experiment 1, and involved a free recall task. In addition, the same continuous visual CRT task was used. The order of the attention conditions was counterbalanced and the assignment of lists to the different locus of interference conditions was done as in Experiment 1. In addition to having the concurrent tasks performed in the DA conditions, participants also performed the concurrent tasks alone twice in each of the three 1-second attention conditions, as well as in the 3-second attention condition.

Procedure

The procedure used was similar to that used in Experiment 1 with the changes noted above, including a rate of presentation of 3 seconds per word. Two trial types were used for the baseline secondary task condition. The first trial type was introduced for 1 second during each 3-second period (as was done in Experiment 1), and the other was introduced throughout the 3-second interval.

Results and Discussion

Recall task

The mean percentage of words recalled correctly (out of 12) in the different secondary task conditions appears in Figure 1. Note that, as expected, the decrease in the study duration for each word from 6 seconds in Experiment 1 to 3 seconds in Experiment 2 resulted in a decrease of about 15% in the respective full attention conditions. More importantly, the figure indicates that, as in Experiment 1, performance under full attention was superior to performance under all three DA conditions, and that performance showed a gradient of decreasing interference as a function of the study phase temporal locus of the interference. A one-way ANOVA with the locus of interference as the independent variable (including the conditions of full attention, locus of interference at each of the three phases, and the interference throughout the encoding phases condition) showed a significant effect, F (4, 156) = 12.42, MSE = 96.7, p < .01. Specific post-hoc comparisons using the Duncan test indicated, as in Experiment 1, that performance under each of the DA conditions ( M = 34.8%, 37.0%, and 41.1% for the three encoding phases, and M = 30.7% for the interference throughout condition, respectively) was poorer than under the full attention condition ( M = 44.8%), p < .05 for the first and the second phase, p < .10 for the third phase, and p < .05 for the DA throughout condition. In addition, the comparisons showed that performance was significantly worse when the interference occurred during the first phase of encoding than during the third phase, p < .05, and marginally significantly worse in the second than in the third phase, p < .10. Finally, the condition in which the interference occurred throughout the encoding interval ( M = 30.5%) resulted in a significantly poorer performance than that in each of the conditions where interference occurred during one of the encoding phases, p < .05, for interference during the second, and third phases, respectively, and p < .10 for interference during the first phase.

Concurrent CRT task

Average response times on the continuous visual CRT task were calculated for performance in the 1-second baseline (single task) condition and in each of the different DA conditions, and are shown in Figure 2. The figure indicates that performance during the first study phase seems to slow down relative to the baseline condition. This result replicates the outcome of Experiment 1. A one-way ANOVA with the locus of interference as the independent variable (using the specific three interference conditions and the 1-second baseline condition) showed a significant effect, F (3, 117) = 3.39, MSE = 1,194, p < .05. Specific post-hoc comparisons using the Duncan test indicated that performance during the first phase ( M = 491 ms) but not during the second and third phases (469 ms, and 486 ms, for Phases 2 and 3, respectively) slowed down relative to the baseline condition ( M = 474 ms), p < .05 for the first comparison, and p > .10 for the second and third comparisons. The comparisons also showed that performance was significantly slower when the interference occurred during the first phase of encoding than during the second phase, p < .05.

Another analysis indicated that there was a significant effect of interference throughout the encoding period as indicated by the higher CRT performance in the condition where interference occurred during the entire 3-second encoding period ( M = 550 ms) relative to the appropriate 3-second baseline condition ( M = 513 ms), p < .01. This result is in line with those reported in the literature, showing that encoding processes require attentional resources. Interestingly, the CRT performance in the condition where it was used throughout the study period was slower than that observed for each of the specific interference conditions (after the appropriate baselines were subtracted, providing a measure of attentional costs), p < .05, for Phase 1, and p < .01 for Phases 2 and 3, respectively (see Table 1).

The fact that the CRT task in this experiment still interfered with recall performance in Phases 2 and 3, but was not interfered with by the processes involved in the encoding of the words during these phases, appears to suggest that these effects reflect different aspects of processing, one pertaining to interference effects on memory, and the other to the attentional costs required to carry out the memory processes themselves. Although in many cases these effects show up simultaneously, there are several cases in which they do not. For example, a process may be interrupted without necessarily requiring greater attentional resources for its execution, such as in cases of structural interference. Likewise, a process may not be interrupted at all, yet still require substantial resources for its execution (See Craik et al., 1996; Naveh-Benjamin & Guez, 2000, in the context of retrieval processes.)

General Discussion

The results of both experiments converge on similar patterns. First, both the memory and the CRT task results replicate those reported in the literature (e.g., Craik et al., 1996; Naveh-Benjamin et al., 1998; Naveh-Benjamin, Craik, Gavrilescu, et al., 2000; Naveh-Benjamin, Craik, Perretta, et al., 2000; Naveh-Benjamin & Guez, 2000) in showing that DA at encoding results in a significant decrease in later memory performance (an average drop of 20% relative to the full attention condition), and that encoding processes require substantial attentional resources, as reflected by the significant increase in the concurrent CRT task during encoding relative to a baseline condition. Second, the results of both experiments, which show that concurrent task interference at any phase of encoding results in a significant decline in memory performance, seem to indicate that all phases of encoding are affected by the concurrent task, from processes that happen rather early in the study phase and processing stream, possibly associated with an initial perceptual registration of information, to those that happen later in the study phase and that possibly involve more strategic processes.

The fact that memory performance declines more when attention is divided throughout the encoding process than when it is divided under each of the study phases alone (Experiment 2) may indicate that performance in the former case is a result of the accumulation of interruptions of encoding processes at different phases. In addition, the concomitant larger attentional costs associated with encoding when attention is divided throughout the encoding period than those associated with each phase alone (Experiment 2) may indicate that in addition to the attentional costs associated with each encoding phase, there are also costs associated with processes that are operating throughout the encoding period. The maintenance of such processes may require extra mental effort. This interpretation is consistent with results reported by Naveh-Benjamin (2002), which demonstrated that the attentional resources associated with intentional encoding are larger than those required during incidental learning. This pattern can be interpreted to indicate that entering an encoding mode, the mental state associated with preparing and maintaining intentional encoding processes throughout the study phase, also requires sustained attentional resources, possibly due to the need to download and maintain encoding procedures in working memory.

The results reported here are in line with previous attempts to assess the effects of DA on various encoding processes. As discussed in the Introduction, the attempts to pinpoint specific types of processes as the locus of the interference effects resulted in equivocal support (e.g., Craik & Kester, 2000; Naveh-Benjamin, Guez, & Marom, 2003). The current results seem compatible with the complex picture indicated by previous results, with no one specific mechanism functioning as the sole mediator of these DA effects. In this sense, the current studies point to a less parsimonious and more multifaceted picture according to which DA during encoding does not affect only one specific type of process, but instead, seems to interfere with a multitude of encoding processes. This conclusion is in line with results reported by Mangels, Picton, and Craik (2001), who used an analysis of the ERPs associated with attention during encoding and showed that the use of a secondary task during encoding affected mostly the N340 wave, which they suggested to represent initial lexical/semantic processes, as well as later sustained potentials from the interaction of frontal and posterior regions, which they suggested to be related to additional elaborative processes that happen later in the processing sequence.

Nevertheless, somewhat surprisingly and unexpectedly, the current studies also demonstrate that the initial encoding phase, tentatively associated with the initial registration of information, seems especially vulnerable to interference: Memory performance in both experiments was poorest when interference occurred during this phase. In addition, the encoding activities during this phase required more attentional resources than during other encoding phases. These results are in line with those reported by Naveh-Benjamin, et al. (1998), which showed that time periods around the encoding of a given word during study are associated with an increase in attentional costs compared to periods between the words. However, the strongest interference pattern during the initial registration phase is surprising, since the more obvious candidates for the locus of the DA effect are those processes that follow the initial registration and are related to strategic elaboration (Moscovitch, 1992). Interestingly, this initial encoding phase seems to be defined more in terms of relative than absolute duration, since the patterns in both experiments were fairly similar even though the initial phase duration in Experiment 2 (1 second) was only half of that in Experiment 1 (2 seconds).

Theoretically, there are several candidates for the special sensitivity of the initial stage to the interruption by concurrent activity, including novelty detection processes (Kinsbourne & George, 1974; Tulving, 2002; Tulving, Markowitsch, Kapur, Habib, & Houle, 1994), critical initial consolidation processes (Tulving, 2002), and perceptual registration. However, in principle, these processes are thought to be data-driven and relatively automatic in nature (e.g., Moscovitch, 1992). As such, we might have expected them to be somewhat resilient to interference and to be completed with relatively little effort. It is therefore not immediately apparent how the current results fit this characterization of initial encoding processes. One possibility may be related to the attention model applied here. We have used a resource sharing notion within a limited capacity model. Although this model has been used successfully in the past, especially for characterizing overall encoding activity (e.g., Craik et al., 1996), it may be better suited for the analysis of effortful processes such as elaboration, which are involved in the later stages of encoding processes. This model may have to be supplemented here by another model that explains what happens during the initial encoding phase. At this phase, the appearance of the stimuli in the secondary task (the asterisks) during the presentation of the item may engage selection processes that divert attention away from the primary task (the auditorily presented word) to the concurrent one, much like what happens with involuntary attention shifts in perceptual processing (e.g., Yantis, 1993). This may account for the poorer subsequent memory performance, as some of the words may have not been encoded well in the first place. If, according to this explanation, the execution of the secondary task becomes the first priority at this point of processing, how may we then explain the poorer performance on the concurrent task during the initial encoding phase? Speculatively, assuming that on some occasions a failure on one task (the initial encoding of the word) directs one's attention back to this task (as participants realize that they have missed hearing the word), this may result in poorer performance on the concurrent task, as well. This catastrophic failure model, wherein performance failure on the word encoding task triggers a re-allocation of resources to this task and thereby results in poorer performance on the concurrent task, may explain the pattern observed.

A somewhat different but related explanation addresses the type of attention required by both tasks. It is only during the first phase that both the memory and the secondary CRT tasks require the same type of exogenous attention. This is because both the auditory presentation of a word and the sudden onset of the secondary task visual target (an asterisk) might capture the attention of the participants, leading to poorer performance on both (see Pessoa & Ungerleider, 2004, for a review).

Alternatively, the pattern of mutual interference during the initial phase of encoding can be explained by an interruption of initial rehearsal (Naveh-Benjamin & Jonides, 1984). Such a sensitivity of the initial study phase in the current results is in line with the interruption of initial rehearsal hypothesis. Naveh-Benjamin and Jonides (1984) showed that the initial rehearsals of an encoded target (in contrast to further rehearsals) are especially important for establishing long-term memory traces. At the same time, these rehearsals require an especially large amount of attentional resources for their execution.

Finally, yet another interpretation of the larger interference and attentional cost during the first phase of encoding views these processes as outcomes of interruption of controlled attentional processes involved in initial word registration and acquisition. There is some evidence that controlled attentional processes influence the speed and accuracy of low-level processes such as stimulus detection (e.g., Schell, Wynn, Dawdson, Sinaii, & Niebala, 2000), and the operation of these processes might compete with those involved in the secondary task. Further studies are needed to assess this less obvious possibility.

Overall, the current work provides more specific information on the underlying causes of the effects of DA at encoding on later memory performance. As indicated by the measures of memory and concurrent task performance in the two experiments, the results of which converge on similar patterns, it seems that all phases of encoding, reflecting a multitude of processes, are affected by the concurrent task. Although it was expected that some of these processes, such as effortful elaboration, would be interrupted by the concurrent activity, other processes that are associated with the initial registration of information seem, unexpectedly, to be just as vulnerable to interference. An important task of future research will be to assess the viability of each of the aforementioned candidates as the potential source of the initial processing interruption by a concurrent activity, as well as elucidating the manner in which this simultaneous activity affects the array of processes associated with encoding.

Footnotes

1 One potential limitation of the secondary task costs measure in both experiments is the way in which RTs in the baseline conditions were assessed. Since the secondary task was run in a continuous manner for the same duration as the encoding phase but without a clear marking of the start of each new segment (comparable to the appearance of a word in the DA conditions), it was difficult to estimate separate baseline performance for each of the three phases. As a result, the attentional costs measure for each phase was assessed relative to the aggregate baseline measure. It would have been desirable to assess the cost for each phase by subtracting the RT for the specific baseline for a given phase from the RT in this phase in the DA condition. To do so, we designed an additional small-scale study, similar to Experiment 2, where we measured separate baseline performance at each of the three phases (each 1-s long). We did so by presenting a beep every 3 seconds that signified the beginning of a segment, and then introducing the secondary task during either the first, the second, or the third second after this beep. The data for this additional experiment show clearly that, if at all, the secondary task RT baseline for the first phase was faster than for the other two phases, which were about equal (baseline CRTs were 344 ms, 418 ms, and 419 ms, for Phases 1, 2, and 3, respectively). This strengthens the reported results, indicating that the attentional costs associated with the first phase are even larger than was found in the two reported experiments.

2 It is interesting to note that study time and attention seem to have independent effects at encoding. A reasonable assumption is that the decrease in memory performance under DA at encoding is due to the time that subjects devote to performing the secondary task, leaving less time for the processing of relevant information. However, an ANOVA combining the results of the two current experiments with the four attention conditions (the three DA ones, each for the different temporal phases, in addition to the full attention one) as one factor, and experiment as the other factor (reflecting the different times per phase in each experiment (2 sec in Experiment 1 and 1 sec in Experiment 2), showed no interaction effect on memory performance, F (3, 234) = 0.35, ns (see Figure 1). This result seems to suggest that reduction in memory performance under DA at encoding does not solely stem from reduced processing time. Similar conclusions were drawn from a study using a shared time model analysis, where Craik et al. (1996) showed that the decrease in processing time could explain only part of the deficit shown under DA at encoding. Likewise, Naveh-Benjamin, Craik, Gavrilescu, and Anderson (2000), using a similar shared time model approach, showed that reduced memory performance as a result of DA at encoding was only partially related to reduced functional time available for encoding.

3 A related possible reason for the somewhat larger interference effects during the first phase of encoding might be the fact that this is the only phase that coincided with external stimulus presentation. For example, in Experiment 2, Phase 1 completely coincided with the 1-s long word presentation. Any significant interference effects during this phase might be related to this aspect of the procedure rather than to the disruption in the first phase of initial processing. One argument against this suggestion is that such a confound may actually reflect the commonplace integration of stimulus presentation and initial processing in everyday life situations. In spite of this possibility, we decided to pursue this suggestion empirically by conducting an additional small-scale study using methods similar to those employed in Experiment 2, but with an additional condition where the word appeared for half a second before the beginning of the secondary task, allowing for a partial nonoverlap between the appearance of the secondary task and that of the word. Results showed that interference in Phase 1 was reduced in this condition relative to the one shown in Phase 1 in Experiment 2. However, interference still emerged in this phase, relative to the full attention condition. These results seem to suggest that interference in Phase 1 reflects simultaneous external stimulus presentation in both tasks, as well as further interruption of the initial processing of the word.
