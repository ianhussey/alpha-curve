More Reasons to Adopt the Five-Factor Model

By: Robert R. McCrae
National Institute on Aging, NIH
Paul T. Costa Jr.
National Institute on Aging, NIH

The reactions of Dachowski (September 1987) and Waller and Ben-Porath (September 1987) to our earlier comment (McCrae & Costa, 1986) seem to indicate growing interest in the five-factor model of personality and its applications. The five-factor model is. a descriptive classification of traits into broad domains we call Neuroticism, Extraversion, Openness, Agreeableness, and Conscientiousness. Dachowski extended the range of application of the model by pointing out conceptual similarities with the indexes of the widely-used Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI7; Myers & McCaulley, 1985). Waller and BenâPorath challenged our view of an emerging consensus on the comprehensiveness of the model by noting that several important personality systems were âyet to be incorporated into the five-factor modelâ (p. 887).

We fully agree with Waller and BenâPorath that additional research is needed to relate the five-factor model to alternative personality systems and are pleased to report that a good deal has been done since our first comment appeared. We have used the NEO Personality Inventory (NEO-PI; Costa & McCrae, 1985) as our primary measure of the model and have examined it in relation to the Eysenckâs scales (McCrae & Costa, 1985a), Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (MMPI) factors (Costa, Busch, Zonderman, & McCrae, 1986), and the State Trait Personality Inventory of Spielberger and his colleagues (Costa & McCrae, 1987). In a recent study (Costa & McCrae, 1988), we provided evidence that the five-factor model can be used to organize the Murray needs measured by Jacksonâs (1974) Personality Research Form.

Perhaps most interesting are results comparing the NEO-PI with the MBTI (McCrae & Costa, in press), which strikingly confirm the interpretations offered by Dachowski (1987). Dachowski predicted that there should be strong associations between MBTI ExtroversionâIntroversion and the Extraversion factor, and between MBTI Sensingâintuition and the Openness factor; she expected moderate correlations of MBTI ThinkingâFeeling and JudgingâPerception with the Agreeableness and Conscientiousness factors, respectively. Using self-report data in samples of 267 men and 201 women aged 19 to 93, we confirmed all these hypotheses with correlations ranging in absolute magnitude from .44 to .74 (all p < .001); these associations were replicated when mean peer ratings on the NEO-PI were correlated with MBTI scores (|r|s = .25 to .61, all p < .05). The more modest correlations with the last two MBTI indexes are understandable in terms of item content: The MBTI ThinkingâFeeling index seems to assess the attitudinal aspect of Agreeableness but not the interpersonal aspect; the MBTI JudgingâPerception index is not a pure measure of Conscientiousness because the spontaneous and flexible elements of the Perceiving types represent high Openness as well as low Conscientiousness.

We are not the only researchers who continue to be impressed by the five-factor model. Funder and Dobroth (1987) and Noller, Law, and Comrey (1987) have adopted it as a basic framework for classifying traits. Digman and Inouye (1986) have provided further specification of the factors, and Hogan (1986) has made them one of the conceptual foundations of his new personality inventory. Wiggins (1987) recently reported analyses of the MMPI personality disorder scales and concluded that âa full understanding of their personological implications can only be achieved within the context of the Big Five dimensions of personalityâ (p. 10).

We are presently collecting data that will allow us to relate the NEO-PI to the Millon Clinical Multiaxial Inventory (Millon, 1983) and to Goughâs (1987) revised California Psychological Inventory. We hope other investigators will join us in systematically examining the major personality instruments in relation to the five-factor model. Such research not only tests the comprehensiveness of the model, but it also allows interpretation of scales from a variety of instruments in terms of a common framework. The five factors can be seen as a kind of lingua franca that can allow users of one test to communicate with users of another. For example, our research (Costa & McCrae, 1988; McCrae, 1987; McCrae & Costa, 1985b, in press) suggests that Murrayâs needs for Sentience, Understanding, and Change are akin to Tellegenâs (1982) Absorption and to Zuckermanâs (1979) Sensation Seeking, and all are likely to be high in individuals classified as Intuitive types by the MBTI. All reflect the common dimension of one of the five factors. Openness to Experience.

We should make it clear that we do not think of the five-factor model as a replacement for other personality systems, but as a framework for interpreting them. Similarly, measuring the five global factors should be considered the first step, not the last, in any complete personality assessment. But because they provide at least some indication of the individualâs standing on each important aspect of personality, global measures of the five factors can be useful in many situations.

A great deal of research has gone into the formulation and evaluation of the five-factor model: this research will and should continue. But equally important questions concern the practical application of the model in organizational, educational, and clinical settings. We have instruments, such as the NEO-PI, that yield reliable and valid assessments of each of the five factors, but what is the practitioner to make of these assessments? Studies of the associations of one scale with another or with an outcome variable cannot automatically be translated into specific prescriptions for particular individuals. Here we must call on the judgment and experience of practitioners.

One approach is illustrated by the MBTI, which is widely used in applied and counseling settings to explain personality to individuals and to their families, friends, and co-workers, with the expectation that individuals will be better able to work with others and develop their own strengths when they appreciate individual differences. Although we have reservations about the theoretical basis of the MBTI (McCrae & Costa, in press), we think it provides reasonably good descriptions of four factors of personality and would be even more valuable if reinterpreted in terms of the five-factor model. Other measures of the five factors might also be used to help laypersons understand themselves and those around them.

In our first comment, we suggested that the five-factor model could be used to provide a general description of the individualâs emotional, interpersonal, experiential, and motivational styles that should be of value to clinical psychologists. In the past year, we have been gratified to hear from several clinicians that such descriptions are indeed useful. For example, T. Miller (personal communications, July 2, 1987; July 31, 1987) found that the model provided âa kind of map of the pitfalls and opportunities for psychotherapy presented by each new clientâ and that it allowed him to âget a jump on accurate empathy.â

Miller also offered a number of insights into the ways that this information could be used. Wigginsâs (1987) research suggests that the five factors may be useful in the diagnosis of personality disorders, and Miller pointed out that they can also form a context for the interpretation of specific problems. Anxiety and distress are a routine part of life for the client high in Neuroticism, but signs of some acute difficulty for one low in Neuroticism.

Miller noted that personality descriptions can be used to anticipate the likelihood of success in therapy and thus help set up realistic expectations. He speculated that persons low in Agreeableness are often not willing to cooperate in is therapy because âthey are just not very interested in how they affect the people around them, even the ones they love.â People low in Conscientiousness may be well-intentioned but lack the energy and self-discipline to work effectively on their problems.

Finally, Miller argued that knowledge of the clientâs standing on the five factors could be useful to the clinician in selecting the approach to therapy. He suggested that individuals high in Openness might profit more from techniques like dream work and gestalt, whereas those low in Openness might prefer more traditional talk therapy; cognitive therapy might appeal more to introverts, clientâcentered therapy more to extraverts. He concluded that âtherapy will finally become both a true art and a true science when therapists become able to predict who will respond to what treatment approaches, and to know the reasons why.â

The importance of client variablesâespecially personality variablesâfor the choice of therapy has long been recognized, but systematic research has lagged behind. The recent reinvigoration of research on psychotherapy (e.g., VandenBos, 1986) and the recent rediscovery of the five-factor model of personality combine to make this an ideal time to pursue the topic anew. We can now systematically explore the interactions of any approach to therapy with each of the major dimensions of personality and formulate better generalizations about the best treatment for individuals who vary in levels of Neuroticism, Extraversion, Openness, Agreeableness, and Conscientiousness. This promises to be one of the most exciting and fruitful applications of the five-factor model.
