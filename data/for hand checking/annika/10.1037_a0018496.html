Acknowledgement:

Play therapy is at a point in its history where outside forces are demanding that we look at ourselves and demonstrate that what we do has empirical value. Many of these outside forces have either ignored play therapy or have questioned, even denied, the capacity of play therapy to produce positive outcomes in therapy and counseling of children (Centers for Disease Control & Prevention, 2008; Journal of Clinical Child and Adolescent Psychology, 2008; Kaslow & Thompson, 1998; Kazdin & Weisz, 2003; Lebo, 1953; Phillips, 1982, 1985; Pumfrey & Elliott, 1970).

It is from this perspective that the need for a special research theme issue was born. Detractors and supporters of play therapy need to know what the state of the art is in play therapy research, along with ways we can help move play therapy research exposure and credibility forward. Sandra Frick-Helms was invited to guest edit this first in a series of theme issues for the International Journal of Play Therapy. She in turn asked Athena Drewes to join her in coediting this issue because of her wide range of knowledge, skill, and contacts in the therapy and counseling fields. Together, they have compiled an impressive grouping of authors and researchers who offer the reader food for thought and practical tips for research application.

All of the authors in this issue stress the importance of, as Urquiza put it, “elevating credibility in play therapy through research.” Phillips asked, “How can one of the most widely used and historically illustrious treatments for children warrant only the briefest mention, if that much, within… professional publications?”

This theme issue includes two articles that provide an overview and critique of the current state of play therapy research. Urquiza acknowledges the rich history of play and its value as is a primary means through which children experience and make meaning. His article is a guideline for what researchers should do and the sequence. Phillips takes up where he left off in a 1985 review and critique of play therapy research. Together, the Urquiza and Phillips articles provide a guideline for what researchers should and should not do. They give a useful sequence of research activities that should be followed to establish the empirical base we desire for play therapy. Several of these, such as manualization of play therapy theories and establishing a base of small, well designed studies upon which to build larger studies, have already begun to be addressed. As will be seen they agree on many points. Single case design research is one endeavor which Urquiza suggests would provide a useful base for larger scale studies. This is covered in detail in the article by Ray.

Urquiza and Phillips also point out problems and pitfalls of previous play therapy research. Phillips says, “One reads much of the current play therapy research and is left wondering about implicit and explicit models or theories of change within play therapy.” Urquiza notes that “one of the difficulties of conducting play therapy research is that it has been applied to a wide range of child problems (and) it appears difficult to expect that play therapy can fix everyone with every problem. He suggests that “a treatment approach should be developed within the context of a specific theoretical foundation and explain how a specific theory-based activity (i.e., treatment) would result in client change.” Both suggest that standardized measures should be used in play therapy research. Phillips says, “Measure what matters and measure it carefully.” And both stress the need for emphasis on carefully randomized control trials in play therapy research. They give extensive guidelines regarding how this should be done and point to expert resources for accomplishing it.

In her article, Glazer notes that, “qualitative research contributes to our understanding of phenomena in different ways. Research on play is one area that Glazer addresses as suitable for qualitative research methods,” including answering questions about the types of play that occur under everyday conditions, folk theories that people hold about the development and the nature of play, and the context of play. This should be examined alongside Phillips’ question, “Where is the play in Play Therapy Research?” He notes that few researchers have measured anything about play in their investigations and continues, “the point is the same no matter the framework: if… play is critical to treatment, then one ought to measure it and link it systematically to outcomes.”

There are other suggestions and criticisms made in the special edition, some of which may be surprising. Since Phillips’ article is a reprise of his earlier critique of play therapy research, Baggerly and Bratton respond to the points made in his critique. They summarize the accomplishments that APT members and APT as an organization have made in moving play therapy research forward, especially in the last decade.

We hope readers of this research theme issue will come away from reading it having had some questions answered and having discovered new questions. It is most important to note that we hope that readers will be inspired to “do their part” in raising play therapy research to the level of esteem it deserves.
