Considerable attention has been directed to the role of language in adult therapy (e.g., Russell, 1986). In contrast, the study of language variables in child therapy has been minimal. This lack of attention is surprising. There is a striking relationship between language and childhood disorders; half of the children with language disorders have a psychiatric disorder (Baker & Cantwell, 1987), and a large percentage (i.e., 28–50%) of psychiatrically referred children also present unsuspected language disorders (Cohen, Davine, & Meloche-Kelly, 1989). Children with learning disabilities, which often involve expressive or receptive language problems, comprise a significant percentage of the child treatment population (Tuma & Pratt, 1982). Research also indicates an important role for language mediation in obtaining adaptive levels of behavioral self-regulation and control (Richman & Lindgren, 1981). Moreover, goals in child therapy may include increasing the child’s verbalization of problematic experiences, his or her current thoughts and feelings, and relationships (Freedheim & Russ, 1983). The roles of language in child pathology, self-regulation, and treatment suggest the need for a review of language processes in child treatment. In this study meta-analytic techniques were applied to a set of 26 treatment/control comparisons of child therapy, each of which included at least one language measure as an outcome measure.

Method

From Weisz, Weiss, Alicke, and Klotz’s (1987) meta-analysis of child therapy of 105 treatment studies, 18 studies, containing 26 treatment/control comparisons, included at least one language outcome measure. Studies focused on a variety of psychological problems (e.g., hyperactivity, anxiety, severe shyness, misconduct, underachievement), but were not included in the original or current sample if problems were identified as concerning only “underdeveloped reading, writing, or knowledge of specific school subjects” (Weisz et al., p. 544) or if treatments consisted of “teaching or tutoring only to increase knowledge of a specific subject” (p. 543). No children were described as suffering from psychosis, pervasive developmental disorder, or mental retardation. There was no difference in the distribution of problem types in “undercontrolled,” “overcontrolled,” and “other” categories between the current and Weisz et al.’s original sample, χ2(2, N = 186) = 1.02, p > .5. Moreover, the children’s ages (M = 9.8, SD = 3.7) did not significantly differ from those in the Weisz et al. sample (M = 10.23, SD = 4.00) ; and the number of treatment sessions (M = 18.7, SD = 10.6) , unreported in Weisz et al., was similar to that (M = 17.4, SD = 25.3) reported in Kazdin, Bass, Ayers, and Rodgers’s (1990) study of child/adolescent treatment. The percentage of boys in the current study (78.9%) did differ from that (66%) in the larger sample, χ2(1, N1 = 26, N2 = 105) = 4.17, p < .05. Weisz et al., however, reported no significant difference in average overall effect sizes between majority male versus majority female treatment groups.

Language outcome measures were divided into two categories: expressive/receptive (e.g., Auditory Reception from the Illinois Test of Psycholinguistic Abilities, Kirk & McCarthy, 1961), and reading measures (e.g., Wide Range Achievement Reading Scores, Jastak & Wilkinson, 1984; California Achievement Test Comprehension Scores, Stewart, 1978). Nonlanguage measures included such indices as Matching Familiar Figures Test (Kagan, 1964), school suspension rates, and Taylor Manifest Anxiety Scale (Taylor, 1953). Agreement between two raters for classification of outcome measures as expressive/receptive, reading, or nonlanguage were well within the acceptable range: 84% agreement, κ = 0.57, p < 0.01. Similarly, all comparisons were coded for modality of treatment (individual, group, parent or other), and type of presenting problem (overcontrol, undercontrol, other), following Weisz et al. (1987); 83% agreement, κ = .70, p < .01; 100% agreement, respectively.

Three types of verbal process were derived from characterizations of child treatment. The first type is characterized by spontaneous verbal interaction in process-oriented therapy, as emphasized in traditional therapies (e.g., insight and nondirective). The second type is characterized by verbal interaction constrained by preset tasks and problem-solving goals, as in cognitive-behavioral treatments (e.g., problem-solving and self-instructional training). The third type is characterized by behavioral exchanges in which verbal interaction plays a secondary role as in operant and counterconditioning modes of treatment (e.g., behavioral parent-training, relaxation, systematic desensitivization). Two coders classified the studies’ type of language emphasis by identifying treatment technique types (100% agreement).

Effect sizes were computed for the 26 treatment/control comparisons using Cohen’s d = (M1 − M2 )/s (Smith, Glass, & Miller, 1980). M1 and M2 are the means of the contrasting groups on an outcome measure, and s is the control group standard deviation.

Results and Discussion

Language

Does child therapy affect patient language proficiencies? Is its impact similar to that on nonlanguage proficiencies? In this sample, treatment had a positive impact on language (expressive and reading) proficiencies: The effect size, d = .32, is significantly different from 0, p < .05. Treatments also had a greater impact on language than nonlanguage variables, Z = 2.94, p < .01, as determined by the Wilcoxon 2 sample test. Further comparisons revealed that the effects of treatment on reading versus expressive/receptive language skills were not significantly different, Z = 0, p > .05, but treatment impact on both language proficiencies was greater than on nonlanguage measures, Z = 1.98, p < .05, and Z = 2.54, p < .05, respectively. These results suggest that child therapy can positively affect a vital area of functioning that often has been overlooked in treatment and research. The greater impact on language versus nonlanguage variables may suggest that language assessment can reveal more subtle and perhaps different kinds of change than nonlanguage measures. Although there was a trend for changes in language and nonlanguage variables to be positively correlated, r (23) = .39, p < .06, different change processes seem to be at work, as indicated by the significant correlation of language effect sizes with number of sessions, r (29) = .45, p < .01, but a nonsignificant correlation for nonlanguage effect sizes with number of sessions, r (22) = .15. This finding of significant positive covariation is surprising, because, across outcome measures, length of treatment had been previously reported to be significantly negatively correlated, r (59) = −.28, with treatment effect sizes (Casey & Berman, 1985). Although the current findings are open to interpretation, one possibility is that increases in language proficiencies are fostered by the child’s intensive participation in the focused communicative interaction of treatment. This interpretation and the results, however, must be viewed with caution. Although the effect sizes on the language variables are comparable to the effect sizes found by Smith et al. (1980) on academic and work achievement measures, the effect sizes for nonlanguage measures are lower than overall language plus nonlanguage effect sizes (.79) reported in previous meta-analysis of child treatment (cf. Weisz et al., 1987).

Modality

Do the effects of treatment on language proficiencies vary with modality of treatment? Individual therapy was an effective, although not often used (N = 6) , modality of treatment, d = 1.31, p < .05. It produced an effect size more than 10 times the pooled other modalities, Z = 2.9, p < .01. Perhaps individual treatment produced these larger effects on language proficiencies, because it required more concentrated language interaction between the therapist and the child. A modeling effect and a tendency to reduce anxiety around verbal performance may also be present in stronger form than in group therapy. This interpretation gains plausibility, because across the three types of emphasis on verbal interaction in therapy (i.e., traditional verbal, cognitive/problem solving, and operant/relaxation), the effect sizes for individual treatment were at least 5 times as large as the effect sizes for group treatment.

Verbal process

Does the type of emphasis on verbal process in therapy affect the magnitude of change in language proficiencies at outcome? When treatments were classified by type of verbal process, treatments that emphasized spontaneous verbal interaction yielded effect sizes that were over 10 times as large as treatments using verbal interaction constrained by goal-oriented formats and approximately 4 times as large as the effect sizes for treatments emphasizing behavioral exchanges. Although of large magnitude, these differences did not achieve statistical significance. Note, however, that the same trend in the magnitude of effect sizes emerged when comparing types of language emphasis across individual and group treatments, taken separately. Future meta-analyses with more statistical power are necessary to better assess the reliability of these large differences and the possibility of a language emphasis by presenting problem-type interaction.

Type of problem

Do the effects of treatment on language proficiencies vary with patients’ problem types? A comparison of effect sizes for the language outcome variables indicated that children presenting with peer rejection, low self-esteem, or underachievement showed four times the gains on language measures than both overcontrolled and undercontrolled children, a disparity in the opposite direction of what has been found generally (i.e., that both under- and overcontrolled children respond more favorably to treatment than these other children; see Casey & Berman, 1985; Weisz et al., 1987). This trend was not statistically significant, however, and may be the result of confounds with type of language emphasis and modality of treatment. A hypothesis for future research is that the large change on language measures by the first group results from the less pervasive nature of their problems, which may be more centrally connected to those communicative proficiencies exercised and strengthened in treatment and later tested at outcome.

Summary and Conclusion

We explored four questions on the impact of therapy on children’s language proficiencies. Results indicate that therapy produced significant improvement in children’s language proficiencies and that this improvement was significantly more positive in individual than in group treatment. Interestingly, change in language but not nonlanguage measures covaried with the number of treatment sessions, suggesting that differing processes of change may be at work. Results also suggest several trends worthy of future research, namely, (a) that therapies emphasizing spontaneous verbal interaction may produce relatively larger changes in language proficiencies at outcome than therapies that stress restricted types of language interaction or behavioral interventions and (b) that children with emotional and achievement problems may reap more benefits in the domain of language proficiencies than children with more entrenched under- or overcontrolled behavior problems. Because of the small sample of studies that included language assessment and the variable methodological quality of child therapy research (see Greenwald & Russell, in press; Kazdin et al., 1990; Weiss & Weisz, 1990), these latter trends as well as the statistically significant findings should be viewed with caution. The small sample size did not allow an examination of the interaction of modality, type of emphasis, and type of presenting problem.

What this meta-analytic review does strongly suggest, however, is (a) that a potentially rich and clinically significant area of child functioning has been only sporadically studied (i.e., only 17% of the 105 child outcome studies contained a language measure) and that ratings or categorizations of the in vivo therapist and child language interaction is uniformily missing and (b) that this domain of functioning seems sensitive to the effects of child treatment, providing an additional, nonreactive dimension for the assessment of outcome. Moreover, if the significant positive covariation of language change and number of treatment sessions can be replicated, an important area of functioning will be opened for more detailed investigation of the treatment dose/response relationship. What is needed now is a more intensive effort by child therapy researchers to assess the impact of treatment on children’s language proficiencies as well as on their clinical significance, gauged not only in terms of their improved test scores relative to normal comparisons, but in the more pervasive context of their everyday language performances (e.g., contrasting the childrens’ pre- and posttherapy ability to make appropriate linguistic requests for action or information, or to narratively encapsulate their recent experiences in an interpersonally engaging way; Russell & Hack, 1991; Russell & van den Broek, 1989). Such research might impel therapists to orient more explicitly to the linguistic/communicative bases of therapeutic change mechanisms and problems in adaptive functioning (Russell, 1989).
